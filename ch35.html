🌠 The Universe

Everything in space — the moons, asteroids, comets, stars, planets, solar systems and galaxies — makes up the universe.

The study of all the objects in our universe outside Earth's atmosphere is called astronomy. Over time, many astronomers have helped to develop our understanding of what the universe is and how it began.

🔭 Changing Models of the Universe

Our understanding of the universe has changed dramatically over centuries. Here is the key timeline:

384–322 BCE
Aristotle — Geocentric Idea
Ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle believed all celestial objects rotated around Earth.
100–170 CE
Ptolemy — Geocentric Model
Egyptian astronomer Ptolemy made a formal geocentric model of the universe, placing Earth at its centre. This model was widely accepted for over 1,000 years.
~1515
Nicolaus Copernicus — Heliocentric Model
Polish astronomer Copernicus (1473–1543) proposed that Earth and other planets in our solar system in fact rotate around the Sun — a heliocentric model.
1610
Galileo Galilei — Proof
Galileo (1564–1642) proved the heliocentric model was correct when he used a new telescope to observe the moons of Jupiter orbiting the planet — not Earth.
1927
Georges Lemaître — Big Bang Theory
Belgian astronomer Georges Lemaître (1894–1966) proposed that the universe started from a single point, which exploded and began expanding. His theory was initially rejected by famous scientists, including Einstein.
1929
Edwin Hubble — Expanding Universe Confirmed
Astronomer Edwin Hubble confirmed Lemaître's idea that the universe is expanding when he observed that galaxies are moving away from Earth. Hubble's law states that the more distant a galaxy is from us, the faster it is moving.
Geocentric Model
A model of the universe with Earth at the centre. All other celestial objects were believed to rotate around Earth.
💡 "Geo" = Earth. Used for over 1,000 years but proved wrong by Copernicus and Galileo.
Heliocentric Model
A model with the Sun at the centre of the solar system. Earth and the other planets orbit the Sun.
💡 "Helio" = Sun. Proposed by Copernicus, confirmed by Galileo in 1610.
Hubble's Law
The more distant a galaxy is from us, the faster it is moving away.
💡 This was key evidence that the universe is expanding — and still expanding today.

Eureka! – Edwin Hubble (1889–1953)
Edwin Hubble proved that there are many more galaxies beyond our Milky Way, which had previously been mistaken for gas and dust clouds. The Hubble Space Telescope was named in his honour.
💥 How the Universe Began

In 1927, Georges Lemaître proposed that the universe started from a single point which exploded and began expanding to become the universe. Edwin Hubble confirmed this in 1929 by observing galaxies moving away from Earth.

🔥 The Big Bang
The Big Bang Model
13.8 billion years ago, a very hot, dense point in space called a singularity exploded and began to expand. All the matter in the universe is believed to have come from this moment in time.
💡 The explosion only lasted a fraction of a second but reached approximately 1,000 trillion degrees Celsius. The universe has been expanding ever since.
Singularity
The name for the very hot, dense single point from which the Big Bang explosion originated.
💡 Think of it as the very first "dot" — infinitely small and infinitely hot — from which everything in the universe came.
🎯
CONCENTRATE! The Big Bang model states the universe started 13.8 billion years ago. The explosion originated from a singularity. The universe continues to expand today. These three facts come up repeatedly in exam questions.
⏱️ What Happened After the Big Bang?

After this intense expansion of matter, the following happened in sequence:

1
Hot particles expanded and cooled
Hot particles, together with light and energy, continued to expand and began to cool down. This allowed protons and neutrons to form.
2
380,000 years — atoms form
For the next 380,000 years, protons and neutrons crashed together. Once they had cooled down enough, they joined together to form atoms.
3
Gravity forms stars and galaxies
Gravity began to attract matter and these atoms eventually turned into stars and galaxies.
4
Heavier elements created
Stars created bigger and heavier elements, now found in comets, asteroids and planets. Whenever the stars exploded, these elements went on to form other stars and solar systems.
5
9 billion years — our solar system forms
Nine billion years after the Big Bang, our own solar system formed. The universe continues to expand today.

🔬 Evidence for the Big Bang Model
1. Big Bang 'Echo' — Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR)

The Big Bang model tells us that the universe is filled with waves of radiation left over from the massive heat given off during the explosion that caused it to begin expanding.

Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR)
The leftover heat radiation from the Big Bang explosion, spread uniformly throughout the universe. It is the black-and-white fuzz you see, or the hiss you hear, between analogue TV and radio stations. It is commonly known as the Big Bang 'echo'.
💡 In 1964, Robert Wilson and Arno Penzias discovered that no matter where they pointed their radio telescope, the same background noise was present — coming from the universe itself.
📡 Evidence 1 — CMBR (Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation)
In 1964, two American astronomers — Robert Wilson (1936–present) and Arno Penzias (1933–present) — found that no matter where they pointed their radio telescope, they could hear the same background noise. They concluded it was coming from the universe, left over from the Big Bang. This radiation is the "echo" of the initial explosion and supports the Big Bang model.
2. Expanding Universe — Hubble's Law
🌌 Evidence 2 — Galaxies Moving Away (Hubble's Law)
Edwin Hubble observed in 1929 that galaxies are moving away from Earth, and the more distant a galaxy is, the faster it moves. This shows the universe is still expanding — exactly as the Big Bang model predicts. If the universe is expanding now, it must have started from a single, much smaller point in the past.
🛰️ Space Telescopes

In the twentieth century, technology allowed scientists to develop powerful space telescopes. Because these operate from space rather than Earth, they are not affected by rain, clouds, light pollution or the atmosphere. Thanks to their impressive range and ability to observe what cannot be seen with the human eye, these telescopes provide evidence to support the model of an expanding universe.

Light Year
A light year is the distance light can travel in one year (over 9 trillion km).
💡 When a telescope looks at an object 1 light year away, it sees the light that left that object a year ago — so telescopes are looking back in time!
🔭
Hubble Space Telescope (HST)
Launched in 1990 aboard the space shuttle Discovery. Can view celestial objects as far as 13.4 billion light years away. Has made more than 1.5 million observations and captured images of new planetary systems.
When it looks 13.4 billion light years away, the images are from 13.4 billion years ago — looking almost back to the Big Bang!
🪐
James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)
Launched into space in 2021. Made of high-precision mirrors and a sunshield the size of a tennis court. Over 100× more powerful than Hubble and can observe up to 100 objects at the same time.
Captures highly focused infrared images of stars from the early stage of the universe, adding to our understanding of the Big Bang.
🎯
CONCENTRATE! Space telescopes operate from space so they avoid clouds, light pollution and the atmosphere. They look back in time because light from distant objects takes millions/billions of years to reach us. The Hubble Telescope looks 13.4 billion light years away = images from 13.4 billion years ago.

✏️ Summary Questions
Q1 — What is astronomy?
What is astronomy?
Astronomy is the study of all the objects in our universe outside Earth's atmosphere.
Q2 — Copernicus & Galileo
a) How did Nicolaus Copernicus change our understanding of the universe?   b) How did Galileo prove Copernicus's theory?
a) Copernicus proposed the heliocentric model — that the Earth and other planets orbit the Sun, not Earth at the centre. b) Galileo used a telescope to observe the moons of Jupiter orbiting Jupiter (not Earth), proving that not everything revolves around Earth.
💡 Key names: Copernicus = proposed heliocentric. Galileo = proved it with Jupiter's moons.
Q3 — What does the Big Bang model state?
What does the Big Bang model state?
The Big Bang model states that 13.8 billion years ago, a very hot, dense point in space called a singularity exploded and began to expand. All the matter in the universe is believed to have come from this moment.
💡 Three key facts to remember: 13.8 billion years ago / singularity exploded / universe still expanding today.
Q4 — Lemaître & Hubble contributions
a) Outline the contribution of astronomers Georges Lemaître and Edwin Hubble to our understanding of the Big Bang model.   b) What is Hubble's law?
a) Lemaître (1927): Proposed the universe started from a single point that exploded and began expanding (the Big Bang theory). Hubble (1929): Confirmed the theory by observing that galaxies are moving away from Earth, proving the universe is expanding. b) Hubble's law: The more distant a galaxy is from us, the faster it is moving away.
💡 Lemaître = proposed the theory. Hubble = confirmed/proved it with observations.
Q5 — True or False
Identify whether the following statements are true or false:
a) The universe began slowly and heated up gradually.
b) A hot, dense point called a singularity exploded 13.8 billion years ago.
c) After the initial Big Bang explosion, particles cooled and began to form atoms.
d) Our solar system formed 9 billion years after the Big Bang explosion.
e) The universe has stopped expanding.
a) FALSE — The Big Bang was an almost instantaneous explosion, not gradual. b) TRUE — The singularity exploded 13.8 billion years ago. c) TRUE — Particles cooled and protons/neutrons joined to form atoms. d) TRUE — Our solar system formed 9 billion years after the Big Bang. e) FALSE — The universe continues to expand today.
Q6 — CMBR as evidence
Explain how cosmic microwave background radiation (CMBR) is evidence of the Big Bang model.
The Big Bang model predicts that a massive explosion would have left behind heat radiation spread throughout the universe. In 1964, Wilson and Penzias discovered that no matter where they pointed their radio telescope, they detected the same background radiation/noise. This is the CMBR — leftover heat energy from the Big Bang explosion — supporting the model.
💡 Everyday example: the static/fuzz on old analogue TVs between channels is partly CMBR from the Big Bang!
Q7 — Space telescopes
a) Name one space telescope.   b) Why can space telescopes tell us more about the universe than Earth-based telescopes?
a) The Hubble Space Telescope (or James Webb Space Telescope). b) Space telescopes operate outside Earth's atmosphere, so they are not affected by rain, clouds, light pollution or atmospheric interference. This gives them a much greater range and clarity of observation.
Q8 — What is a light year?
What is a light year?
A light year is the distance light can travel in one year — over 9 trillion km (9,000,000,000,000 km).
💡 It's a unit of distance, not time! When a telescope looks at something 13.4 billion light years away, those images are from 13.4 billion years ago.
📝 Past Paper Questions
Final Examination 2022, Question 8
During your studies you learned about a scientific model that helps us understand the origin of the universe.
a) Name the model you studied.
b) Outline two pieces of evidence that support this model.
a) The Big Bang model. b) Evidence 1: Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) — A constant background radiation is detected throughout the universe. This is leftover heat energy from the Big Bang explosion, supporting the idea of a massive initial explosion. Evidence 2: Galaxies moving away (Hubble's Law) — Edwin Hubble observed that all galaxies are moving away from us, and the more distant they are, the faster they move. This shows the universe is still expanding, consistent with the Big Bang model.
💡 Always name the model first, then give two clearly explained pieces of evidence. Each piece of evidence should link the observation to the Big Bang prediction.
