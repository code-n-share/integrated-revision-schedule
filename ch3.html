🌱 Characteristics of Life
Organism
In science, a living thing is called an organism. There are seven common features that tell us if something is living. These are known as the characteristics of life.
💡 Remember them with MRSGREN: Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity (Response), Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.
Characteristic Description
Movement Changing position to find food, escape danger or absorb sunlight
Respiration Releasing energy from food in the organism's cells
Response Reacting to changes in the environment
Growth Increasing in size when new cells are made or when cells become larger
Reproduction Producing new organisms
Excretion Removing waste made in cells from the organism
Nutrition Needing and getting food
🎯
CONCENTRATE! A fire can move and grow, but it does not respire, reproduce, respond, excrete, or require nutrition in the biological sense — so it is not a living organism. All seven characteristics must apply!
🔬 Cells & Organelles
Cell
A cell is the basic building block of life and has all the characteristics of life. All cells contain structures that have special functions. These cell structures are called organelles.
💡
Eureka – Robert Hooke (1635–1703)
Robert Hooke coined the term 'cells'. When he looked at plant cells under a microscope, he noticed structures that reminded him of the small rooms monks lived in, which were known as cells.
🐾 Animal Cells

An animal cell contains three main organelles: cell membrane, cytoplasm and nucleus.

Fig. 3.2 – Animal Cell Diagram

Organelle Description
Cell membrane Surrounds the cell, controlling which materials move in and out of it.
Cytoplasm A jelly-like substance surrounded by the cell membrane. Contains the nucleus and dissolved substances, including oxygen.
Nucleus The control centre of the cell. Contains genes that control inherited characteristics, e.g. hair colour.
🌿 Plant Cells

A plant cell contains a cell membrane, cytoplasm and nucleus — plus three more organelles unique to plant cells:

  • Cell wall
  • Chloroplast
  • Vacuole

Fig. 3.4 – Plant Cell Diagram

Organelle Description
Cell wall Made of cellulose, which gives plant cells their shape.
Chloroplast Contains the green pigment chlorophyll, which allows the plant cell to absorb sunlight energy so the plant can make its own food by photosynthesis.
Vacuole Membrane-bound structures that store water, food and waste products. Vacuoles are also found in animal cells, but they are much smaller.
Comparing Animal and Plant Cells
Organelle Animal Cells Plant Cells
Cell membrane ✅ Yes ✅ Yes
Cytoplasm ✅ Yes ✅ Yes
Nucleus ✅ Yes ✅ Yes
Cell wall ❌ No ✅ Yes
Chloroplasts ❌ No ✅ Yes
Vacuoles ⚠️ Yes, but much smaller ✅ Yes
🔭 The Light Microscope
Light Microscope
A light microscope is a piece of laboratory equipment that allows biologists to study organelles by passing a beam of light through cells.
💡 The sample of cells studied under a microscope is called a specimen.
💡
Eureka – Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723)
The light microscope was invented in the seventeenth century by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek. It allowed scientists to 'see into' cells for the first time.
Parts of a Light Microscope
Base
Keeps the microscope stable on the bench.
Stage & Stage Clips
Holds the glass slide in place during viewing.
Light Source
A built-in light bulb or mirror that lights up the specimen.
Diaphragm
Controls the amount of light passing through the specimen.
Objective Lens
Magnifies the specimen by different factors (×4, ×10, ×40).
Eyepiece Lens
Magnifies the specimen, usually by a factor of 10 (×10).
Coarse Focus Wheel
Brings the image into rough focus. Always adjust this first.
Fine Focus Wheel
Brings the image into sharp, precise focus.
🎯
CONCENTRATE! Always start the objective lens on the lowest factor. Always adjust the coarse focus wheel before adjusting the fine focus wheel.
Calculating Magnification
Total Magnification
Total magnification = eyepiece lens × objective lens
🤔
Figure it Out!
Calculate the overall magnification of a cell in a specimen when the eyepiece lens is set at ×10 and the objective lens is set at ×40.

Answer: 10 × 40 = ×400
🧪 Experimental Investigation 3.1
How can we look at animal cells?
Equipment
Sterile cotton swabs / tongue depressors Cover slip Seeker or mounted needle Water Dropper Microscope Glass slide Methylene blue stain Tissue paper
⚠️
Safety
• Be careful when handling glass slides and cover slips.
• Only use sterile swabs.
• Never share swabs.
• Dispose of swabs responsibly after use.
Method – Without Stain
  1. Gently rub the inside of your cheek with a sterile swab.
  2. Place a drop of water on a glass slide and gently roll the swab over the surface to transfer the cheek cells.
  3. Using a seeker, gently lower a cover slip onto the glass slide.
  4. Ensure the stage is set to its lowest position.
  5. Place the glass slide on the stage and secure with the clips.
  6. Click the lowest power objective lens into position first.
  7. Use the coarse focus wheel to get the image in rough focus, then sharpen the image by using the fine focus wheel.
  8. Click the medium power objective lens into place and use fine focus only to sharpen the image.
  9. Repeat step 8 with the highest power objective lens.
  10. Draw labelled diagrams of what you see.
Method – With Stain (Methylene Blue)
  1. Gently rub the inside of your cheek with a fresh sterile swab.
  2. Place a drop of water on a fresh glass slide and gently roll the swab over the surface to transfer the cheek cells.
  3. Add a drop of methylene blue stain to the slide and remove any excess with tissue paper.
  4. Repeat steps 3–10 of the 'without stain' method.
🧪 Experimental Investigation 3.2
How can we look at plant cells?
Equipment
Onion Knife Water Glass slide Seeker Iodine stain Chopping board Forceps Dropper Cover slip Microscope Tissue paper
⚠️
Safety
• Be careful when handling glass slides.
• Take care when using a knife.
Method – Without Stain
  1. Cut an onion on a chopping board. Use forceps to gently peel off a thin layer of cells from the inner surface of the onion.
  2. Using a dropper, add a drop of water to the glass slide. Carefully place the thin layer of cells on the glass slide, ensuring the layer of cells doesn't fold over.
  3. Follow steps 3–10 from Experimental Investigation 3.1.
Method – With Stain (Iodine)
  1. Place a drop of water on a fresh glass slide and carefully place a new, thin layer of cells on the surface, ensuring the layer doesn't fold over.
  2. Using a dropper, add a drop of iodine stain to the slide. Remove any excess stain using tissue paper.
  3. Using a seeker, gently lower a cover slip onto the glass slide.
  4. Follow steps 4–10 from Experimental Investigation 3.1.

⚛️ The Electron Microscope
Electron Microscope
Biologists use an electron microscope to study cells in even greater detail. A beam of electrons is passed through the specimen. This forms a hugely magnified image (×1,000,000) called a micrograph.
💡 Micrographs display in black and white. Two organelles only visible with an electron microscope: mitochondria and ribosomes.
Organelle Description
Mitochondria Release energy through respiration
Ribosomes Make proteins, including enzymes
🎯
CONCENTRATE! Light microscope → can see: cell membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, chloroplasts, vacuole, cell wall.
Electron microscope → can additionally see: mitochondria and ribosomes.
🏗️ The Organisation of Life

All organisms are organised in the same way: they are made up of cells, which form tissues, organs and organ systems.

Organelle
Structure in a cell that carries out a particular role.
↓
Cell
Building block of life, made up of organelles. Different cells do different jobs, e.g. white blood cells fight infection.
↓
Tissue
Group of cells that work together, e.g. blood is a tissue made up of red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets and plasma.
↓
Organ
Group of tissues that work together, e.g. the heart is an organ made up of cardiac muscle tissue, connective tissue and fat tissue.
↓
System
Group of organs that work together, e.g. the heart is the organ at the centre of the circulatory system, which pumps blood around the body.
↓
Organism
The result of all systems working together, e.g. the circulatory system works with the breathing system to transport oxygen around the body.

Problem 1 — Characteristics of Life
Explain why a dog is a living thing.
A dog shows all seven characteristics of life: Movement – A dog can walk/run to find food or escape danger Respiration – It releases energy from food in its cells Response – It reacts to its environment (e.g. responds to its name) Growth – It increases in size from puppy to adult Reproduction – It can produce new organisms (puppies) Excretion – It removes waste products (urine, faeces) Nutrition – It needs and gets food to survive
✅ All seven MRS GREN characteristics apply → the dog is a living organism.
💡 For any exam question like this, go through each of the seven characteristics and give a specific example for that organism.
Problem 2 — Living vs Non-Living
Explain why a fire is NOT a living thing.
A fire does NOT show all seven characteristics: Movement – It can spread, but not purposefully ✗ Respiration – It oxidises fuel, but not cellular respiration ✗ Response – It does not respond to its environment ✗ Growth – It can grow bigger, but cells are not made ✗ Reproduction – It cannot produce new organisms ✗ Excretion – It does not excrete biological waste ✗ Nutrition – It burns fuel, but does not need nutrition ✗
✅ A fire fails all seven biological criteria → it is NOT living.
💡 The key difference: fire involves chemical combustion. Living things involve cellular processes — respiration, growth, reproduction etc.
Problem 3 — Microscope Magnification
Calculate the total magnification when the eyepiece lens is ×10 and the objective lens is ×40. Which objective should you begin with when setting up the microscope?
Total magnification = eyepiece × objective Total magnification = 10 × 40 = ×400 Always begin with the LOWEST objective lens (×4 or ×10) Then move to medium (×10) then high (×40)
✅ Total magnification = ×400 | Always start with the lowest objective.
💡 Always start low! If you start at ×40 you risk smashing the slide into the lens and you won't be able to find the cells.
Problem 4 — Animal vs Plant Cell Differences
List three structures found in plant cells that are NOT found in animal cells.
Plant-only structures: 1. Cell wall – Made of cellulose; gives the cell its rigid shape 2. Chloroplast – Contains chlorophyll; enables photosynthesis 3. (Large) Vacuole – Stores water, food and waste (much larger than in animal cells)
✅ Cell wall, Chloroplast, Large Vacuole
💡 Animal cells DO have vacuoles, but they are much smaller. The question asks for structures absent or significantly different.
Problem 5 — Organisation of Life
Put the levels of the organisation of life in order, starting with the simplest level:
Tissue   System   Organelle   Organ   Cell   Organism
Simplest → Most complex: 1. Organelle (e.g. nucleus, chloroplast) 2. Cell (e.g. white blood cell) 3. Tissue (e.g. blood) 4. Organ (e.g. heart) 5. System (e.g. circulatory system) 6. Organism (e.g. human)
✅ Organelle → Cell → Tissue → Organ → System → Organism
💡 Mnemonic: "Only Clever Tigers On Safari Observe" (Organelle, Cell, Tissue, Organ, System, Organism)
Problem 6 — Electron Microscope
Name two organelles that can be seen with an electron microscope but NOT with a light microscope.
1. Mitochondria – release energy through respiration 2. Ribosomes – make proteins, including enzymes
✅ Mitochondria and Ribosomes
💡 Electron microscopes can magnify up to ×1,000,000 — far beyond a light microscope. That extra power reveals these much smaller organelles.

The three main parts of an animal cell are: → Cell membrane – outer boundary controlling entry/exit of materials → Cytoplasm – jelly-like substance filling the cell → Nucleus – dark, round structure; the control centre of the cell Which part controls the activities of the cell? → The NUCLEUS controls the cell's activities.
