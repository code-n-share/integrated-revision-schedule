⚡ Energy Resources — Introduction

Energy resources are resources that the physical world provides us with that we can make energy from. These energy resources are very important for the economy. However, our use of them can have a big impact on society and the environment.

♻️
Renewable Energy
Energy resources that can be used over and over again. They will not run out.
Examples: wind, solar, water, geothermal, biomass
🛢️
Non-Renewable Energy
Energy resources that can only be used once. They will eventually run out.
Examples: oil, coal, gas, peat
🔥
Fossil Fuels
Fuels made from decomposing plants and animals. Used for electricity, transport and heating.
Around 80% of the world's energy comes from fossil fuels. In Ireland, about 86%.
💡
Ireland's Energy Imports (Fig 5.3) Ireland imports most of its energy. The largest share comes from oil (66%), followed by natural gas (24.6%), biofuels (7.1%), electricity (1.3%), and coal (1%).
🛢️ Non-Renewable Energy Resources

Non-renewable energy comes from sources that will eventually run out. Fossil fuels such as oil, coal, gas and peat are all examples. They cause great harm to the environment, and people are too dependent (rely on too much) on them.

🗺️
Identifying on OS Maps Power stations using non-renewable energy resources are identified on OS maps by the symbol: ⊙ (Stáisiún cumhachta breosla iontaiseach / Power Station Fossil).
🛢️ Oil

Oil is a very important non-renewable source of energy in the world today. It is used for transport (petrol/aviation fuel), heating homes, and car maintenance. About 66% of Ireland's energy imports are oil.

✅ Advantages of Oil
  • Gives off hardly any smoke
  • Produces great heat
  • Very efficient
❌ Disadvantages of Oil
  • It is expensive
  • In Ireland, it has to be imported
  • Transported by sea in tankers — oil leaks can cause serious pollution
📍 Location of Oil

Many countries including the USA and Norway have oil reserves. However, the vast majority of reserves are located around the Persian Gulf in the Middle East — Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Iran, Iraq, UAE.

Oil prices are set by OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries), which causes the price to fluctuate (rise and fall).

🇮🇪 Oil in Ireland

In Ireland, 46% of our energy comes from oil. Ireland has not exploited its oil reserves because:

  • The location of Irish oil reserves makes it very difficult to drill for oil.
  • The oil may not be of high quality.

In 2021, the Irish government brought in a policy that stopped any new exploration for oil, as it goes against their goal of a low-carbon future. The Barryroe oil field off the west Cork coast is still being explored for possible exploitation.

💡 The average household energy bill in 2024 was over €3,200.
🔵 Gas

In Ireland, over 30% of our energy needs comes from natural gas, which is a non-renewable energy source. Most gas is used in generating electricity. Kinsale Head gas field is located off the coast of Co. Cork. From 1978 to 2020, gas was exploited from this field. Barryroe is another key site off west Cork.

📋 Case Study
Non-Renewable Energy: Corrib Gas Field

In late 2015, the Corrib gas field came into operation. It is located off the north-west coast of Ireland, near Co. Mayo. Gas is brought ashore through a pipeline more than 90 km long, running along the seabed into a tunnel under Sruwaddacon Bay, then to the onshore gas terminal at Bellanaboy Bridge, near Belmullet.

The Corrib gas field is expected to cease operations and run out by the end of 2030. It currently provides around 50% of Ireland's electricity needs. No new licences are being given for gas exploration. However, surveys suggest the field could provide gas for a further ten years.

💰 Economic Impacts
  • Cost around €3.6 billion to develop
  • Produces 100% of Ireland's home-produced gas
  • Makes up 30% of Ireland's overall gas needs
  • Created over 1,400 jobs during construction; currently employs 150 people directly
  • Roads network upgraded costing €22 million
👥 Social Impacts
  • Local communities benefited — Belmullet GAA received €450,000 for training grounds
  • Reduced migration from the area
  • Grants to local projects annually — €130,000 in 2022 alone
  • Sponsors festivals and shows in the region
  • Two scholarships to local secondary school students annually
🌿 Environmental Impacts
  • Safety concerns about explosion along the onshore pipe route
  • Harm to Carrowmore Lake (local drinking water source) during construction
  • Waste material released into Broadhaven Bay — impacts dolphins, otters, grey seals and whales
  • Carbon, nitrogen and sulphur released into a clean rural atmosphere
  • Visual impact of the Bellanaboy Bridge gas terminal
⬛ Coal

Coal is a non-renewable solid fuel used to heat many homes. While Ireland's dependency on coal is constantly declining, around 7% of our energy still comes from coal. In 2024, the ESB (Electricity Supply Board) began converting its only coal-powered station at Moneypoint to an oil-fired station. It will then act as a back-up generator for the Irish market.

🟤 Peat

Peat is a fuel made up of decayed plants and other organic matter found in bogs. It has been an important source of heating and energy in Ireland for hundreds of years. Since 2022, commercial exploitation of peat has ceased. The selling of peat (turf) is prohibited. Exploitation is now only allowed at a local level where people can remove peat from their own land for personal use. As a result, only 1.9% of Irish energy now comes from peat.

♻️ Renewable Energy Resources

The energy resources people rely on now are mostly non-renewable or finite, meaning they will eventually run out. Because of this, the use and production of renewable energy needs to be developed faster. Types of renewable energy include: hydroelectric power (HEP), wind energy, solar energy, geothermal energy, and biomass energy.

🗺️
Identifying on OS Maps Hydroelectric power station: ○ (Stáisiún cumhachta uisce / Power Station Hydro). Wind farm: ✝ (Feirm Ghaeoithe / Wind Farm).
💧 Hydroelectric Power (HEP)

Hydroelectric power (HEP) is the creation of electricity by using the force of falling water. HEP is the largest source of renewable energy globally — it generates around 17% of the world's electricity.

How HEP Works:
  1. A concrete wall called a dam is built across a river to trap and control flow of water.
  2. Water builds up behind the dam, forming a reservoir. This acts as a supply of water for HEP production.
  3. When part of the dam is opened, water rushes through.
  4. The water is used to turn a turbine — a machine where liquid (water) or gas (wind) flows through and turns a wheel with blades to generate electricity.
Requirements for a HEP Station:
1
Water Supply
Constant supply of water. Built on fast-flowing, high-volume rivers.
2
Slope
A drop in the course of the river causes water to flow quickly.
3
Bedrock
Reservoir must be built on impermeable bedrock so water does not seep away.
4
Climate
HEP stations need areas with high levels of rainfall.
Impacts of HEP:
💰 Economic
  • Generates cheap, clean, renewable electricity
  • Water in reservoir can supply local area
  • Dam may help prevent flooding
👥 Social
  • Families may have to be relocated (costly and disruptive)
  • New roads and bridges built around reservoir and station
  • Reservoir can be a leisure facility (fishing, rowing)
🌿 Environmental
  • Creation of reservoir may mean loss of good farmland
  • Dam can stop fish swimming upstream — reduces fish stocks
📋 Case Study
Hydroelectric Power in Ireland: Inniscarra Dam

Inniscarra Dam was constructed in the 1950s as part of the River Lee Hydroelectric Scheme. The dam is located west of Ballincollig in Co. Cork. The dam stands at a height of 42 m.

The Inniscarra reservoir (also known as Inniscarra lake) was created in 1956 by flooding parts of the Lee valley, west of Cork City. The reservoir occupies an area of 530 hectares or 5 km². There are approximately 40 km of bank area that can be fished.

The Inniscarra HEP station generates 27 megawatts (MW) of electricity for the area. A megawatt (MW) is a unit used to measure electricity — one megawatt powers about 600 homes.

📏
Key Terms Megawatt (MW) = unit of electricity. 1 MW powers ~600 homes.  |  Gigawatt (GW) = 1,000 megawatts.
💨 Wind Energy

The force of the wind can be used to turn turbines and create electricity. The blades on wind turbines can deal with winds blowing at up to 320 km/h. Modern wind turbines are very large in size and are generally grouped together in what is known as a wind farm.

Wind energy is a clean source used by more than 127 countries worldwide. It is becoming increasingly important in Ireland. Currently, over 35% of Ireland's electricity is produced by wind.

Ireland and Wind Energy:

Ireland is an ideal location because it is an island on the edge of Europe right by the Atlantic Ocean. The strong winds from the Atlantic make the west coast one of the best places for a wind farm. Most wind farms are located in upland areas near the west coast and on cutaway bogs (bogs no longer used to produce peat).

Ireland's largest wind farm is Oweninny Wind Farm in north Co. Mayo. It creates enough energy to meet the demands of around 140,000 homes. Wind energy now employs more than 5,000 people nationally.

💡 The largest wind farm in the world is located in the province of Gansu in China. Ireland plans to produce 5 GW of electricity by 2030, 20 GW by 2040 and 37 GW by 2050 through wind energy — this would cover 80% of Ireland's energy needs.
✅ Advantages of Wind Energy
  • Clean energy resource that is sustainable
  • Cuts overall costs of energy as it reduces the need to import energy sources
  • Creates employment in rural areas, both in construction and maintenance
❌ Disadvantages of Wind Energy
  • Wind is unreliable from day to day
  • Wind farms are very expensive to build
  • Noise and visual pollution can impact local residents and lead to planning objections
  • Wind farms can be hazardous for local wildlife (e.g. birds)
☀️ Solar Energy

Solar energy is created using heat and light from the Sun. It is the fastest growing renewable energy source in the world. Solar cells on solar panels capture the heat and light and convert this into electricity.

In Ireland, you can see solar panels on the roofs of many homes. Solar energy has become Ireland's fastest-growing renewable power source. The SEAI provides grants to homeowners to install solar panels. It is estimated that solar energy on a sunny day accounts for powering the equivalent of over 280,000 homes.

Around 75% of solar energy is produced from May to September. Excess electricity can be exported to the national grid or stored in a battery. In 2023, the Irish government announced a programme for schools to install solar panels. Dublin Airport has a solar farm with 15,000 solar panels that can power more than 10% of the airport's electricity needs.

🌋 Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy is created by using the heat from the Earth's crust.

  1. Water is pumped deep into the Earth's crust and heated by the rocks.
  2. The heated water is then pumped back to the surface where it can be used to heat homes.
  3. The hot water can also be converted to steam, which is used to turn turbines and generate electricity.

Geothermal energy can be exploited in areas where there is volcanic activity. Iceland and New Zealand are two countries that produce huge amounts of geothermal energy.

🌿 Biomass Energy

Biomass energy comes from burning organic material. Examples include wood logs, chips, pellets, manure, sewage and certain crops like rapeseed oil.

Biomass materials can also be used to produce biofuels. These biofuels include biodiesel, which is used to power engines such as those used in agricultural machinery. This cuts down on the use of non-renewable fuels like petrol and diesel.

🇮🇪 Ireland and Hydroelectric Power

Ireland has a long history of using hydroelectric power. The country's first and largest HEP station, Ardnacrusha HEP Station in Co. Clare, was built in 1929. About 2.5% of Ireland's electricity is generated from hydroelectricity. HEP is ideal for Ireland due to the climate and annual rainfall levels.

🌍 Environmental Consequences of Energy Production

Burning fossil fuels has a major impact on the environment, both on a local and global scale. The three main impacts of fossil fuel use are:

🌡️
1. Global Warming
The increase in the temperature of the Earth due to human activities like burning fossil fuels, caused by greenhouse gases trapping the Sun's heat.
🌧️
2. Acid Rain
Formed when gases from burning fossil fuels (sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide) mix with water vapour in the atmosphere.
🌫️
3. Smog
Pollution that occurs when smoke from burning fossil fuels mixes with sunlight and heat. It looks like fog and is caused by smoke.
🌡️ Global Warming

Global warming is the increase in the temperature of the Earth due to human activities like burning fossil fuels. The Earth's temperature is gradually rising every year. Since 2000, the Earth has experienced one record-breaking hottest year after another.

Although global warming has been happening for hundreds of years, scientists believe it got faster since the Industrial Revolution in the 1700s, because people have been burning more and more fossil fuels to produce energy.

Greenhouse Gases
Burning fossil fuels causes an increase in greenhouse gases, which trap the Sun's heat in the atmosphere, causing temperatures to rise. The resulting climate change is the biggest environmental issue of our time.
🌧️ Acid Rain

Acid rain is formed when gases from the burning of fossil fuels mix with water vapour.

  • The gases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide are released into the atmosphere when fossil fuels are burned in factories.
  • These gases combine with water vapour in the atmosphere and fall as acid rain.
pH Value
A pH value is a scale of measurement of how acidic or alkaline a substance is. Pure water has a pH of 7 (neutral). Normal rain has a pH of around 5.6. Acid rain can have a pH of around 4 — making it acidic enough to cause serious damage.
Problems Caused by Acid Rain:
  • Washes nutrients from the soil — stops crops from growing and forces farmers to use more fertilisers
  • Can wipe out fish stocks in lakes (fish cannot reproduce if water is too acidic)
  • Forests become vulnerable to disease — many trees can die (Black Forest in Germany is heavily affected)
  • Cities can be damaged as chemicals in acid rain attack old buildings and weather (wear away) the stone
Solutions to Acid Rain:
  • Use more renewable and clean energy resources (solar, wind)
  • Use natural gas instead of coal in power stations (cleaner fuel)
  • Use filters on coal and oil power stations to reduce sulfur dioxide emissions
  • Promote use of electric or hybrid cars (by 2030, all new registered cars in Ireland must be electric or hybrid)
  • Encourage people to use public transport
📊
Success Story Acid rain was a big problem in Europe and North America in the 1980s and 1990s, but measures were introduced (replacing coal with natural gas) and acid rain has significantly reduced in these regions. However, in Asia — especially China — acid rain remains a major environmental problem.
🌫️ Smog

Smog is pollution that occurs when smoke from burning fossil fuels mixes with sunlight and heat. Because it looks like fog and is caused by smoke, it is called smog.

Smog was common during the last century in industrial areas. In 1952, the smog was so bad in London that roads, railway lines and airports were closed. It caused around 4,000 deaths. It remains a familiar sight in cities today, especially in the densely populated and booming cities of China and India.

🃏 Key Terms Flashcards
What are energy resources?
Key Term
Resources that the physical world provides us with that we can make energy from.
What is the difference between renewable and non-renewable energy?
Core Concept
Renewable energy can be used over and over again and will not run out (e.g. wind, solar, HEP, geothermal, biomass). Non-renewable energy can only be used once and will eventually run out (e.g. oil, coal, gas, peat).
What are fossil fuels? Give three examples.
Fossil Fuels
Fuels made from decomposing plants and animals. Examples: oil, coal, gas (and peat). Around 80% of the world's energy and 86% of Ireland's energy comes from fossil fuels.
What is OPEC and what does it do?
Oil
Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries. It sets oil prices and causes them to fluctuate (rise and fall). Prices fall when there is a lot of oil available, and rise if it is difficult to access oil due to conflicts in oil-producing countries.
What is HEP? How does it work?
HEP
Hydroelectric power (HEP) is the creation of electricity by using the force of falling water. A dam is built across a river, water builds up forming a reservoir, then released to turn a turbine and generate electricity.
Name the 4 requirements for a HEP station.
HEP
1. Water supply — constant supply on fast-flowing rivers. 2. Slope — a drop in the course of the river. 3. Bedrock — impermeable bedrock so water does not seep away. 4. Climate — high levels of rainfall.
What is geothermal energy?
Geothermal
Energy created by using the heat from the Earth's crust. Water is pumped deep into the crust, heated by rocks, then pumped back to the surface to heat homes or generate electricity. Best exploited in areas with volcanic activity — e.g. Iceland, New Zealand.
What is biomass energy? What is a biofuel?
Biomass
Biomass energy comes from burning organic material (wood logs, chips, pellets, manure, sewage, crops like rapeseed). Biomass materials can produce biofuels such as biodiesel, used to power engines and reduce use of non-renewable fuels.
What is global warming? What causes it?
Global Warming
The increase in the temperature of the Earth due to human activities like burning fossil fuels. Burning fossil fuels increases greenhouse gases which trap the Sun's heat in the atmosphere, causing temperatures to rise. Got faster since the Industrial Revolution (1700s).
How is acid rain formed? What problems does it cause?
Acid Rain
Formed when sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide (released by burning fossil fuels) mix with water vapour in the atmosphere and fall as acid rain. Problems: washes nutrients from soil; kills fish in lakes; damages forests (e.g. Black Forest, Germany); erodes stone buildings.
What is smog? Where is it a problem?
Smog
Pollution that occurs when smoke from burning fossil fuels mixes with sunlight and heat. Looks like fog, caused by smoke. Remains a major problem in densely populated cities in China and India. In London in 1952, smog caused around 4,000 deaths.
Name the Corrib gas field case study: 3 economic impacts
Corrib
1. Cost €3.6 billion to develop. 2. Produces 100% of Ireland's home-produced gas (30% of overall gas needs). 3. Created over 1,400 jobs during construction, currently employs 150. Roads upgraded costing €22 million.
Name the Inniscarra Dam case study: key facts
Inniscarra
Built in the 1950s on the River Lee. Located west of Ballincollig, Co. Cork. Dam height: 42 m. Reservoir = 530 hectares / 5 km². Generates 27 MW of electricity (enough for ~16,200 homes).
