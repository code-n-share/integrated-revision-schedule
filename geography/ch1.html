🌍 What is Geography?

Geography is the study of the world around you. It explores where you live and makes you think about your village, town, city, country and the whole world.

🏔️
Strand 1: Exploring the Physical World
Looking at the physical world and examining how and why the world is shaped the way it is. E.g. formation of soil (Ch 16).
🌾
Strand 2: Interacting with the Physical World
Looking at how people use the planet Earth to their advantage and the impacts of this. E.g. growing crops (Ch 4), desertification (Ch 16).
🌆
Strand 3: People, Place & Change
Looking at the people in the world, the places they live and the conditions in which they live.
These three strands overlap — most topics in Geography involve all three!
📏 Scale & Ratio
Scale
The scale of a map is the ratio of the distance on the map to the corresponding distance on the ground.
💡 Ratio is how much of one thing there is compared to another. Trick: multiply or divide by the same value.
Discovery Ireland Series
1 : 50,000
1 cm on map = 500 m (0.5 km) on ground
Every 2 cm = 1 km
Urban / Town Maps
1 : 10,000
1 cm on map = 100 m (0.1 km) on ground
Every 10 cm = 1 km
⚠️
Larger scale = more detail, smaller area shown. 1:10,000 shows MORE detail than 1:50,000. The bigger the second number, the LESS detail shown.
🇮🇪 The National Grid

The national grid is used to locate places on 1:50,000 OS maps. The grid divides Ireland into 25 squares, each called a subzone, covering 100 km × 100 km. Each subzone is labelled with a letter (A–Z, skipping I).

➡️
Eastings
Vertical lines on OS maps. Numbers get higher as you go further east.
Think: "Go in the door" — move along east first!
⬆️
Northings
Horizontal lines on OS maps. Numbers get higher as you go further north.
Think: "Go upstairs" — then go up (north)!
📍 Grid References

Grid references locate areas or exact points on an OS map. The term LEN helps you remember the order:

LLetter of Subzone
EEasting (across)
NNorthing (up)
🚪
"Go in the door before you go upstairs" Always write the easting (across) BEFORE the northing (up). E comes before N in the alphabet — and in grid references!
Four-Figure Grid Reference

Identifies a 1 km² grid square. Used for areas (e.g. a forest, a lake).

  1. Write the subzone letter (e.g. V)
  2. Read the easting — two-digit number at the bottom-left corner of the square (e.g. 44)
  3. Read the northing — two-digit number at the bottom-left corner (e.g. 99)
  4. Combine: V 44 99
Six-Figure Grid Reference

Pinpoints an exact location (e.g. a church, post office). Imagine each grid square divided further into 10 × 10.

  1. Write the subzone letter (e.g. V)
  2. Write the two-digit easting, then estimate tenths into the square → easting becomes 3 digits (e.g. 44 → 447)
  3. Write the two-digit northing, then estimate tenths up the square → northing becomes 3 digits (e.g. 99 → 996)
  4. Result: V 447 996
Grid Reference on a 1:10,000 Map
📘 Example — 1:10,000 Grid Reference
On a 1:10,000 map, move along the bottom to find the letter directly under your square, then up the side to find the number directly beside your square. Combine them: e.g. B1.

⛰️ Identifying Height

Height is shown in five ways on a 1:50,000 OS map:

🎨
Colour
Green = lowland (up to 200 m). Changes to light cream → light brown → dark brown as height increases. Dark brown = upland.
〰️
Contours
Lines joining places of equal height. Placed 10 m apart. Closer together = steeper slope.
△
Triangulation Pillars
Concrete pillars on mountain peaks/hilltops. Shown as triangle with height beside it.
•
Spot Heights
A black dot with a number showing the exact height in metres at that point. Can appear anywhere on the map.
🏔️
Summit Heights
Coloured dot showing the highest point of a hill/mountain. Brown = 600m+, Orange = 400–599m, Green = below 400m.
Summit
The highest point of a hill or mountain.
Contour Lines
Lines on a map that show areas of equal height. Height in metres is written on them — placed 10 m apart. Closer lines = steeper slope.
🧭 Direction

Direction is given using the cardinal points of the compass: North, South, East and West. Between these are intermediate points: NE, NW, SE, SW, and finer points like NNE, ENE, etc. North is always shown on OS maps.

North is always shown at the top of OS maps with an N arrow symbol.

💡
Direction tip: When stating direction, note whether you are travelling towards an area or coming from it. "I am travelling in a northerly direction from Cork to Dublin."
✏️ Sketch Maps
Sketch Map
A sketch map is a hand-drawn copy of a part of an OS map. Features must be drawn to scale and not out of proportion.
How to Draw a Sketch Map — 10 Steps
  1. Always use a pencil.
  2. Draw a frame: measure the OS map width ÷ 2 and height ÷ 2 to get your frame size in cm.
  3. Add a title: "Sketch map of _______ OS map".
  4. Divide the OS map into 8 sections using a ruler (draw diagonals + centre cross).
  5. Divide the sketch map frame into the same 8 sections.
  6. Draw any major physical feature (coastline, upland, river, large lake) to help with location.
  7. Draw only the features asked — don't add extra detail.
  8. Draw a key / legend using different symbols and colours for each feature.
  9. Draw the north arrow and the subzone letter.
  10. Rub out the section lines drawn in step 5.
📊 Bar Charts
Bar Chart
Bar charts show data in a simple form using rectangles of different heights. They help compare two or more pieces of information.
🧂 Remember SALT when drawing any graph!
S
Scale
Draw a scale on the axes
A
Axes
Check intervals are even on the Axes
L
Labels
Check the axes are Labelled
T
Title
Give your graph a Title
📋 How to Draw a Bar Chart
  • Write a title describing the information being shown.
  • Draw an x-axis (horizontal) and a y-axis (vertical).
  • x-axis: work out how many groups to show (e.g. 8 years → 8 bars).
  • Make bars the same width; leave equal space between each. Label each bar.
  • y-axis always starts at 0. Choose appropriate units and even intervals.
  • Label both axes with titles AND units.
🥧 Pie Charts
Pie Chart
A pie chart is a circular graph. Data is shown in slices or sectors. The size of the slice shows how important that slice is. Pie charts usually show data in percentages.
📋 How to Draw a Pie Chart (you need: compass, protractor, calculator)
  • A full circle = 360° = 100%. Total of all data = 360°.
  • Find each value as a percentage: (value ÷ total) × 100.
  • Find each value in degrees: (value ÷ total) × 360°.
  • Draw a circle with a compass. Draw a line from the centre to the edge (your starting line).
  • Use a protractor to measure each sector's angle. Draw a line. This becomes the start of the next sector.
  • Label each sector or provide a colour-coded key.

📈 Trend Graphs
Trend Graph
Trend graphs show a trend or pattern of data over a period of time. They can also show the relationship between two or more sets of data.
💡 Example: Global demand for energy vs. supply of fossil fuels — one rising, one falling over time.
📋 How to Draw a Trend Graph
  • Write a title. Draw an x-axis and y-axis.
  • x-axis: time (years, months, etc.) — label it.
  • y-axis: starts at 0. Choose units and even intervals — label it.
  • Plot each data point for each group/line.
  • Join the points with a line to show the trend. Use different colours for different lines.
  • Include a key/legend if there are multiple lines.
📐 Units of Measurement
Percentages
Percentage Formula
To get a percentage: divide the number by the total and multiply by 100.
Example: 18 out of 30 → (18 ÷ 30) × 100 = 60%
Area
Area
Area = length × width. Measured in mm², cm², m², km², acres, or hectares.
💡 1 hectare = 10,000 m². 1 acre ≈ just over 4,000 m². A 1:50,000 OS map covering 8 grid squares × 10 grid squares = 80 km².

What is a cartographer?
OS Maps
A person who creates maps. Maps are representations of the world created by cartographers.
What is the national grid?
OS Maps
A grid used to locate places on 1:50,000 OS maps. Divided into 25 subzones (100 km × 100 km each), each with a letter (A–Z, no I).
What does LEN stand for in grid references?
Grid Refs
L = Letter of the subzone · E = Easting (across first) · N = Northing (up second). Always "go in the door before you go upstairs."
What is the difference between a 4-figure and 6-figure grid reference?
Grid Refs
4-figure: identifies a 1 km² grid square (an area). 6-figure: pinpoints an exact location within that square by estimating tenths into the square (adds one extra digit to both easting and northing).
What do contour lines show? What do closely-spaced contours mean?
Height
Contour lines join places of equal height. They are placed 10 m apart. Contours that are close together indicate a steep slope. Far apart = gentle slope.
What are the five ways height is shown on a 1:50,000 OS map?
Height
1. Colour (green → cream → brown) · 2. Contour lines · 3. Triangulation pillars (△) · 4. Spot heights (•) · 5. Summit heights (coloured dot)
What is a sketch map?
Sketch Maps
A hand-drawn copy of a part of an OS map. It must be drawn to scale, include a title, key, north sign and subzone letter. Always drawn in pencil.
What does SALT stand for in graph drawing?
Graphs
Scale · Axes (even intervals) · Labels (both axes) · Title
What is a ratio? Give a map scale example.
Scale
Ratio is how much of one thing there is compared to another. On a 1:50,000 map, 1 cm on the map = 50,000 cm (500 m) on the ground.
What is a triangulation pillar?
Height
A concrete pillar used by map-makers, usually found on mountain peaks or hilltops. Shown as a triangle (△) with the height in metres beside it.
