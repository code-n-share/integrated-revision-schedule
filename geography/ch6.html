🪨 Weathering & Erosion

The rocks and soil of the Earth's crust are constantly being worn down by processes known as denudation. The two main processes of denudation are weathering and erosion.

🪨
Weathering
The breaking down of rocks and soil that are exposed to the weather. The broken-down material does not move from the place where weathering occurred.
💨
Erosion
The breaking down of rocks and soil and the transportation of the eroded material. Erosion is caused by moving water, moving ice (glaciers), and moving air (wind).
🌍
Denudation
The overall process of wearing down the Earth's crust. It includes both weathering and erosion working together over time.

There are three types of weathering: mechanical weathering, chemical weathering, and biological weathering.

❄️ Mechanical Weathering
Mechanical Weathering
The physical breaking up of rocks into smaller pieces by putting pressure on the rock. The rock breaks up but its chemical composition does not change.
Freeze-Thaw Action
Freeze-Thaw Action
A type of mechanical weathering where rock is broken down due to water in the cracks repeatedly freezing and thawing. This occurs high up in mountainous areas where there is a lot of precipitation and the temperatures regularly rise and fall below freezing point (0°C). Temperatures that repeatedly rise and fall are called fluctuating temperatures.
Process — How It Works
1
Day
Water seeps into cracks and joints in the rock during the day.
2
Night
Temperatures fall below freezing. Water freezes and expands by 9%, putting pressure on the rock.
3
Over Time
Cracks widen and joints weaken. Eventually pieces break off — the broken-down rock is called scree. Scree collects at the base of a slope.
📍 Example
Freeze-thaw action occurs in upland areas like Croagh Patrick in Co. Mayo. Temperatures here frequently rise above and fall below freezing point.
🧪 Chemical Weathering
Chemical Weathering
When rocks are dissolved because of a chemical reaction. The chemical composition of the rock is changed in the process.
Carbonation
Carbonation
A type of chemical weathering in which limestone is broken down by the carbonic acid in rainwater.
Process — How Carbonation Works
  1. The atmosphere contains carbon dioxide (CO₂).
  2. Rainwater (H₂O) mixes with the carbon dioxide (CO₂) to form a weak carbonic acid (H₂CO₃).
  3. Limestone is composed mostly of calcium carbonate. It reacts when it comes in contact with the weak carbonic acid in the rainwater, causing it to dissolve.
  4. Limestone is permeable, so rainwater can pass right down through the rock, creating unique karst landscapes.
📍 Irish Example
The next section looks at how carbonation has created a unique landscape in the Burren, a limestone region in Co. Clare.
🌿 Biological Weathering
Biological Weathering
When rocks are broken down by plants or animals. The roots of plants can enter cracks in rocks, and as they grow, they widen the cracks and weaken the rock, eventually breaking it. Animals burrowing in the ground can also dig into cracks, further widening and weakening the rock.
🗺️ Carbonation & Karst Landscapes
Karst Landscape
A limestone area with surface and underground features made by carbonation.
💡 Karst landscapes are named after a limestone region called Karst in Slovenia.

An example of a karst landscape in Ireland is the Burren in Co. Clare. The soil was removed from the Burren by ice sheets during the last ice age. This left the bare limestone rock exposed to the weather. Carbonation has created a unique landscape in the region.

🏔️ Surface Features of a Karst Landscape
Limestone Pavement
Limestone Pavement
The surface of a karst landscape is called a limestone pavement.
How a Limestone Pavement Forms
  1. Rainwater falls onto the exposed limestone and passes through joints — long vertical cracks in limestone rock.
  2. Carbonation makes the joints wider by dissolving the calcium carbonate in the limestone until they look like grooves or gaps. These grooves or gaps are called grikes.
  3. The blocks of limestone left in between the grikes are called clints.
🔲
Clint
A block of limestone that is left standing between the grikes. The exposed surface of a limestone pavement.
〰️
Grike
A groove or gap in the limestone pavement formed by carbonation dissolving the rock along a joint.
🕳️
Joint
A long vertical crack in limestone rock through which rainwater flows, allowing carbonation to occur.
Swallow Hole
Swallow Hole
Rivers that flow over a limestone surface can disappear underground through a swallow hole.
How a Swallow Hole Forms
  1. As the river flows over limestone, the water widens the joints in the rock through carbonation.
  2. Eventually, the river disappears from the surface and begins to flow underground along the bedding planes — the horizontal spaces between the layers or strata in limestone rock.
  3. Carbonation and hydraulic action (the physical force of the moving water) enlarge the joints and bedding planes.
  4. The place where the river disappears underground is called a swallow hole.
📍 Example
Pollnagollum swallow hole in the Burren is over 6 m wide and 16 m deep.
🕳️ Underground Features
Caves and Caverns
🦇
Cave
A natural hollow space under the ground that has an opening on the surface. Formed as rivers flow underground through swallow holes, enlarging passages by carbonation and hydraulic action.
🌑
Cavern
An underground cave that has an opening that is also underground. Formed by the same processes as caves, but the space grows even larger over time.
📍 Irish Examples
The Aillwee Caves in the Burren, Co. Clare and Crag Cave in Castleisland, Co. Kerry.
Stalactites

As water seeps through the rock, it carries dissolved limestone with it. Some of this water eventually reaches the roof of a cave or cavern below ground.

  1. These water-drops on the roof slowly evaporate and leave behind small deposits of pure limestone called calcite.
  2. Gradually, the calcite builds up to form icicle-like shapes called stalactites, which hang from the roof.
Stalagmites

Drops of water may also seep through the rock and fall on the ground.

  1. This water evaporates and leaves deposits of calcite on the floor of the cave directly below the stalactites.
  2. The calcite builds up, forming upside-down icicle-like shapes called stalagmites.
Pillars (Columns)

After thousands of years, a stalactite and a stalagmite can meet and join, resulting in the formation of a pillar or column.

💡
Top Tip: Stalactites vs Stalagmites The g in stalagmite stands for ground, and the c in stalactite stands for ceiling. Stalactites hang from the ceiling; stalagmites grow from the ground.

🌿 Case Study: The Burren, Co. Clare
📋 Case Study — A Karst Landscape: The Burren, Co. Clare

The limestone of the Burren was formed around 350 million years ago under the sea. The Burren is now a region of exposed limestone. Chemical weathering in the Burren has resulted in a unique landscape in Ireland.

The region is also known for:

  • Its historical attractions, with over 2,700 ancient monuments dating back 6,000 years.
  • Its unique mix of wildflowers, including flowers found in Alpine, Arctic and Mediterranean regions.
  • The Cliffs of Moher, which receives upwards of 1.6 million visitors each year.

Tourism in the Burren

Tourism in the Burren is promoted by its inclusion on the popular Wild Atlantic Way tourist trail, which spans the entire west coast of Ireland. The region is also part of the Burren and Cliffs of Moher UNESCO Geopark, which are internationally protected areas that promote sustainable tourism.

With upwards of one million people passing through the region annually, tourism can have many advantages and disadvantages:

✅ Advantages

  • Tourism provides employment in the area for local people in the form of restaurants, accommodation, leisure facilities and transport.
  • It reduces migration from the area, meaning younger people have opportunities for employment locally.
  • It brings income to the region and therefore improves services and infrastructure.

❌ Disadvantages

  • Increased pollution caused by tourism can cause damage to the limestone and the natural flora and fauna.
  • New buildings and increased services can destroy the scenic beauty of the region.
  • Visitors can cause damage to the rare flora and fauna of the region.

Transport in the Burren

The Burren is an area where road access is limited due to the physical landscape. There is only one national secondary road (the N67) that runs through the Burren, and the coastal route is serviced by a regional road. These roads are unsuitable for the current amount of traffic that uses them. Due to tourism, upwards of 20,000 buses use the road network in the Burren annually, causing huge disruption.

⛰️ Mass Movement

Weathering and erosion produce loose material called regolith.

🪨
Regolith
Made up of rock particles, dust and soil that sits on the Earth's bedrock, produced by weathering and erosion.
⛰️
Mass Movement
When regolith moves down a slope due to gravity. It is classified by the speed at which it occurs — slow or fast.
🔍 Factors That Affect Mass Movement

Mass movement is usually caused by two or more of the following factors:

📐 Gradient
Gradient refers to the steepness of the slope. Mass movement generally occurs in upland areas or areas that have steep slopes. The steeper the slope, the faster the movement of the regolith.
💧 Water Content
Water makes regolith heavier and saturated (absorbed all the water it possibly can). Heavier, more saturated regolith moves downslope faster. Water also acts as a lubricant, reducing friction and making movement easier.
🏗️ Human Activity
People sometimes dig into slopes during construction of things such as wind turbines, roads, quarrying or mining. Digging into the slope makes it steeper and more unstable, leading to mass movement.
🌱 Vegetation
Grass and shrubs protect the soil from weathering and erosion. Plant and tree roots bind the soil together and keep it stable. When trees and vegetation are removed, it can lead to mass movement.
🐄 Animals
Burrowing animals can loosen the soil, making it unstable. Overgrazing by cattle and sheep removes the vegetation cover, exposing the soil and making the slope unstable and prone to mass movement.
🏔️ Types of Mass Movement

Mass movement is classified by the speed at which it occurs. There are slow (soil creep) and fast (bog bursts, mudflows, landslides and avalanches) mass movements.

🐢 Slow
Soil Creep
Soil creep is the slow movement of soil down a slope under the influence of gravity. It is the slowest form of mass movement and becomes evident only when you examine the landscape closely.
Evidence of soil creep:
• Trees grow at an angle, with the base of the tree turned downslope.
• A series of steps called terracettes form, giving the surface a wrinkled appearance.
• Fences bend and telegraph poles tilt downhill.
• Walls crack and break when soil piles up behind them.
⚡ Fast
Bog Burst (Bog Slide)
A bog burst is the sudden movement of a mass of bog or peat down a slope after a period of heavy rainfall. The peat becomes so saturated that water can no longer soak downwards, so the peat moves downslope. It can block roads, knock down trees, damage roads and buildings and pollute lakes and rivers.
⚡ Fast
Mudflows
Mudflows occur when soil and regolith become saturated with water after periods of heavy rainfall and move downslope like a river of mud. They are one of the fastest forms of mass movement and can reach speeds of over 100 km/h. Mudflows can also occur in the aftermath of a volcano — this meltwater mixes with ash, soil and rock fragments to create a particularly dangerous kind of mudflow known as a lahar.
⚡ Fast
Landslides
A landslide is the rapid movement of regolith down a steep slope that has become unstable. Causes of landslides include coastal erosion, deforestation, heavy rainfall, earthquakes and undercutting by road building or quarrying.
Did you know? The biggest landslide in recorded history was triggered by the eruption of Mount St Helens in 1980. Nearly 3 km³ of rock moved downslope.
⚡ Fast
Avalanches
An avalanche is the rapid movement of snow and ice downslope when the weight of the snow becomes too much for the slope to hold. In February 2023, several avalanches over the course of two days led to the deaths of 10 people in the Austrian-Swiss Alps.
📋 Case Study — Landslide: Papua New Guinea

The mountainous area of Maip Mulitaka, Enga Province, Papua New Guinea, is home to six villages and an estimated 4,000 people. At 3 a.m. local time, on 23 May 2024, a huge landslide occurred there.

The landslide occurred in this upland area due to the extraordinarily high levels of rainfall the region experienced in the weeks and days leading up to the disaster. Due to the timing of the landslide, casualties were much higher as people were asleep when the landslide buried their homes.

Impact of the Landslide

  • Over 2,000 people were buried and killed as a result of the landslide.
  • Over 7,800 people were displaced and lost their homes.
  • The regolith was 10 m deep in some areas.
  • Upwards of 200 m of the main highway linking the remote region was blocked and covered.

Response

  • Residents had to use shovels and their bare hands to dig and search for survivors.
  • Rescue teams were slow to reach the region due to its remoteness and the road access being blocked by the landslide.
  • Military equipment was eventually brought in from over 400 km away but was not used due to the fear of triggering more landslides in the area.
  • Australia, Papua New Guinea's nearest neighbour, pledged an AU$4.5 million aid package to the affected area.
🛑 Controlling Mass Movement

Humans can try to control natural disasters caused by mass movement. These hazards can be reduced or prevented in the following ways:

🌳
Vegetation
Trees and vines can be planted on slopes to stabilise them. Their roots bind the soil and prevent regolith from moving.
🪜
Steps
Steps can be built into a mountainside to trap moving material and slow down or prevent mass movement.
🐄
Restrict Overgrazing
Restricting overgrazing keeps vegetation cover on slopes and stops soil creep from occurring.
💥
Controlled Explosions
Controlled explosions in mountainous areas can be used to trigger small avalanches, making large, life-threatening avalanches less likely.
🔖 Key Terms Flashcards
What is denudation?
Weathering
Denudation is the breakdown of rocks and soil that are exposed on the Earth's surface. The two main processes are weathering and erosion.
What is freeze-thaw action and where does it occur?
Mechanical
Freeze-thaw action is a type of mechanical weathering where rock is broken down due to water in cracks repeatedly freezing and thawing. It occurs in mountainous areas where temperatures fluctuate above and below 0°C — e.g. Croagh Patrick, Co. Mayo.
What is scree?
Mechanical
Scree is the broken-down rock that falls to the base of a slope as a result of freeze-thaw action. The pieces pile up at the base.
Explain how carbonation occurs.
Chemical
Rainwater (H₂O) mixes with CO₂ in the atmosphere to form weak carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). This acid reacts with calcium carbonate in limestone, dissolving it. This creates karst features like grikes, swallow holes, caves and stalactites.
What are clints and grikes?
Karst
Clints are the blocks of limestone left in between the grikes on a limestone pavement. Grikes are the grooves or gaps in the limestone pavement formed when carbonation widens the joints.
What is the difference between a stalactite and a stalagmite?
Karst
A stalactite hangs from the ceiling of a cave — built up from calcite deposits left by dripping water. A stalagmite grows upward from the ground — formed by the same process directly below. (Tip: ceiling = stalactite; ground = stalagmite)
What is mass movement? Name five factors that affect it.
Mass Movement
Mass movement is when regolith moves down a slope due to gravity. Five factors: Gradient · Water content · Human activity · Vegetation · Animals.
Name two types of slow and three types of fast mass movement.
Mass Movement
Slow: Soil creep (and technically terracettes as evidence). Fast: Bog bursts · Mudflows (lahars) · Landslides · Avalanches.
What is a lahar?
Mudflows
A lahar is a particularly dangerous type of mudflow that occurs in the aftermath of a volcanic eruption. Meltwater mixes with ash, soil and rock fragments to create a fast-moving river of mud.
Give three ways humans can control mass movement.
Controlling
1. Plant vegetation (trees/vines) on slopes to bind soil. 2. Build steps into a mountainside to trap moving material. 3. Controlled explosions to trigger small avalanches and prevent large ones. Also: restrict overgrazing to preserve vegetation cover.
