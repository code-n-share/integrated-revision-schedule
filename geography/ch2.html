🌍 The Structure of the Earth

Cross-section of the Earth — four distinct layers

🪨
Crust
The outside layer of the Earth. Made of solid rock. Oceans and continents lie on top of the crust.
🔥
Mantle
Lies under the crust. Contains hot, molten (melted) or semi-molten rock called magma, moved around by convection currents like bubbles in boiling water.
⚙️
Outer Core
Part of the core — the centre of the Earth. Made of iron and nickel. The hottest part at around 6,000°C.
💛
Inner Core
The very centre of the Earth. Also made of iron and nickel. Solid despite extreme heat due to immense pressure.
The core can be divided into inner and outer core.
🧩 Plates & Plate Tectonics
Plate
The Earth's crust is broken into pieces called plates. Like the cracked shell of a hard-boiled egg. Continental plates have land on top; oceanic plates have oceans on top.
Plate Boundary
The line where two plates meet is called a plate boundary.
Plate Tectonics
The study of plates and their movements. The theory was first proposed by German scientist Alfred Wegener.
Continental Drift & Pangaea

Wegener noticed the shapes of continents fit together like jigsaw pieces. He proposed they were once one huge landmass called Pangaea (meaning 'all land'). When it broke up, the continents moved to their present positions — this is called continental drift.

💡
Evidence for Continental Drift: In the 1950s scientists discovered the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — where the North American Plate is separating from the Eurasian Plate. This supported Wegener's theory.
Convection Currents

Magma in the mantle is moved in circular motion by convection currents, causing plates to move. This causes plates to collide, separate, or slide past each other.

  1. Magma is heated in the mantle and rises towards the crust.
  2. As it rises, it cools and sinks back down, creating a convection current.
  3. This circular motion is constantly repeated.
  4. The movement pushes and pulls the plates via slab pull and ridge push.
⬇️
Slab Pull
When older, denser plates sink into the mantle, pulling plates behind them.
↔️
Ridge Push
When new crust is formed, it pushes the plates at either side apart.
💥 What Happens at Plate Boundaries?
🔴 Destructive Boundary
(Convergent — plates collide)
Huge pressure builds up. The heavier plate is pushed under the lighter plate in a process called subduction. The heavier plate melts back into the mantle. The lighter plate is lifted up, forming fold mountains. Also causes earthquakes and volcanoes.
📍 Example: The Andes — Nazca Plate colliding with the South American Plate.
🟢 Constructive Boundary
(Divergent — plates separate)
Plates move apart, forming a gap. Magma rises to fill the gap, cools and hardens, forming new crust. Under the sea this is called sea-floor spreading. Forms volcanic mountains, volcanic islands and mid-ocean ridges.
📍 Example: Iceland — Eurasian Plate separating from the North American Plate.
🔵 Passive Boundary
(Transform — plates slide past)
Crust is neither created nor destroyed. Plates get locked together and release huge amounts of energy when they slide past each other. Causes earthquakes and creates fault lines (long, deep cracks in the Earth's crust).
📍 Example: San Andreas Fault, USA — Pacific Plate sliding past the North American Plate.
🌋 Volcanic Activity

Most volcanic activity happens around the edge of the Pacific Plate — this area is known as the Pacific Ring of Fire.

Parts of a Volcano
🏔️
Magma Chamber
An area within the Earth's crust where magma collects before an eruption.
🕳️
Vent
A narrow tube in the crust through which magma travels to the surface.
🔴
Lava
Magma that has reached the surface. It flows from the vent during an eruption.
🏔
Cone
The cone-shaped mountain formed by layers of lava, ash and rock hardening over many eruptions.
⭕
Crater
The opening at the top of the volcano from which lava continues to flow.
☁️
Ash Cloud
Hot ash and rocks thrown into the air as the volcano erupts, forming a cloud above it.
Types of Volcano
🔴
Active
Erupts regularly.
Examples: Mount Etna (Sicily, Italy), Mount St Helens (USA)
🟡
Dormant
Has not erupted for a long time but might erupt again.
Example: Mount Vesuvius (Italy)
⚫
Extinct
Will not erupt again.
Example: Croghan Hill, Co. Offaly (Ireland)
Mid-Ocean Ridges & Volcanic Islands
Mid-Ocean Ridge
Magma rises from the mantle through cracks in the crust at constructive boundaries. It cools, hardens and builds up a ridge of rock called a mid-ocean ridge.
📍 Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge — formed as the North American and South American Plates separate from the Eurasian and African Plates.
Volcanic Islands
Mountains on the seabed formed when magma wells up through cracks — sometimes rising above the ocean surface.
📍 Example: Iceland
⚠️ Impacts of Volcanic Activity
Socio-economic
Refers to both social factors (people) and economic factors (money) and how they relate to each other.
✅ Positive Impacts
  • Soils from lava are rich in minerals — excellent for farming (e.g. Campania, Italy).
  • Tourists visit famous volcanoes — economic boost.
  • Hot springs (geysers) are tourist attractions.
  • Geothermal energy — renewable electricity from underground heat.
❌ Negative Impacts
  • Lava flows destroy homes and land — loss of life.
  • Ash clouds disrupt air travel (e.g. Iceland 2010 — 100,000+ flights cancelled).
  • Sulfur dioxide gases cause acid rain and poison nearby communities.
  • Lahars — rivers of mud (volcanic material + water) can destroy towns (e.g. Colombia 1985: 22,000 deaths).
Lahar
A river of mud formed when volcanic material mixes with water from rivers, rain or melting snow and ice. Can destroy towns and villages.
Geothermal Energy
A renewable form of energy that uses heat from underground volcanic activity to generate electricity.
Case Study 🇮🇸 Volcanic Activity: Iceland

Iceland has high volcanic activity because it sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. The physical landscape has greatly influenced the economic and social activities of its people.

♻️ Geothermal Energy
  • Earth's heat used to heat water → steam → generates electricity.
  • Powers factories, offices, houses, offices and schools.
  • One of the cheapest and most sustainable energy forms.
  • Sustainability = using Earth's resources responsibly so they are available for future generations.
🌾 Agriculture
  • About 20% of Iceland's land is fertile (rich in volcanic minerals).
  • Iceland is self-sufficient in meat, dairy products and eggs.
  • Crops (tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, flowers) grown in geothermally heated greenhouses.
  • Geothermal water reused to water plants — sustainable water use.
🏔️ Tourism
  • Over 2 million visitors per year — 6× Iceland's population.
  • Attractions: black-sand beaches, geysers, geothermal spas (e.g. Blue Lagoon), 32 active volcanoes.
  • Tourism provides many jobs but can cause soil erosion and rising property prices.
Case Study 🌋 Natural Disaster: Cumbre Vieja, La Palma (2021)

Cumbre Vieja volcano on La Palma, Canary Islands, erupted on 19 September 2021 — its first eruption since 1971. The eruption lasted 85 days. No deaths, but major damage.

💥 Damage Caused
  • 70 km of road networks destroyed, cutting off villages.
  • Over 3,000 buildings destroyed.
  • The town of Todoque (pop. 1,300) was completely lost.
  • 350 hectares of banana plantations ruined.
  • Over 1,000 people lost their homes.
⚡ Short-term Responses
  • Army deployed to help with evacuation.
  • All flights cancelled to/from La Palma airport.
  • Temporary shelters provided for 1,000+ displaced people.
  • Exclusion zone created along coastline where lava was heading.
🏗️ Long-term Responses
  • Estimated cost of damage: €843 million.
  • Spanish government pledged €225 million to help locals.
  • 400 people still in temporary accommodation one year later.
  • Studies underway to exploit geothermal energy as a sustainable future energy source.
🏚️ Earthquakes
Earthquake
Earthquakes are sudden tremors or vibrations in the Earth's crust. They occur when plates pressing against (colliding) or sliding past each other build up pressure along the fault line. When the plates move, the sudden release of pressure causes an earthquake.
📍
Focus
The point beneath the surface where the earthquake happens.
🎯
Epicentre
The point on the surface directly above the focus. Seismic waves are strongest here — greatest damage occurs closest to the epicentre.
🌊
Seismic Waves
Tremors/vibrations from an earthquake. Strongest at the epicentre, reducing in strength the farther away you go.
〰️
Aftershock
Smaller tremors that follow an earthquake.
🌊
Tsunami
A huge wave caused when an earthquake occurs under the sea, displacing water. Can travel to coastlines causing devastation.
⚡
Fault Line
A long, deep crack in the Earth's crust. Earthquakes occur along fault lines (e.g. San Andreas Fault, USA).
Measuring Earthquakes
Magnitude
The measure of the size of an earthquake.
Seismologist / Seismograph
A seismologist studies earthquake activity using instruments called seismographs to measure tremors in the Earth's crust.

Richter Scale: Ranges from 1–10. Each unit is 10 times stronger than the one before. So a magnitude 6 earthquake is 10× stronger than magnitude 5. The Moment Magnitude scale is used for larger earthquakes (over magnitude 8).
Damage Caused by Earthquakes
  • Loss of life.
  • Buildings damaged or collapsed.
  • Damage to electricity lines or gas pipelines may cause fires.
  • Damage to sewers and water supplies may spread disease.
  • Bridges, roads and railway lines damaged — difficult to get help into the area.
  • Landslides and avalanches can be triggered.
  • Earthquakes under the sea can cause tsunamis.
How to Reduce Earthquake Damage
  • Build earthquake-resistant buildings with shock absorbers between the building and foundations; reinforce walls with steel.
  • Conduct earthquake and tsunami drills so people know what to do.
  • Set up early-warning systems to alert people of possible tsunamis.
Case Study 🇹🇷 Earthquakes: Türkiye / Syria (6 February 2023)

An earthquake measuring 7.8 on the Moment Magnitude scale struck near Gaziantep, southern Türkiye. A second earthquake measuring 7.5 followed nine hours later. This was a result of the Eurasian and Arabian Plates colliding.

💥 Damage Caused
  • Over 56,000 people killed.
  • 3 million people displaced between the two countries.
  • 230,000 buildings destroyed in Türkiye + 10,600 in Syria.
  • In Aleppo (Syria): 3,500 buildings destroyed, 700 unsafe.
⚡ Short-term Response
  • Many countries sent emergency rescue teams.
  • WHO immediately sent health supplies.
  • Temporary camps set up for homeless people.
  • UN provided food assistance to 1.7 million people.
  • Rubble clear-up began immediately.
🏗️ Long-term Response
  • Total damages: up to €31.1 billion in Türkiye + €4.7 billion in Syria.
  • 210 million tonnes of rubble needed to be removed.
  • In Syria, poverty rose from 40% to 52% in the aftermath.
  • Many people still homeless or displaced long after the event.
⛰️ Fold Mountains
Fold Mountains
Fold mountains form when two plates collide with each other. When a heavier and lighter plate collide, the heavier plate sinks under (subduction) and the lighter plate buckles upwards forming fold mountains. The magma can move to the surface forming volcanoes.
↑
Anticline
An up-fold of rock — forms the peaks/ridges of fold mountains.
↓
Syncline
A down-fold of rock — forms the valleys between peaks.
Periods of Folding
PeriodWhen?Plates InvolvedExamples
Alpine 30–35 million years ago Various — formed the highest mountains Alps (Europe), Andes (S. America), Rockies (N. America), Himalayas (Asia)
Armorican ~250 million years ago Eurasian & African Plates MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Galtees, Comeragh, Knockmealdown Mtns (Ireland); Black Forest (Germany)
Caledonian ~400 million years ago Eurasian & American Plates Dublin & Wicklow Mountains (Ireland); Appalachian Mtns (N. America); Norway, Sweden, Scotland
💡
Irish fold mountains are much older (and therefore lower) than the Himalayas because they were formed during much earlier folding periods. Older = more time for erosion = lower mountains.
Mountains & People
✅ Economic Activities
  • Tourism: Mountaineering, hillwalking, skiing, river rafting, rock climbing. Creates many direct and indirect jobs.
  • Farming: Main type is cattle and sheep rearing. Transhumance (bringing cattle to summer pastures). Terraces cut for fruit and vines.
  • Forestry: Coniferous trees planted — grow faster than deciduous trees. Important economic activity in Ireland.
⚠️ Challenges
  • Tourism can cause pollution from buses and traffic; litter; avalanches on ski slopes.
  • Farming is challenging — difficult to use machinery on steep slopes; poor weather conditions.
  • Forestry can lead to soil erosion, landslides and flooding when trees are cut down.
🃏 Key Terms Flashcards

Click Reveal Answer to check your definition.

What are the four layers of the Earth?
Structure
Crust → Mantle → Outer Core → Inner Core. The crust is solid rock; the mantle contains magma moved by convection currents; the core (iron and nickel) can be divided into outer and inner, with the inner core at ~6,000°C.
Who proposed continental drift and what is Pangaea?
Tectonics
Alfred Wegener (German scientist) proposed the theory. He noticed continents fit together like jigsaw pieces. Pangaea was the single huge landmass that broke apart ~225 million years ago, with continents drifting to their present positions.
Explain the difference between destructive, constructive and passive plate boundaries.
Boundaries
Destructive (convergent): Plates collide → subduction → fold mountains + volcanoes + earthquakes (e.g. Andes). Constructive (divergent): Plates separate → new crust formed → sea-floor spreading, mid-ocean ridges, volcanic islands (e.g. Iceland). Passive (transform): Plates slide past → earthquakes + fault lines (e.g. San Andreas Fault).
What is subduction?
Boundaries
Subduction is the process at destructive boundaries where the heavier (denser) plate is pushed under the lighter plate and melts back into the mantle. The lighter plate is lifted up, forming fold mountains.
Name and describe the three types of volcano.
Volcanoes
Active: erupts regularly (e.g. Mt Etna, Mt St Helens). Dormant: has not erupted for a long time but might again (e.g. Vesuvius). Extinct: will never erupt again (e.g. Croghan Hill, Co. Offaly).
What is a lahar and give an example?
Volcanoes
A lahar is a river of mud formed when volcanic material mixes with water (rivers, rain or melting snow/ice). It can destroy towns and villages. Example: Colombia 1985 — a lahar resulted in the death of 22,000 people.
What is the difference between the focus and epicentre of an earthquake?
Earthquakes
The focus is the point beneath the surface where the earthquake actually happens. The epicentre is the point on the surface directly above the focus — this is where seismic waves are strongest and damage is greatest.
How is earthquake magnitude measured?
Earthquakes
By a seismologist using a seismograph. Two scales are used: the Richter scale (1–10, each unit is 10× stronger than the previous) and the Moment Magnitude scale (more accurate for larger earthquakes over magnitude 8).
How do fold mountains form?
Fold Mountains
When two plates collide at a destructive boundary, the heavier plate sinks (subduction) and the lighter plate buckles upwards — forming fold mountains. The magma can move to the surface forming volcanoes. Example: Andes (Nazca + South American Plates).
Name the three periods of folding and examples from each.
Fold Mountains
Alpine (30–35 million years ago): Alps, Andes, Rockies, Himalayas. Armorican (~250 million years ago): MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Galtees (Ireland); Black Forest (Germany). Caledonian (~400 million years ago): Wicklow Mountains (Ireland); Appalachians (USA); Scottish Highlands.
Give two positive and two negative impacts of fold mountains on people.
Fold Mountains
Positive: Tourism (skiing, hiking, scenic views = jobs); Farming (cattle/sheep, terraced crops). Negative: Tourism causes pollution and avalanches on ski slopes; Forestry clearing causes soil erosion and flooding.
