🪨 Types of Rock

The Earth's crust is made up of rock. Rocks are composed of different minerals — naturally occurring elements usually found in the ground. These minerals are held together by a natural cement. Rocks differ from one another in terms of hardness, texture, colour and mineral content.

All rocks can be divided into three groups depending on how they were formed:

1. Igneous
Igneous Rocks
Formed as a result of volcanic activity. When hot molten magma escapes from the mantle, it cools and hardens. Lava cools quickly on the surface; magma inside the crust cools more slowly.
2. Sedimentary
Sedimentary Rocks
Formed from particles of other rocks, as well as dead plants and animals. Sediments are deposited on sea and lake beds and on land. They are compressed (pressed tightly together) and cemented (stuck together) to create solid rock.
3. Metamorphic
Metamorphic Rocks
Formed when existing igneous or sedimentary rocks are changed into new, harder rocks by great heat or pressure or sometimes both. The heat and pressure come from magma in the mantle or from plate movement.
🔄 The Rock Cycle

Rocks are continuously being formed, broken down and reformed in what is called the rock cycle. The processes of weathering, erosion, transportation, deposition, sedimentation, compression, cementation, burial, melting and volcanic activity all drive the cycle.

🌋 Igneous Rocks

Igneous rocks are formed as a result of volcanic activity. There are two main types: extrusive (formed above the surface — basalt) and intrusive (formed below the surface — granite).

Basalt
🪨 Basalt
ColourGrey / black
TextureFine-grained (tiny crystals, invisible to naked eye)
FormationAn extrusive igneous rock. Lava spreads over the Earth's surface during volcanic activity, then cools and hardens quickly in the open air, producing tiny crystals.
UseBase for roads and pavements; tourist landscape attraction.
Example in IrelandAntrim–Derry Plateau (NE Ireland). The Giant's Causeway — lava contracted as it cooled to form famous hexagonal (six-sided) columns.
Extrusive Rock
Igneous rock formed above the surface of the Earth, when lava cools rapidly in open air. Produces small crystals.
Crystal
A solid form of mineral. The size of crystals in an igneous rock depends on how quickly the magma or lava cooled — slow cooling = large crystals; fast cooling = small crystals.
Granite
🪨 Granite
ColourRange of colours — black, grey, pink and cream
TextureCoarse-grained (large crystals visible to naked eye)
FormationAn intrusive igneous rock. Molten magma pushes into the crust and cools very slowly over millions of years below the surface, producing large crystals. Includes minerals quartz, feldspar and mica. Exposed at surface when overlying rock is worn away.
UseCounter tops in kitchens; everyday products due to hardness and durability. Mount Rushmore (USA) carved from granite.
Example in IrelandThe Wicklow Mountains in the east of Ireland.
Intrusive Rock
Igneous rock formed below the surface of the Earth, when magma cools slowly deep within the crust. Produces large crystals.
⚡
Basalt vs Granite — Key Difference Basalt cools quickly above ground → small crystals, fine-grained.
Granite cools slowly underground → large crystals, coarse-grained.
🏔️ Sedimentary Rocks

Sedimentary rocks are formed from particles of other rocks, as well as dead plants and animals. These sediments are deposited on sea and lake beds. The weight of upper layers compresses and cements the lower layers into strata — layers of solid rock separated by bedding planes.

Sediment
Particles that sink to the seabed or lake bed and build up over millions of years.
Strata
Layers of solid rock within sedimentary rock. Each layer is called a stratum.
Limestone
🪨 Limestone
ColourGrey
TextureCoarse-grained
FormationMade from particles of shells and the remains of sea creatures' skeletons. When these particles sink to the seabed they are called sediment. The sediment piles up in warm, shallow seas over millions of years; upper layers compress and cement lower layers into strata. Ireland's limestone formed ~350 million years ago when Ireland was near the equator.
PropertiesLimestone is permeable (water can pass through it) and soluble (dissolves in water).
UseMonuments, construction of buildings, making cement; ground into lime to improve soil fertility.
Example in IrelandThe Burren, Co. Clare — one of Ireland's biggest tourist attractions.
Permeable
Water can pass through the rock.
Soluble
The rock can dissolve in water.
Sandstone
🪨 Sandstone
ColourReddish / brown
TextureCoarse-grained
FormationFormed from sand worn away from the Earth's surface. Wind and rivers carry this sand and deposit it in seas, lakes and deserts; layers build up over time, are compressed and cemented to form sandstone.
UseUsed in patios and as a building material in the construction industry.
Example in IrelandMacGillycuddy's Reeks in Co. Kerry and the Galtee Mountains in the south of Ireland. Uluru (Ayers Rock) in Australia is a world-famous sandstone landmark.
💎 Metamorphic Rocks

Metamorphic rocks are formed when existing igneous or sedimentary rocks are changed into new, harder rocks by great heat or great pressure (or both). The heat and pressure come from magma in the mantle or from plate movement.

Marble
🪨 Marble
ColourWhite / red / green / black (varies)
TextureSmooth
Original RockLimestone
FormationFormed when limestone is put under great heat or pressure due to plate movement, or when molten magma comes in contact with limestone. It is a hard rock that can vary in colour.
UseCan be cut to any size and polished to make it more appealing. Used for monuments, floor tiles, fireplaces and ornaments.
Examples in IrelandCo. Antrim (white), Co. Cork (red), Connemara, Co. Galway (green), Co. Kilkenny (black).
Quartzite
🪨 Quartzite
ColourWhite / grey
TextureGranular (grainy)
Original RockSandstone
FormationFormed when sandstone is put under great heat or pressure due to plate movement or when molten magma comes in contact with sandstone. An extremely hard rock found on the tops of many of Ireland's mountains because it is not easily broken down.
UseCounter tops, base for roads and pavements, making glass.
Examples in IrelandCroagh Patrick in Co. Mayo and the Great Sugarloaf in Co. Wicklow.
💡
Metamorphic Memory Aid Limestone + heat/pressure → Marble
Sandstone + heat/pressure → Quartzite
⛏️ Uses of Rock

Rocks are a natural resource — something in the natural world that people can use to their advantage. They can be exploited (used for a benefit) in many different ways. Rocks can be used as sources of energy and as building materials. The production of energy and building materials are of great socio-economic importance.

Natural Resource
Something in the natural world that people can use to their advantage.
Exploit
To use something for a benefit.
Energy — Fossil Fuels

Some rocks contain fossil fuels — fuels made from decomposing plants and animals. There are three main types of fossil fuels, each extracted from the crust in different ways.

⛏️
Coal
Mined in open pits (if near the surface) or shaft mining (vertical tunnels underground if deeper). Example: Castlecomer, Co. Kilkenny.
🛢️
Oil and Gas
Extracted by drilling deep into the Earth's crust and pumping to the surface. Can also be done offshore from a rig. Oil or gas is then piped ashore.
Fossil Fuel
Fuels made from decomposing plants and animals found in rock. Include coal, oil and gas.
Mining
The process of extracting fossil fuels or other resources from within the Earth's crust, including shaft mining (underground tunnels).
Building Materials — Quarrying

Quarrying is the process of extracting rock directly from the surface of the Earth. Large machinery cuts rock out of the crust, or explosives break rock away from the crust. Rocks used as building materials include limestone, sandstone and marble.

Quarrying
The process of extracting rock directly from the surface of the Earth using machinery or explosives.
📍 Case Study — Irish Cement: Platin Works, Drogheda, Co. Louth

Irish Cement quarries limestone to produce cement at Platin Works factory, near Drogheda in Co. Louth. Limestone is the main raw material in cement production, which is why the factory is located next to the quarry.

At the quarry, carefully controlled explosions dislodge up to 80,000 tonnes of rock at a time. The rock is transported by truck to the factory, crushed to tennis-ball sized chunks, then blended with raw materials (bauxite, iron ore and shale) and put through an intense heat process in a kiln for burning, baking and drying. The result is milled to form cement.

Platin Works is the largest of Irish Cement's two factories. Together with their Limerick factory, they employ 306 people.

⚖️ Impacts of Mining and Quarrying

Mining and quarrying have economic, social and environmental impacts, and sometimes these can be in conflict with each other. This can make mining or quarrying a controversial activity.

✅ Positive (Economic) Impacts
  • Provides direct and indirect employment (stationery suppliers, cleaners, etc.).
  • Supports local economy and other economic activities.
  • Provides raw materials for copper, steel, iron and glass.
  • Local services such as roads can be improved due to high traffic volume.
  • Reduces need to import building and energy materials.
❌ Negative (Social & Environmental) Impacts
  • Noise pollution from controlled explosions (e.g. Belgard Quarry, Tallaght, weekly blasting).
  • Can be an eyesore — bare rock exposed.
  • Large trucks cause traffic congestion on narrow country roads.
  • Abandoned quarries can form dangerous lakes.
  • Quarries leave scars on the landscape when closed.
  • Dust and air pollution — hazardous to people's health.
  • Wildlife and biodiversity heavily impacted. Damage to natural habitats is hard to fix.
  • Local water supply quality can be affected.
Biodiversity
All the different kinds of plants and animals you will find in a particular area or region.
🃏 Flashcard Revision
What are the three groups of rocks? How does each form?
Types
Igneous — formed from cooled magma/lava. Sedimentary — formed from compressed and cemented sediment (particles of rock, plants and animals). Metamorphic — formed when existing rock is changed by great heat or pressure.
What is the difference between basalt and granite?
Igneous
Basalt is extrusive — it forms above ground when lava cools quickly, giving it fine-grained, tiny crystals (grey/black). Found at the Giant's Causeway, Co. Antrim. Granite is intrusive — forms below ground when magma cools very slowly, giving it coarse-grained, large crystals (mixed colours). Found in the Wicklow Mountains.
What does it mean that limestone is permeable and soluble?
Sedimentary
Permeable = water can pass through the rock. Soluble = the rock dissolves in water. Both properties are important in shaping limestone landscapes like the Burren, Co. Clare.
What are strata? Which rock type has them?
Sedimentary
Strata are layers of solid rock found in sedimentary rocks. They are separated by bedding planes. They form because sediment is deposited in layers over millions of years, with each layer being compressed and cemented.
Name the two metamorphic rocks, their original rock and one use each.
Metamorphic
Marble: from limestone → used for floor tiles, monuments, fireplaces. Quartzite: from sandstone → used for counter tops, road bases, glass-making.
What is quarrying? How is it different from mining?
Uses of Rock
Quarrying extracts rock directly from the surface using machinery or explosives. Mining extracts resources from within the Earth's crust, often using underground shaft mines or open pits.
Name three fossil fuels and how each is extracted.
Energy
Coal — mined in open pits or via shaft mining underground. Oil — extracted by drilling and pumping from the crust (or offshore rigs). Gas — also extracted by drilling and pumping; piped ashore.
Give one positive and one negative environmental impact of quarrying.
Impacts
Positive: Provides employment and raw materials; supports local economy. Negative: Quarries damage biodiversity and natural habitats; can leave scars on the landscape; dust and air pollution is a health hazard; abandoned quarries form dangerous lakes.
Where is basalt found in Ireland, and what famous tourist attraction does it form?
Igneous
Basalt is found in the Antrim–Derry Plateau in the north-east of Ireland. The Giant's Causeway in Co. Antrim is part of this plateau — as lava cooled and contracted (shrank), it formed the famous hexagonal (six-sided) columns.
