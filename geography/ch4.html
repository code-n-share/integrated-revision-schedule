How We Use the World's Natural Resources

💼 Economic Activities & Natural Resources

Economic activities are what people do to earn a living. They can be put into three groups: primary, secondary and tertiary economic activities.

1. Primary
Primary Activities
Jobs that involve working directly with the Earth's resources — rocks, soils and waters.
Jobs: Farmer, Fisher, Forestry worker, Miner
2. Secondary
Secondary Activities
Jobs that involve making or manufacturing something.
Jobs: Carpenter, Factory worker, Baker, Builder
3. Tertiary
Tertiary Activities
Activities that involve providing a service that is useful to people.
Jobs: Teacher, Garda, Solicitor, Shopkeeper
Did you know? In Ireland, less than 5% of the working population is employed in the primary sector. In less economically developed countries, this can be up to 60%. The physical landscape influences the type of primary economic activity in an area.
🌍 Natural Resources

A natural resource is something in the natural world that people can use to their advantage such as land, rivers and seas. There are two types:

♻️ Renewable (Infinite) Resources
Can be used over and over again. If looked after and used sustainably, they will not run out.
Examples: Water, wind, fish, wood, solar energy
⛔ Non-Renewable (Finite) Resources
Can only be used once. Each time one is used, less is left. They cannot be replaced quickly enough and will eventually run out.
Examples: Oil, coal, gas, iron ore, peat
Over-exploitation
Using a resource too much, meaning it might not be available for future generations. This sort of exploitation is unsustainable.
💧 Water & The Water Cycle

Water is very important because people and animals need potable water to survive and plants need water to grow. The Earth has a limited amount of water, which is continuously recycled in the water cycle.

Potable Water
Water that is safe for people and animals to drink.

There are four main stages of the water cycle:

1
Evaporation
The Sun heats up water in rivers, lakes, oceans, trees and plants and turns it into water vapour.
2
Condensation
Water vapour rises, cools and turns back into water droplets, which come together to form a cloud.
3
Precipitation
When so much water has condensed that the air cannot hold it anymore, it falls as rain, hail, dew, sleet or snow.
4
Run-off
Water that falls on land either soaks into the soil (groundwater) or runs off into oceans, lakes or rivers — where the cycle starts again.
Water in Ireland

Uisce Éireann is responsible for the public water supply in Ireland. It is also responsible for Ireland's waste water.

📍 Case Study — Cork City Water Supply

Uisce Éireann is upgrading the water network across Cork City. This development will ensure a sustainable supply of clean drinking water, provide additional capacity for the city's growing population, and reduce leaks.

Key projects include:

  • Western Trunk Watermain: 5.5 km pipe link between the Cork City Water Supply Scheme and the Cork Harbour and City Water Supply Scheme, supplied from the Inniscarra Water Treatment Plant.
  • Eastern Strategic Trunk Watermain: Replacing 2.4 km of pipes between Camden Quay and Shanakiel Reservoir.
  • Shanakiel Watermains Upgrades: Upgrading the supply network on the north side of Cork City, including two new pumping stations.
  • Lee Road Water Treatment Plant: €40 million upgrade; provides over 70% of drinking water to the Cork City area.
Irrigation & Sustainable Water Use

Irrigation is the artificial watering of the land. It is done to help crops grow in areas where there is a shortage of rainfall.

Irrigation
The artificial watering of the land to help crops grow in areas with a shortage of rainfall.
📍 Case Study — Indus Basin Irrigation System (IBIS), Pakistan

The Indus Basin covers over 1 million km² across Afghanistan, China, India and Pakistan. Pakistan's climate is arid or semi-arid, so the country needs irrigation. The IBIS is the world's largest irrigation system, providing water to over 1.6 million km² of agricultural land in Pakistan.

The system uses a network of canals, dams and reservoirs to trap and store water during the rainy season, which is then used during the dry season for growing food.

✅ Advantages of IBIS

  • Dams and reservoirs store water and can create hydroelectric power (e.g. Tarbela and Mangla dams); also control flooding.
  • Forty per cent more land is now available for farming.
  • Improved crop yields → more food and greater food security.
  • The diet of people in the region has improved due to greater crop variety.

❌ Disadvantages of IBIS

  • High temperatures cause large water loss in reservoirs through evaporation.
  • Dams trap sediment, a natural fertiliser — decreasing soil fertility over time.
  • Construction and maintenance costs are very high.
  • Distribution of water can be unfair, with some farmers benefiting more than others.
🐟 Fishing in Ireland

Ireland has many fishing ports because it is located beside the Atlantic Ocean. Ports are located in sheltered, deepwater harbours such as Killybegs in Co. Donegal and Dingle in Co. Kerry. The shallow waters of the continental shelf create ideal conditions for fish. There is also a lot of plankton — microscopic organisms that fish and whales feed on.

Continental Shelf
Area of seabed around a continent, where shallow waters create ideal conditions for fish to live and grow.
Plankton
Small and microscopic organisms that fish and whales feed on. Some types convert the Sun's energy into food energy through photosynthesis.
Overfishing

Overfishing occurs when fish are taken from the water faster than they can reproduce. This is a major problem in Ireland and many other parts of the world, causing a large number of people to lose their jobs.

Reasons for overfishing:

  • Membership of the EEC/EU (1973): Ireland had to share its waters with other EU member countries. Trawlers from other EU countries could fish in Irish fishing areas, leading to more and more fish being caught.
  • Modern technology: Improved sonar and radar equipment makes fish easier to locate and catch. Larger supertrawlers can stay at sea longer and catch more fish. Factory ships process fish at sea, allowing trawlers to stay out even longer.
  • Mesh sizes: Small mesh sizes mean young, underdeveloped fish are caught, reducing the opportunity for fish to breed and replace their stocks.
  • Seasons: No closed season meant fish could be caught all year round, preventing them from breeding.
  • Quotas: The lack of quotas meant no limit on the amount of fish a trawler could catch.
Quota
A limit on the number of fish that can be caught.
Sustainable Exploitation of Fish

The Irish government and the EU promote sustainable exploitation of fish through the Common Fisheries Policy (CFP). Measures introduced include:

  • Creation of conservation zones around Ireland (the Irish Conservation Box — established 2003, extended 2022).
  • Introduction of yearly quotas for fish catches.
  • A ban on fishing some endangered species.
  • Shortening of the fishing season for some types of fish.
  • Increasing net mesh sizes to allow young fish to escape and grow into adulthood.
Conservation Zone
An area where natural resources are to be protected and maintained. The Irish Conservation Box was created in 2003 and altered in 2022 to extend further south.
🌾 Agriculture in Ireland

Agriculture is the practice of farming, which includes the growing of crops and the rearing of animals. It is a very important primary economic activity, providing employment and a food supply. In Ireland, 6.5% of the total workforce are employed in agriculture, and agri-food products are worth more than €18 billion to the Irish economy.

Farming is a system made up of inputs, processes and outputs:

📥
Inputs
Things needed to begin a process. On a farm: cattle, machinery (tractors), seeds, water, fertiliser, labour.
⚙️
Processes
Tasks or jobs done within the system. On a farm: milking, ploughing, cutting silage, harvesting.
📦
Outputs
Products or results of the system. On a farm: milk, vegetables, livestock, grain.
Types of Farming
🐄
Dairy Farming
The keeping of cows for milk, butter, cheese, etc.
🌾
Arable Farming
The growing of crops (maize, wheat, barley, etc.). Also known as tillage farming.
🥬
Horticulture
The growing of vegetables, flowers, fruits, salad, etc. Also known as market gardening.
🐑
Pastoral Farming
The grazing of sheep or cattle.
🐷
Livestock
Keeping cattle, sheep, pigs, horses, etc.
🔀
Mixed Farming
A mixture of keeping livestock and growing crops and vegetables.
🗺️
Agricultural Regions of Ireland The type of agriculture in an area depends on the type of soil and the landscape. Sheep suit hilly and mountainous western land. Livestock and arable farming suit the central lowlands. Fringe land is not suitable for intensive farming.
Impact of Farming on the Environment
✅ Benefits
  • Provides employment and income for rural communities.
  • Produces food supply for the country.
  • Agri-food products worth over €18 billion to the Irish economy.
❌ Environmental Impacts
  • Insect sprays on crops can be harmful and end up in our food.
  • Fertilisers run off into streams and rivers, causing algae overgrowth, blocking sunlight and consuming oxygen (kills fish).
  • Livestock produce methane as part of digestion — a harmful greenhouse gas.
  • Illegal dumping of silage/slurry can pollute rivers and kill fish.
Sustainable Agriculture — The CAP

As an EU member, Irish agriculture is strongly influenced by the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). The main aims of the CAP are:

  1. Provide income support to farmers.
  2. Ensure guaranteed market prices for farmers in the EU.
  3. Support rural development programmes.
  4. Provide Europe with a good supply of quality food.

The latest CAP 2023–27 reforms align with the European Green Deal (EU to become climate neutral by 2050). Measures include:

  1. Diversifying crops — main crop cannot cover more than 75% of the land.
  2. Farmers must keep permanent grassland to protect plants and animals.
  3. Water sources on farmland must be fenced off to prevent cattle drinking from them.
  4. Farmers must protect certain species such as the barn owl.
  5. Artificial fertilisers (nitrates) and livestock manure (slurry) spreading is restricted.
  6. Emphasis on education — farmers must attend courses and farm visits to receive payments.
🌲 Forestry in Ireland

The forestry industry in Ireland employs 9,500 people directly and indirectly, making it a very important primary economic activity. Wood is a natural resource that can be renewable if managed sustainably.

Ireland has one of the lowest forest covers in Europe — 11.6% in 2023, compared to EU averages of 30%. However, the amount of forest area has been increasing significantly in recent decades.

The natural vegetation of Ireland is deciduous forest. During the English colonisation of Ireland, coniferous trees (spruce, pine) were introduced as they grow faster than deciduous trees.

Deciduous Trees
Trees that lose their leaves in winter. Examples: beech, birch, oak, ash.
Coniferous Trees
Evergreen trees that don't lose their leaves in winter. Examples: spruce, pine. They grow faster than deciduous trees.

Impact of Deforestation on the Global Climate

Deforestation is the clearing of all trees from an area by burning or cutting. Cleared land is then used in other ways. Trees absorb carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere and release oxygen (O₂). Carbon dioxide is a greenhouse gas that causes climate change. The reduction of forested areas worldwide has been a contributing factor to climate change.

Deforestation
The clearing of all trees from an area by burning or cutting. A major contributing factor to climate change as it reduces the amount of CO₂ being absorbed.
Reforestation in Ireland

Reforestation is the planting of trees on areas of land that were once covered by forest but were affected by deforestation.

The Forestry Division of the Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine is responsible for forestry development in Ireland. The semi-state body Coillte owns around half of the forests in Ireland.

  • Farmers and landowners can apply for grants from the Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine.
  • The Forestry Programme covers 100% of the costs to plant the forest.
  • All income from the forest is tax free.
  • Forestry is especially suited to upland areas with poor soil where it is difficult to use heavy farming equipment.
Reforestation
The planting of trees on areas of land that were once covered by forest but were affected by deforestation.

Resources
Renewable (infinite) resources can be used over and over again (e.g. water, wind, fish). Non-renewable (finite) resources can only be used once; they will eventually run out (e.g. oil, coal, gas).
Name and describe the four stages of the water cycle.
Water Cycle
1. Evaporation — Sun heats water into water vapour. 2. Condensation — vapour rises, cools, forms clouds. 3. Precipitation — water falls as rain, hail, snow, etc. 4. Run-off — water flows back into oceans/lakes/rivers or soaks into the ground.
What is overfishing? Give three reasons it has occurred in Ireland.
Fishing
Overfishing occurs when fish are taken from the water faster than they can reproduce. Reasons: (1) EU membership — other countries could fish in Irish waters. (2) Modern technology — sonar, supertrawlers, factory ships. (3) Small mesh sizes catching young fish. (4) No closed season. (5) Lack of quotas.
Name three measures introduced to deal with overfishing in Ireland.
Fishing
Any three from: (1) Irish Conservation Box (conservation zone). (2) Yearly quotas for fish catches. (3) Ban on fishing some endangered species. (4) Shorter fishing seasons. (5) Larger net mesh sizes to allow young fish to escape.
What is agriculture? Name the inputs, processes and outputs of a farm.
Agriculture
Agriculture is the practice of farming, including the growing of crops and the rearing of animals. Inputs: cattle, tractors, seeds. Processes: milking, ploughing, cutting silage. Outputs: milk, vegetables, livestock.
Name two ways farming can negatively impact the environment.
Agriculture
Any two from: (1) Insect sprays can end up in food. (2) Fertiliser run-off causes algae overgrowth in rivers, killing fish. (3) Livestock produce methane — a greenhouse gas. (4) Illegal dumping of silage/slurry can pollute rivers.
What is deforestation? How does it affect the global climate?
Forestry
Deforestation is the clearing of all trees from an area by burning or cutting. Trees absorb CO₂ and release O₂. Cutting them down means more CO₂ stays in the atmosphere. CO₂ is a greenhouse gas that causes climate change. Deforestation in the Amazon rainforest in Brazil is a major global example.
What is reforestation? How does Ireland encourage it?
Forestry
Reforestation is the planting of trees on areas once covered by forest. Ireland encourages it by: (1) grants covering 100% of planting costs. (2) Income from forests is tax free. (3) Coillte (semi-state body) manages about half of Ireland's forests. Coniferous trees are planted in upland areas with poor soil.
Name one advantage and one disadvantage of the Indus Basin Irrigation System (IBIS).
Irrigation
Advantage: 40% more land available for farming; improved food security and crop yields. Disadvantage: Dams trap sediment, reducing soil fertility; high construction and maintenance costs; unequal distribution of water.
