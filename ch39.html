📖 Key Terms at a Glance
Sustainable resource
One that meets current energy needs and will remain available for future generations.
Non-renewable
Energy sources that will eventually run out (e.g. coal, oil, natural gas).
Renewable
Energy sources that will not run out in our lifetime (e.g. solar, wind, hydro).
Solar
Energy from sunlight — captured by photovoltaic panels or thermal collectors.
Wind
Energy from moving air — turns turbine blades to generate electricity.
Biomass
Organic material (plants, crop waste) burned to run turbines or engines.
Geothermal
Heat energy from the Earth's core used to heat buildings or power turbines.
Hydroelectricity
Electricity generated by water flowing over a dam to power turbines.
Nuclear fission
Splitting nuclei of radioactive atoms (e.g. uranium) with neutrons to release huge amounts of heat energy.
Nuclear fusion
Joining nuclei of atoms together to release energy — the same process stars use.

How a Turbine Generates Electricity
When a magnet moves near a coil of wire, an electrical current flows. Turbine blades spin coiled copper wire inside a magnetic field, generating electrical energy. This is the principle behind almost all electrical generation today.
💡 Discovered by Michael Faraday (1791–1867) in 1831 — his simple principle still powers most of the world's electricity.

Eureka! – Michael Faraday (1791–1867)
Michael Faraday developed the world's first electricity generator in 1831. He discovered that when a magnet moves near a coil of wire, electrical current flows. This simple method has been the foundation of most of our electrical generation ever since.
🌍 Energy Needs

Our energy needs have grown as society has developed. The world's population is rising fast — it has increased from 2.5 billion people in 1950 to over 8 billion people today. In fact, the world has used as much energy in the past 70 years as the 11,700 years before added together. As countries continue to modernise and as technology and transport use increases, the huge demand for energy will grow.

Sustainable Resource
A sustainable resource is one that can be used to meet our current energy needs and will remain available to meet the energy needs of future generations.
💡 Our reliance on energy means it is very important that the sources it comes from are sustainable. Fossil fuels are NOT sustainable — they will eventually run out.
⚡ Sources of Energy

The sources of energy used to produce electricity are either non-renewable or renewable.

🪨 Non-Renewable Energy Sources Will Run Out

Coal, oil and natural gas are non-renewable energy sources, meaning they will eventually run out.

These sources of electricity are called fossil fuels because they form from the decayed remains of animals and plants over millions of years. When fossil fuels are burned, the heat released is used to generate electricity.

The majority of power plants that generate our electrical energy continue to rely on coal and natural gas. This is a big issue facing society, as it is predicted that all fossil fuels will be used up in the next 50 to 150 years, based on current use. Another major disadvantage of fossil fuels is that they release carbon dioxide (CO₂) into the atmosphere when burned, which contributes to global warming.

🪨 Fossil Fuels (Coal, Oil, Natural Gas)
Description Advantages Disadvantages
Formed from the decayed remains of animals and plants over millions of years. When burned, heat generates electricity via turbines.
  • Cheap and widely available
  • Large amounts of energy released
  • Reliable — not weather-dependent
  • Will run out in 50–150 years
  • Release CO₂ → global warming
  • Cause air pollution
  • Not sustainable
🌱 Renewable Energy Sources Will Not Run Out

These sources of energy are called renewable because they can keep being produced and will not run out in our lifetime. The advantages and disadvantages of each renewable source are shown below.

☀️ Solar Energy
Description Advantages Disadvantages
  • Photovoltaic solar panels generate electricity when sunlight strikes them.
  • Thermal solar energy uses sunlight to heat water, which can heat a building or make steam in a turbine generator.
  • Produces energy every day
  • Emits no CO₂
  • Low running costs once installed
  • High installation costs
  • Efficiency depends on the amount of sunshine
  • Cannot generate at night
🌬️ Wind Energy
Description Advantages Disadvantages
Wind turbines, inland or offshore, use high winds to turn rotor blades and convert kinetic energy to electrical energy.
  • Wind is plentiful and cost-free
  • Emits no CO₂
  • Can be offshore or onshore
  • Efficiency depends on the amount of wind
  • Turbines are considered an eyesore by some
  • Noise pollution
🌿 Biomass
Description Advantages Disadvantages
Organic matter (material that is or was living), such as plants and crop waste, is burned to run turbines or purified into a fuel used in engines.
  • Organic matter is plentiful and cheap
  • More efficient than fossil fuels
  • Can use waste material
  • Emits CO₂
  • Can lead to the destruction of habitats when land is cleared
🌋 Geothermal Energy
Description Advantages Disadvantages
Heat from the Earth's core heats water in underground pipes, which can heat a building or power a turbine generator.
  • Very low greenhouse gas emissions
  • Available all year round
  • Reliable — not weather-dependent
  • Harmful gases may be released during installation
  • High installation costs
  • Only available in certain locations
💧 Hydroelectric Energy
Description Advantages Disadvantages
Large volumes of water are stored in a reservoir, then released to flow over a dam and power a turbine generator.
  • Water is plentiful and cheap
  • Emits no CO₂
  • Reliable output
  • Risk of flooding
  • High installation costs
  • Disrupts local ecosystems

☢️ Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is an energy source that falls between non-renewable and renewable. This is because the radioactive material used for nuclear power comes from millions of years of decay in the Earth's crust. However, we need such a small quantity to produce massive amounts of energy that we will never use it up. There are two methods of producing nuclear energy:

  • Nuclear fission
  • Nuclear fusion
Nuclear Fission
Nuclear Fission
Nuclear fission is the most commonly used type of nuclear power. In nuclear fission, the nuclei of atoms of radioactive material, e.g. uranium, are split by neutrons. A huge amount of heat energy is released, which can be used to power turbine generators.
💡 "Fission" = splitting apart. A uranium atom is hit by a neutron → it splits → releases huge heat energy → boils water → steam → turbine → electricity.

Under normal circumstances, nuclear fission does not release harmful gases into the atmosphere. However, there is a low risk that an accident at a nuclear power plant can result in radioactive leaks. This can have devastating effects on the population and agriculture around the power plant and can spread into drinking water or rain for many decades. Even with the few accidents that have unfortunately occurred, nuclear energy is still a safer method of power generation than fossil fuels.

Nuclear Fusion
Nuclear Fusion
In nuclear fusion, the nuclei of atoms are joined together to release energy — the same process stars use to generate energy. It is clean and safe, as it produces no greenhouse gas emissions or radioactive by-products. This method of generating electricity is in the early stages of research, but it has the potential to be the most efficient and sustainable way of meeting our future energy needs.
💡 "Fusion" = joining together. The Sun fuses hydrogen atoms → releases massive energy. 1 kg of hydrogen isotopes could produce as much energy as burning 10 million kg of fossil fuel!
🎯
CONCENTRATE! Fission vs Fusion Fission = splitting atoms apart (used in power stations today). Fusion = joining atoms together (used by stars; still in research stage on Earth). Both release huge amounts of energy, but fusion is cleaner and produces no radioactive waste.
Feature Nuclear Fission Nuclear Fusion
What happens? Nuclei are split by neutrons Nuclei are joined together
Fuel used Uranium (radioactive) Hydrogen isotopes
Greenhouse gases? No (normally) No
Radioactive waste? Yes — low risk of leak No
Currently used? Yes — in power stations No — in early research stage
Energy output Very large Enormously large (potential)
⚛️
Eureka! – National Ignition Facility (December 2022)
In December 2022, scientists at the National Ignition Facility in California successfully produced a nuclear fusion reaction when they released more energy than was used in an experiment. Although only a small amount of energy was generated, this breakthrough finally proved that it is possible for scientists to control fusion. If used efficiently, the method has the potential to produce as much energy from 1 kg of hydrogen isotopes as would be produced from burning 10 million kg of fossil fuel.

✏️ Summary Questions
Q1 — Complete the sentences (turbine generator)
Copy and complete the sentences: "__________ is burned to release heat. Water becomes __________, which generates __________ energy and causes the blades of a turbine to turn. The turbine blades rotate coiled wire near __________, which generates __________ energy."
Fuel is burned to release heat. Water becomes steam, which generates kinetic energy and causes the blades of a turbine to turn. The turbine blades rotate coiled wire near magnets, which generates electrical energy.
💡 The energy chain: chemical (fuel) → heat → kinetic (steam/turbine) → electrical
Q2 — Non-renewable vs Renewable
Explain the difference between non-renewable and renewable energy sources.
Non-renewable energy sources (e.g. coal, oil, natural gas) will eventually run out. They formed over millions of years and cannot be replaced quickly. Renewable energy sources (e.g. solar, wind, hydroelectric) can keep being produced and will not run out in our lifetime. They come from naturally replenishing sources.

Q7 — Coal advantages & environmental issues
Coal is still a major source of global energy production.
a) What are the advantages of using coal to generate electricity?
b) Describe two environmental issues caused by using coal to generate electricity.
a) Advantages of coal: — Cheap and widely available — Produces large amounts of energy — Reliable — not dependent on weather b) Environmental issues: 1. Burning coal releases CO₂ into the atmosphere, contributing to global warming/climate change. 2. Burning coal also releases other pollutants (e.g. sulfur dioxide) causing air pollution and acid rain.
Q8 — Hydroelectric environmental problems
What environmental problems may be caused by constructing a hydroelectric power station?
1. Risk of flooding — large areas of land are flooded to create the reservoir, destroying habitats and displacing communities. 2. Disruption of local ecosystems — dams block the natural flow of rivers, affecting fish migration and aquatic life.

Q10 — Nuclear energy: one advantage & one disadvantage
Give one advantage and one disadvantage of nuclear energy.
Advantage: Nuclear energy does not release CO₂ or greenhouse gases during normal operation, making it a low-carbon source of electricity. It also produces enormous amounts of energy from a small amount of fuel. Disadvantage: There is a low risk of radioactive leaks from accidents at nuclear power plants, which can have devastating and long-lasting effects on the environment, population, agriculture and water supply.
