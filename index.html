⚡ What is Energy?
Energy
Energy is the ability to do work. The SI unit of energy is the joule (J).
💡 Think of energy as "stored ability" — a stretched elastic band, a held ball, a charged battery all have stored energy.
Calculating Work (Energy Used)

To find the energy needed to move or lift something:

Formula
work = force × distance
🎯
CONCENTRATE! Work is calculated the same way whether something is moved along the ground or lifted upward. Always multiply force (N) × distance (m) to get joules (J).
📘 Example 1
Calculate the energy required to move a 20 N box 5 metres.
work = force × distance work = 20 N × 5 m work = 100 J
Simple explanation: Force is 20 N (like the weight of the push needed) and distance is 5 m. Multiply both → 100 joules of energy needed.
📘 Example 2
Calculate the energy required to lift a 250 N person 0.5 metres up in the air.
work = force × distance work = 250 N × 0.5 m work = 125 J
Simple explanation: The person weighs 250 N. Even though lifted only 0.5 m, we still use the same formula — 125 J of energy is needed.
💡
Eureka – James Joule (1818–1889)
James Joule made major discoveries in electricity, electromagnetism, and heat. The unit of energy — the joule (J) — is named in his honour.
🔋 Forms of Energy

All energy is either potential, kinetic, or both — depending on the object and what it is doing.

⬆️
Potential Energy stored
Energy stored by an object due to its position or shape. If an object is lifted or pulled out of shape, its potential energy increases.
Examples: a raised ball, a stretched bow, water at top of a waterfall.
🏃
Kinetic Energy motion
Energy of an object due to its motion. Greater speed = more kinetic energy. Greater mass = more kinetic energy.
Examples: a moving car, a falling stone, a flowing river.
Other Forms of Energy
Form Definition (from textbook) Simple Example
☀️
Light		 The main source of energy for our planet is light from the Sun. Allows plants to grow and produce food during photosynthesis; can power solar cells. Sunlight, torch, solar panel
🔥
Chemical		 Present in food, batteries, fuel, explosives and plants during photosynthesis. When food is eaten or fuel burned, chemical energy changes form. Eating food, burning petrol, battery
🌡️
Heat		 Stored in objects due to changing temperature. As objects heat up, particles vibrate faster. Can be used to power turbines using steam. Hot stove, steam engine, fire
🔊
Sound		 Travels by vibrating atoms in a material. Atoms are pushed out of place and spring back after the sound passes. Speaker, thunder, clapping
⚡
Electrical		 From moving electrons (charged particles). Can be converted into many other forms depending on the device (heaters, speakers, TVs, ovens). Wall socket, lightning, battery power
☢️
Nuclear		 Stored in the nucleus. When nucleus is split or joined with another, massive energy is released as heat and light. Used in power stations and weapons. Nuclear power plant, atomic bomb
♻️ Conservation of Energy
Principle of Conservation of Energy
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, but only converted from one form to another.
💡 When energy seems to "disappear" (e.g., a ball stops bouncing), it has been converted — mostly to heat. The total always stays the same.

📊 Sankey Diagrams
Sankey Diagram
A Sankey diagram shows how the energy that flows into a device is used and dissipated.
💡 Think of it like a river of energy — the main stream goes in, some goes out usefully (straight), some is wasted (curves downward). Wider arrow = more energy.

Rules for reading a Sankey diagram:

  • ➡️ Straight arrow going in = energy flowing into the device
  • ➡️ Straight arrow going out = energy converted usefully
  • ⬇️ Curved arrow pointing downward = energy dissipated (wasted)
  • The thickness of each arrow shows how much energy it represents
Sankey Diagram: Filament Bulb vs CFL
Filament bulb 10% efficient
Mostly wasted as heat
CFL (compact fluorescent) 75% efficient
Most converts to useful light
Useful light energy
Wasted heat energy
Electrical energy in (100 J)
Arrow width ∝ energy  ·  1 px = 1 J

Both bulbs receive 100 J of electrical energy. The filament bulb converts only 10 J to light — wasting 90 J as heat. The CFL converts 75 J to light — only 25 J is wasted. Notice how the light arrow is 7.5× wider in the CFL diagram.

💡
Eureka – H. Riall Sankey (1853–1926)
Irish-born engineer H. Riall Sankey developed Sankey diagrams in 1898 to show how energy was being used or wasted in mechanical systems. His first diagram represented energy conversions in a steam engine.
📐 Energy Efficiency & Dissipation
Efficiency
The efficiency of a device is how well it uses or converts the energy that flows into it.
💡 A 90% efficient device only wastes 10%. A 10% efficient device wastes 90%. Higher % = better device.
Dissipation
When energy is wasted and does not convert to a useful form, it is called dissipation.
💡 A TV dissipates heat from its wires and screen — that heat isn't helping you watch TV!
Efficiency Formula
Percentage Energy Efficiency
% efficiency = (useful energy out ÷ energy in) × 100
Percentage Energy Dissipation
% dissipation = 100 − % efficiency
📘 Example – Mobile Phone Charger
A charger uses 200 J; 180 J charges the phone, 20 J emitted as heat. Find:
a) energy efficiency    b) energy dissipation
a) % efficiency = (180 ÷ 200) × 100 = 90% b) % dissipation = 100 − 90 = 10%
Simple: 180 out of 200 J are useful → 90% efficient. The remaining 10% is lost as heat in the plug.
✏️ Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Work & Energy
Calculate the energy required for a person of weight 200 N to walk 4 metres.
work = force × distance work = 200 N × 4 m work = 800 J
✅ Answer: 800 J
💡 Force = weight = 200 N. Distance = 4 m. Just multiply!
Problem 2 — Finding Weight from Energy
A lift carries 10 people 20 m upwards. 70,000 J of energy is required. How much does each person weigh?
work = force × distance 70,000 = force × 20 force (total) = 70,000 ÷ 20 = 3,500 N Weight per person = 3,500 ÷ 10 = 350 N
✅ Answer: Each person weighs 350 N
💡 Rearrange: force = work ÷ distance. Then divide by 10 people. Always check units — force is in Newtons (N).
Problem 3 — Efficiency Calculation
A toaster uses 2200 J. 150 J escapes as heat.
a) What is the energy efficiency?    b) What is the energy dissipation?
a) Useful energy = 2200 − 150 = 2050 J % efficiency = (2050 ÷ 2200) × 100 % efficiency ≈ 93.2% b) % dissipation = 100 − 93.2 = 6.8%
✅ Efficiency ≈ 93.2%  |  Dissipation ≈ 6.8%
💡 "Useful energy out" = total in − wasted. Then use the formula. The toaster is quite efficient — most energy heats the bread!
Problem 4 — Efficiency of Electric Bike
An electric bike uses 3000 J of energy but only converts 2500 J into kinetic energy. Calculate the percentage energy efficiency.
% efficiency = (useful energy out ÷ energy in) × 100 % efficiency = (2500 ÷ 3000) × 100 % efficiency = 83.3%
✅ Answer: 83.3% efficient
💡 2500 out of 3000 joules go to useful kinetic energy. The rest (500 J) is wasted — likely as heat in the motor.
Problem 5 — Reading a Sankey Diagram
A food mixer has 1500 J of electrical energy flowing in. The Sankey diagram shows 1400 J of useful energy out, 25 J as heat loss, and 75 J as sound.
a) What is the useful energy form?
b) What are the dissipated energy forms?
c) Calculate the percentage energy dissipated.
a) Useful = kinetic (mechanical) energy → 1400 J b) Dissipated = heat (25 J) + sound (75 J) = 100 J wasted c) % dissipation = (100 ÷ 1500) × 100 % dissipation = 6.67%
✅ Useful: kinetic | Wasted: heat + sound | Dissipation: 6.67%
💡 Add up ALL wasted forms for total dissipation. Then divide by energy in × 100.
Problem 6 — Kettle Efficiency (Past Paper style)
A kettle has 3000 J supplied but only 2000 J converts to heating the water.
a) How many joules are dissipated?
b) Calculate the percentage efficiency of the kettle.
a) Dissipated = 3000 − 2000 = 1000 J b) % efficiency = (2000 ÷ 3000) × 100 % efficiency = 66.7%
✅ Dissipated: 1000 J  |  Efficiency: 66.7%
💡 "How many joules wasted?" = energy in − useful energy out. Then use the efficiency formula for the % answer.
