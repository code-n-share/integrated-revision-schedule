📐 Physical Quantities & SI Units

We rely on measurements every day — clocks for time, rulers for length, thermometers for temperature. A physical quantity is anything that can be measured with a measuring instrument.

Accuracy
Making sure that any measurement taken is as close as possible to its correct value. To measure accurately you must choose the correct instrument and use the correct units.
💡 Think of accuracy as hitting the bullseye — correct instrument + correct units = accurate reading.
Precision
When a measurement is taken more than once, it should be the same or close in value each time. This is called precision.
💡 Precision = repeatability. If you measure the same object five times and get almost the same answer each time, your measurements are precise.
SI Units (International System of Units)
A system of units used by scientists worldwide to make sure measurements are carried out the same way and can be understood by everyone. Each unit is represented by a symbol.
💡 SI units are like a universal language for science — everyone uses the same units so results can be shared and compared.

⏱️ Measuring Time

In science, the standard unit of time is the second (s). Instruments used to measure time include stopwatches and timers. Electronic timers connected to circuits can measure time automatically — accurate to hundredths of a second.

💡
Eureka! — Ancient Timekeeping Since the era of the Ancient Egyptians, time has been divided into 12 parts (12 hours of day and night). Some believe this was due to the 12 joints in the fingers — four fingers with three joints each, excluding the thumb.
📘 Figure It Out — Time Conversions
Convert the following into seconds: a) 1 day = 24 × 60 × 60 = 86,400 s b) 1 week = 7 × 86,400 = 604,800 s c) 1 year = 365 × 86,400 = 31,536,000 s Travel time: 3 hours 24 minutes from Donegal to Dublin → in minutes: (3 × 60) + 24 = 204 minutes → in seconds: 204 × 60 = 12,240 s
Simple: 1 min = 60 s · 1 hour = 3,600 s · 1 day = 86,400 s. Multiply up step by step.

Instruments for Measuring Length
📏
Ruler
Measures straight lines up to 30 cm.
💡 Best for small, straight objects like pencils or books.
🪄
Metre Stick
Measures straight lines up to 1 m.
💡 Good for desks, doors, or anything up to 1 metre long.
🎡
Trundle Wheel
Measures long, curved lengths — e.g. a running track. Circumference of 1 m; clicks every metre.
💡 Each click = 1 metre. Count the clicks!
🗺️
Opisometer
Measures short, curved lengths — e.g. roads or rivers on a map. Roll the wheel along the line, then along a ruler.
💡 Looks like a mini trundle wheel with a pointer.
🔧
Digital Caliper
Measures thickness and circular objects — e.g. diameter of pipes or coins. Jaws close around the object.
💡 Used for very precise measurements of small, round objects.
📘 Figure It Out — Length Conversions
Convert into metres: a) 130 cm = 130 ÷ 100 = 1.3 m b) 540 mm = 540 ÷ 1000 = 0.54 m Convert into centimetres: a) 2.3 m = 2.3 × 100 = 230 cm b) 15 mm = 15 ÷ 10 = 1.5 cm Convert into millimetres: a) 30 m = 30 × 1000 = 30,000 mm b) 86 cm = 86 × 10 = 860 mm
Rule: Going to a smaller unit → multiply. Going to a larger unit → divide.
⬛ Measuring Area
Area
Area is the amount of space a two-dimensional shape takes up. The standard unit of area is the metre squared (m²). For smaller areas, cm² and mm² are used; for larger areas, km² is used.
💡 Area always has a squared unit — because you're measuring in two directions (length × width).
Area of a Rectangle
Area = length × width
🎯
CONCENTRATE! The most important part of measuring area is to make sure the correct unit of length is used. If you want the area in metres squared, you should measure the length in metres before calculating the area.
📘 Worked Example — Regular Rectangle
Calculate the area, in m², of a rectangle with sides measuring 0.25 m and 2 m.
Area = length × width Area = 0.25 m × 2 m Area = 0.5 m²
✅ Answer: 0.5 m²
Simple: Multiply length by width. Because both are in metres, the answer is in m².
Area of an Irregular Shape

To find the area of an irregular shape, break it into regular shapes (rectangles, squares, triangles) and calculate the area of each, then add them together.

📘 Worked Example — Irregular Shape
A shape 12 cm wide and 15 cm tall is divided into three rectangles: A (12×6), B (6×6), C (12×3).
A: 12 cm × 6 cm = 72 cm² B: 6 cm × 6 cm = 36 cm² C: 12 cm × 3 cm = 36 cm² Total area = 72 + 36 + 36 = 144 cm²
✅ Answer: 144 cm²
Simple: Split the shape into rectangles you can handle. Add the areas at the end.
📘 Figure It Out — Area Calculations
1. Square with one side 1.2 m: Area = 1.2 × 1.2 = 1.44 m² 2. Rectangle: 30 cm long, 20 cm high: Area = 30 × 20 = 600 cm² 3. Rectangle: 240 cm × 35 cm: Area = 240 × 35 = 8,400 cm²
Remember: For a square, both sides are equal — just square the side length!
🧊 Measuring Volume
Volume
Volume is the amount of space an object takes up. The standard unit of volume is metres cubed (m³). When the volume is smaller, cm³ is used.
💡 Volume is 3D — you measure in three directions (length × width × height).
Volume of a Regular Solid (Cuboid)
Volume of a Cuboid
Volume = length × width × height
📘 Worked Example — Regular Cuboid
Find the volume in m³ of a regular cuboid with dimensions 3 m by 0.75 m by 1 m.
Volume = length × width × height Volume = 3 m × 0.75 m × 1 m Volume = 2.25 m³
✅ Answer: 2.25 m³
Simple: Multiply all three dimensions together. Three metres × metres = m³.
Volume of a Liquid — Graduated Cylinder
Graduated Cylinder
The piece of laboratory equipment used to measure the volume of a liquid. It has graduated lines and measurements along the side.
💡 Always read the bottom of the meniscus at eye level for an accurate reading.
Meniscus
The name of the curve on the top surface of a liquid in a graduated cylinder. Always read the bottom of the meniscus at eye level to ensure an accurate reading.
💡 Water curves downward — read the lowest point of the curve, not the top edges.
Volume of an Irregular Solid — Displacement Method
📘 Experimental Investigation 25.4 — Displacement
How can we measure the volume of an irregular solid? (e.g. a stone)
Equipment: Overflow can, graduated cylinder, water, string, stone Method: 1. Place overflow can on a block above the graduated cylinder. 2. Pour water into overflow can until it flows from spout. 3. Record the volume reading in the graduated cylinder (V₁). 4. Tie string to stone. Lower stone into overflow can until submerged. 5. Water flows out into the graduated cylinder — record new volume (V₂). 6. Volume of stone = V₂ − V₁
✅ The volume of water displaced = the volume of the stone.
Simple: The stone pushes out exactly its own volume of water. Measure that water → you have the stone's volume.
📘 Figure It Out — Volume Calculations
1. Cuboid: 23 cm × 14 cm × 75 cm Volume = 23 × 14 × 75 = 24,150 cm³ 2. Cube: sides = 30 cm Volume = 30 × 30 × 30 = 27,000 cm³ 3. Graduated cylinder before stone: 40 cm³ Graduated cylinder after stone: 55 cm³ Volume of stone = 55 − 40 = 15 cm³
Remember: For a cube, all sides are equal — cube the side length (side³).
⚖️ Measuring Mass
Mass
Mass is the amount of matter in an object. The standard unit of mass is the kilogram (kg). When the mass is smaller, grams (g) are used (1 kg = 1000 g).
💡 Mass stays the same whether you're on Earth or the Moon. Weight changes — mass does not.
Instruments for Measuring Mass
A mass balance (digital or beam) and a spring balance are both used to measure mass.
💡 For very heavy objects (e.g. 100 steel ball bearings), use a spring balance. For very light objects (e.g. a feather), use a sensitive digital balance.
📘 Figure It Out — Mass Conversions
Convert into kilograms (kg): a) 300 g = 300 ÷ 1000 = 0.3 kg b) 25 g = 25 ÷ 1000 = 0.025 kg Convert into grams (g): a) 2.5 kg = 2.5 × 1000 = 2,500 g b) 150 kg = 150 × 1000 = 150,000 g
Rule: kg → g: multiply by 1000. g → kg: divide by 1000.
🌡️ Measuring Temperature
Temperature
Temperature is the measure of the hotness or coldness of an object or body. The practical unit of temperature is degrees Celsius (°C). A thermometer is the instrument used to measure temperature.
💡 Common types: alcohol-in-glass thermometer and digital thermometer.
💡
Eureka! — Handheld Digital Thermometers Handheld digital thermometers were used to check for fever during the COVID-19 pandemic. While they allow temperature to be measured at a safe distance of one metre, they measure external body temperature only and are less accurate than other types of thermometers.
⚠️ Error in Measurement
Error
In measurement, an error is a mistake that is introduced into a reading or an investigation.
💡 Errors are NOT always the student's fault — they can come from the instrument, the environment, or carelessness.
🤦 Blunder
A mistake caused by carelessness.
e.g. Spilling a liquid before measuring it. Not following the investigation method properly.
⚙️ Systematic Error
Due to a problem with the measuring instrument or environment.
e.g. A metre stick worn down at the end. A mass balance not set to zero. Investigation near a radiator.
🎲 Random Error
A mistake made by the person taking the measurement.
e.g. Reading a scale incorrectly. The table is unsteady and equipment moves.
Ethics and Measurement

Errors in measurement can cause major problems for consumers and the economy. Every country has a government department that regularly checks commercial instruments for error — such as petrol station pumps and shop weighing scales. In Ireland these checks are carried out by the National Metrology Laboratory.

✅ See above — learn these four instrument/unit pairs for every exam.
💡 This type of question appears in almost every past paper. The four instruments above are the most commonly tested.
Problem 2 — Area Calculation
A hockey pitch has sides of 91.4 m and 55 m.
a) What instrument would you use to measure this accurately?
b) What is the perimeter (all exterior sides added together)?
c) Calculate the area of the pitch.
a) A trundle wheel — it measures long distances accurately. b) Perimeter = 2 × (91.4 + 55) = 2 × 146.4 = 292.8 m c) Area = 91.4 × 55 = 5,027 m²
✅ a) Trundle wheel | b) 292.8 m | c) 5,027 m²
💡 Perimeter = add all sides. Area = length × width. A trundle wheel is the right tool for a large outdoor space.
Problem 3 — Volume of Irregular Solid
A stone is placed into an overflow can. Before the stone, the graduated cylinder reads 15 cm³. After the stone, it reads 50 cm³.
a) What is the volume of the stone?
b) Why must you read the bottom of the meniscus?
c) Name all equipment used.
a) Volume of stone = 50 − 15 = 35 cm³ b) Water curves upward at the edges of the cylinder (the meniscus). Reading the bottom of the curve gives the true volume. Reading the top of the curve would give a higher, incorrect value. c) Equipment: overflow can, block, graduated cylinder, water, string, stone
✅ a) 35 cm³ | b) Meniscus curves — bottom is the true level | c) See above
💡 Volume of irregular solid = V₂ − V₁. The stone pushes out exactly its own volume of water.
Problem 4 — Errors in Measurement (Final Exam 2022, Q11)
A tradesperson is measuring a wall before painting it. Give an example of:
a) a random error that could occur
b) a systematic error that could occur
a) Random error: The tradesperson misreads the ruler (reads 2.3 m instead of 2.4 m). This is a mistake by the person taking the measurement. b) Systematic error: The measuring tape is old and slightly stretched, so every measurement is slightly too long. This is a problem with the measuring instrument.
✅ a) Misreading the scale (random) | b) Faulty/stretched measuring tape (systematic)
💡 Random = person's mistake. Systematic = instrument/environment problem. Blunder = careless mistake (e.g. dropping the tape).
Problem 5 — Petrol Pump Error Calculation
A petrol pump is incorrectly set and delivers the wrong volume of fuel. It displays 1 litre when only 0.95 litres have been delivered.
a) How much does the customer actually receive when 20 litres are registered?
b) How much fuel does the customer lose per year if they use this pump to put 20 litres in their car every week?
a) Actual fuel per "litre" = 0.95 L Actual fuel for 20 registered litres = 20 × 0.95 = 19 litres b) Loss per fill = 20 − 19 = 1 litre Fills per year = 52 weeks Total loss = 52 × 1 = 52 litres per year
✅ a) 19 litres actually received | b) 52 litres lost per year
💡 This is a systematic error — the instrument (pump) is consistently wrong by the same amount each time.
Problem 6 — Shadow Length Investigation (Final Exam 2022, Q11)
Two groups investigated how the length of a shadow varies over a year. Group A used a pole to cast the shadow. Group B used a student from the group instead of a pole.
a) Name an instrument used to measure the length of the shadow.
b) State one variable Group A should have kept constant.
c) Which group, A or B, carried out a better investigation? Justify your answer.
a) A metre stick or trundle wheel (ruler for shorter shadows). b) Group A should have kept the height of the pole constant (always use the same pole in the same upright position). c) Group A carried out the better investigation. A pole is a fixed, unchanging object — its height stays the same every measurement. A student's height, posture, or standing position could vary, introducing random error.
✅ a) Metre stick / ruler | b) Height of the pole | c) Group A — pole height is constant; a student introduces random error
💡 Good investigations keep all variables constant except the one being tested (controlled variable). A student is not a reliable constant!
