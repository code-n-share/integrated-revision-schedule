🔄 Physical and Chemical Change

Matter can undergo physical and chemical changes. The key question to ask is: has a new substance been formed?

🧊
Physical Change
Matter changes shape or state but no new substance is formed. Changing states, such as melting or boiling, are all examples of physical changes.
💡 The substance stays the same — just in a different form. Usually easy to reverse.
🦀
Chemical Change
A new substance is formed. All chemical reactions are examples of a chemical change. Usually not easy to reverse.
💡 A completely different material is produced. Think rusting, burning, cooking — you can't un-rust a nail!
Comparison Table
Physical Changes Chemical Changes
No new substance is formed A new substance is formed
Easy to reverse Not easy to reverse
Example: Ice melting to water, which can be reversed by freezing. Example: An iron nail rusting to form a new substance called iron oxide.
🎯
Exam Tip – The Golden Rule Always ask two questions: (1) Has a new substance been formed? (2) Is the change reversible? If a new substance formed → chemical change. If the substance just changed shape or state → physical change.
📖 Key Terms
Physical Change
A change where matter changes shape or state but no new substance is formed.
Chemical Change
A change where a new substance is formed. All chemical reactions are examples.
Reactants
The chemicals that react together during a chemical reaction (the starting materials).
Products
The new substance or substances that are formed during a chemical reaction.
Effervescence
Bubbles of gas being produced in a liquid during a chemical reaction.
Law of Conservation of Mass
Matter cannot be created or destroyed — it can only change from one form to another.
⚗️ Chemical Changes

Chemicals often come together to form new substances — for example, when a fuel burns in a fire, when a nail rusts, and when an egg is boiled. These processes are called chemical reactions.

Reactants → Products
The chemicals that react together during a chemical reaction are called reactants. The new substance or substances that are formed are called products.
💡 Reactants go IN; Products come OUT.

🔍 Signs of a Chemical Reaction

There are a number of signs that a chemical reaction has taken place. You might observe:

  • 🎨 A change in colour — e.g., iron turning orange-brown as it rusts
  • 🌡️ A change in temperature — the reaction releases or absorbs heat
  • 💡 The production of light — e.g., a burning candle or fireworks
  • 🔊 The emission of sound — e.g., a bang from fireworks exploding
  • 👃 A distinctive smell — e.g., milk souring produces a sour odour
  • 🫧 Effervescence — bubbles of gas being produced in a liquid (e.g., vinegar + baking soda)
📝
Remember for the Exam Signs are divided into two categories: (a) those caused by a new product being formed (colour change, effervescence), and (b) those caused by energy changes (light, heat, sound). You may be asked to identify which category a sign belongs to.
⚖️ The Law of Conservation of Mass
Law of Conservation of Mass
Matter cannot be created or destroyed but can change from one form to another. In both physical and chemical changes, the total mass of matter stays the same.
💡 Mass before a reaction = Mass after a reaction. Always!
💡
Eureka! — Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794)

French chemist Antoine Lavoisier discovered the law of conservation of mass. When he heated different substances in air, he found that the total mass of the chemicals and all the apparatus at the beginning of the investigation was the same as the total mass at the end, even though a chemical change had occurred.

📘 Example — Opening a Fizzy Drink (Test Tube 4)
When you open a can of a fizzy drink, does the mass of the drink increase, decrease or stay the same? Is there a chemical or a physical change taking place?
When the can is opened, CO₂ gas escapes into the air. The mass of the can + drink DECREASES slightly. However, if measured in a sealed system, mass is conserved. This is a PHYSICAL change — the CO₂ was already dissolved; no new substance is formed when it is released.
Simple: The drink loses mass because gas escapes, but the total mass of gas + drink stays the same. The law of conservation of mass always holds in a closed system.
📘 Example — Steel Wool Burning
In the investigation, steel wool was burned using a Bunsen burner flame. Explain why the mass of the steel wool increases.
Steel wool reacts with oxygen in the air: Iron + Oxygen → Iron oxide (rust) The steel wool gains mass because it is combining with oxygen from the air to form iron oxide. The oxygen atoms are added to the iron atoms.
✅ Mass increases because oxygen from the air becomes part of the new product (iron oxide).
Simple: The iron atoms grab oxygen atoms from the air. Those oxygen atoms have mass, so the total mass of the steel wool goes up. Conservation of mass still holds — the mass gained by the wool = mass of oxygen taken from the air.
📘 Example — Burning Paper
If paper is burned, the mass of the paper before burning is greater than the mass of the ashes. Where does the missing mass go?
Paper + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water vapour + Ash The carbon dioxide and water vapour escape as gases into the air. The remaining ash has much less mass than the original paper.
✅ The "missing" mass has escaped as gases (CO₂ and water vapour) into the atmosphere.
Simple: Mass is NOT destroyed — it goes into the air as invisible gases. In a fully sealed system, the total mass before and after burning would be identical.
🔬 Experimental Investigations
🧪 Investigation 15.1 — Are these changes physical or chemical?

Equipment: Plastic droppers, water, teaspoon, salt, test tube, thermometer, vinegar, baking soda, clock glass, candle, tongs, crackers, Bunsen burner, retort stand & clamp, boiling tube, graduated cylinder, steel wool, wire gauze.

⚠️ Safety Tie back hair. Ensure workspace is clear of flammable materials. Make sure equipment has cooled before touching. Conduct in a fume cupboard as smoke will be produced in final steps.
Method (summary):
  1. Add drops of water to 1 teaspoon of salt — observe & record temperature change.
  2. Add drops of vinegar to baking soda on a clock glass — observe & record temperature change.
  3. Light a candle — observe what happens.
  4. Using tongs, heat a cracker in a Bunsen burner flame — observe & record.
  5. Add 20 ml of water to a boiling tube. Use a thermometer to record starting temperature. Light a cracker and hold under the boiling tube. Record final temperature.
  6. Hold a piece of steel wool with tongs. Heat in Bunsen burner flame. Once alight, hold over wire gauze. Record observations.
⚖️ Investigation 15.2 — How can we investigate the law of conservation of mass?

Equipment: Ice cubes, sealable plastic bags (1 medium, 1 large), mass balance, graduated cylinder, baking soda, vinegar, tissue paper.

⚠️ Safety Clear up any spills immediately.
Part A – Physical Change (Ice melting):
  1. Place some ice cubes in a sealable plastic bag.
  2. Measure the mass using a mass balance.
  3. Set the bag aside until the ice cubes have melted.
  4. Predict whether total mass will increase, decrease or stay the same.
  5. Measure the mass of the bag once all ice has melted.
  6. Is this a physical or chemical change? Explain your results.
Part B – Chemical Change (Vinegar + Baking Soda):
  1. Measure 20 cm³ of vinegar into a large sealable plastic bag.
  2. Measure the mass using a mass balance.
  3. Measure out 5 g of baking soda using a mass balance. Wrap in a small sheet of tissue paper.
  4. Measure the mass of the tissue paper + baking soda.
  5. Calculate and record the total mass of everything.
  6. Drop the tissue paper into the bag of vinegar and seal quickly.
  7. Once the reaction has stopped, find the mass of the bag.

Problem 1 — Classify Physical or Chemical?
Classify each of the following as either physical or chemical changes. Has a new substance been made? Is the reaction reversible?

a) Boiling water in a kettle   b) Burning wood   c) Fireworks exploding   d) Ice cream melting   e) A snowman melting   f) Baking a cake   g) An iron nail rusting
a) Boiling water → PHYSICAL (water becomes steam; still water; reversible) b) Burning wood → CHEMICAL (new substances: CO₂, ash, water; irreversible) c) Fireworks exploding → CHEMICAL (new substances formed; light, sound, heat; irreversible) d) Ice cream melting → PHYSICAL (still ice cream ingredients; reversible by refreezing) e) A snowman melting → PHYSICAL (water → liquid water; reversible) f) Baking a cake → CHEMICAL (new substances formed; colour change; smell; irreversible) g) An iron nail rusting → CHEMICAL (iron oxide formed; colour change; irreversible)
✅ Physical: a, d, e  |  Chemical: b, c, f, g
💡 The key test: has a new substance formed? If yes → chemical. Is it reversible? Physical changes usually are; chemical changes usually aren't.
Problem 2 — Signs of Chemical Reactions
What signs would indicate that a chemical change has taken place in the following examples?
a) A match burning   b) A firework exploding   c) Milk souring   d) A coin rusting
a) Match burning: → Production of light, change in temperature (heat), change in colour (black ash), distinctive smell b) Firework exploding: → Production of light, emission of sound, change in colour, change in temperature c) Milk souring: → Distinctive smell, change in texture/appearance d) Coin rusting: → Change in colour (green/brown patina forms)
✅ Key signs: colour change, temperature change, light, sound, smell, effervescence
💡 Remember to distinguish between signs caused by new products forming (colour, gas) vs signs caused by energy changes (heat, light, sound).
Problem 3 — Conservation of Mass (Sealed System)
A 2 g sample of solid iodine is placed in a tube and the tube is sealed after all the air is removed. The tube and solid iodine together weigh 30 g. The iodine will sublime (change state from solid to gas) in the tube on heating. Will the mass of the tube increase, decrease or stay the same? Explain your answer.
The tube is SEALED — no matter can enter or leave. The iodine changes from solid to gas (sublimation). This is a PHYSICAL change — no new substance is formed. By the law of conservation of mass: mass before = mass after = 30 g
✅ The mass will STAY THE SAME at 30 g.
💡 Because the tube is sealed, the iodine gas cannot escape. Even though the state changes from solid to gas, the total mass inside the sealed tube remains 30 g. Conservation of mass always holds in a closed system.
Problem 4 — Ice Cubes Floating in Water
Three ice cubes are floating in water. Mass of the beaker + ice cubes + water = 58 g. After the ice melts, will the mass of the beaker: a) be greater than 58 g, b) be less than 58 g, or c) stay the same? Explain your choice.
Ice melting is a PHYSICAL change. No new substance is formed — ice (solid water) → liquid water. By the law of conservation of mass: Total mass before = Total mass after = 58 g (nothing leaves or enters the beaker)
✅ Answer: c) Stay the same — 58 g
💡 The ice is still water — just in a different state. The total number of water molecules hasn't changed. Mass is conserved in physical changes.
Problem 5 — Methane Combustion (Past Paper Style)
Natural gas contains methane (CH₄). Methane burns in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O.
a) Mass is conserved during this reaction. What evidence is there for this?
b) The burning of methane is an example of a chemical change. Describe one difference between a physical change and a chemical change.
a) The number and type of atoms on the left side of the equation (reactants) is the same as on the right side (products). In the molecular diagram, the same atoms (C, H, O) appear in both reactants and products — none are created or destroyed. b) In a physical change, no new substance is formed (the matter only changes shape or state). In a chemical change, a new substance IS formed.
✅ a) Same atoms before and after; balanced equation shows conservation.
✅ b) Physical change = no new substance; Chemical change = new substance formed.
💡 For part (a), refer to the molecular diagram or balanced equation. For part (b), always use the phrase "new substance is formed" — it's the key distinction examiners look for.
Problem 6 — Sucrose Melting (Final Exam 2022)
A student investigated the melting point of sucrose (table sugar) by placing it on top of zinc and aluminium metal cubes on a hotplate. Is melting an example of a physical change or a chemical change? Explain your answer.
Melting is a PHYSICAL change. When sucrose melts: • It changes state from solid to liquid. • No new substance is formed — it is still sucrose. • The change is reversible (liquid sucrose can solidify again). ∴ Melting = Physical change.
✅ Physical change — sucrose only changes state, no new substance is formed, and it is reversible.
💡 Even though heat causes the change, what matters is whether a NEW substance forms. Melted sucrose is still sucrose — just liquid. No new substance = physical change.
📝 Past Paper Questions
📄 Final Examination 2019 – Question 4

Natural gas contains methane (CH₄). Methane is a fuel. Methane burns in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water.

The reaction: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

  1. Mass is conserved during this reaction. What evidence is there for this?
    Answer: The molecular diagram shows the same atoms present in both the reactants and the products — no atoms are created or destroyed. The equation is balanced: the number of C, H, and O atoms is the same on both sides.
  2. The burning of methane is an example of a chemical change. Describe one difference between a physical change and a chemical change.
    Answer: In a chemical change, a new substance is formed (CO₂ and H₂O are produced). In a physical change, no new substance is formed — matter only changes shape or state.
📄 Final Examination 2022 – Question 13

A student carried out experiments to investigate the properties of sucrose (table sugar). In the first experiment, the student investigated the melting point of sucrose.

Two metal cubes of equal volume (one zinc, one aluminium) were placed on a hotplate. One gram of sucrose was placed on top of each cube and the hotplate was turned on.

Is melting an example of a physical change or a chemical change? Explain your answer.

Answer: Melting is a physical change. When sucrose melts, it changes state from solid to liquid, but no new substance is formed. The sucrose is still sucrose in liquid form. The change is also reversible — if cooled, the liquid sucrose would solidify again. Therefore, melting is a physical change.
