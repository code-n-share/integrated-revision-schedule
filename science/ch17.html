🔗 Compounds

We know that elements are substances that have only one type of atom. When two or more elements are chemically combined, the substance they make is called a compound. The elements in a compound are chemically joined together by strong forces called bonds.

Compound
A compound is formed when two or more elements are chemically combined. The elements are joined together by bonds. Compounds are all around us.
💡 A compound is NOT just a mix — the elements are chemically bonded together into a completely new substance with new properties.
💧
Water (H₂O)
A compound made up of two elements bonded together: hydrogen and oxygen. Water is a liquid at room temperature.
💡 H₂O — 2 hydrogen atoms bonded to 1 oxygen atom.
🍬
Sugar (C₆H₁₂O₆)
A compound made up of three elements bonded together: carbon, hydrogen and oxygen.
💡 Glucose — a solid at room temperature with a sweet taste.
🧂
Sodium Chloride (NaCl)
Table salt — a compound of sodium and chlorine. A white crystal used to flavour food. Very different properties from its elements.
💡 Sodium is explosive; chlorine is poisonous — but together they make harmless table salt!

⚛️ Molecules
Molecule
A molecule is a group of two or more atoms bonded together. A molecule is the smallest part of an element or compound that has the chemical properties of that element or compound.
💡 Molecules can be elements (like O₂) or compounds (like H₂O and CO₂).
Oxygen molecule
O₂ (element)
Water molecule
H₂O (compound)
Carbon dioxide molecule
CO₂ (compound)

Oxygen is an example of an element made up of molecules — it is made up of two oxygen atoms bonded together. Water and carbon dioxide are examples of compounds made up of molecules, as they contain two different types of atoms.

🌊 Mixtures
Mixture
A mixture is made up of two or more elements or compounds that are physically combined. For example, seawater is a mixture of water and salt. The substances in a mixture can be separated easily from each other without a chemical reaction taking place.
💡 In a mixture, substances are NOT chemically joined — they are just mixed together and can be separated again.
Compound vs Mixture — Key Differences
🔗 Compound
  • Two or more elements chemically combined
  • Joined by bonds — not easy to separate
  • Has new and different properties from its elements
  • Has a fixed composition (always the same ratio)
  • Can only be separated by a chemical reaction
  • Example: Water (H₂O), Salt (NaCl)
🌀 Mixture
  • Two or more elements/compounds physically combined
  • NOT chemically joined — easy to separate
  • Substances keep their own properties
  • Variable composition (any ratio)
  • Separated by physical methods (filtration, etc.)
  • Example: Seawater, air, sand & water
🎯
Exam Tip – Compound vs Mixture The key difference: in a compound, a NEW substance with NEW properties is formed (chemical bonds). In a mixture, each substance keeps its own properties and no new substance is formed. Salt (NaCl) is a compound; seawater (salt dissolved in water) is a mixture.
🔬 Separating Mixtures

One of the characteristics of mixtures is that they can often be easily separated. Separation can be done in the laboratory in a number of ways. The choice of technique depends on the nature of the mixture.

🫙
Filtration
Separates a solid that does NOT dissolve in liquid from a liquid.
💡 Example: Sand and water
🎨
Chromatography
Separates dissolved substances carried at different rates by a liquid.
💡 Example: Ink in a marker
♨️
Evaporation
Separates a solid that DOES dissolve in liquid — the liquid evaporates, leaving the solid.
💡 Example: Salt and water
🌡️
Distillation
Separates a dissolved solid AND keeps the liquid, or separates two miscible liquids.
💡 Example: Salt water; water and alcohol
🫙 Filtration
🫙 Filtration
Filtration is used to separate a solid that does NOT dissolve in liquid from a liquid.
Filtration involves using a material (e.g. filter paper) that allows the liquid but not the solid to pass through. The liquid that passes through is called the filtrate. The solid left behind on the filter paper is called the residue.
📌 Example: Separating sand (solid) from water (liquid) using filter paper.
Filtration Apparatus
🔬 Experimental Investigation 17.1 — Which filter is best?

Equipment: Mass balance, sand, graduated cylinder, water, 2 beakers, filters to be tested (coffee filters, filter paper, cloth), retort stand and clamp, filter funnel.

⚠️ Safety: Clear up any spills immediately.
Method:
  1. Use the mass balance to measure out 10 g of sand.
  2. Use a graduated cylinder to measure out 30 cm³ of water.
  3. Mix the sand with the water in a beaker.
  4. Use the mass balance to get the mass of the filter being tested.
  5. Set up the apparatus for filtration.
  6. Pour the sand and water mix through the filter.
  7. Once the filtrate has passed through, remove the filter and allow it to dry.
  8. Get the mass of the dry filter paper and the sand.
  9. Observe and record your results.
  10. Follow steps 1–9 with different filters.
🎨 Chromatography
🎨 Chromatography
Chromatography is used to separate mixtures of dissolved substances.
The different substances are carried up a piece of chromatography paper by liquid at different rates. Substances that are more soluble travel further up the paper. Substances that are less soluble travel a shorter distance.
📌 Example: Separating the different coloured dyes in ink from a marker pen.
Paper Chromatography
🎨 Experimental Investigation 17.2 — What colours make up black ink?

Equipment: Scissors, chromatography paper, different brands of black markers, pencil, crocodile clip, beaker, water.

⚠️ Safety: Clear up any spills immediately.
Method:
  1. Cut the chromatography paper into strips.
  2. Draw a circle on the chromatography paper with the marker being tested.
  3. Place the chromatography paper in the beaker with a small amount of water.
  4. Allow the water to make its way up the chromatography paper.
  5. Record the colours (in order) that the ink separated into.
  6. Repeat steps 1–5 with different brands of black markers.
♨️ Evaporation
♨️ Evaporation
Evaporation is used to separate a solid that DOES dissolve in liquid from a liquid.
The liquid is evaporated (heated until it turns to gas and escapes), and the solid is left behind in the evaporation dish. Use with a Bunsen burner, tripod stand, wire gauze, and evaporation dish.
📌 Example: Separating salt from salt water — the water evaporates, leaving dry salt crystals behind.
🌡️ Distillation
🌡️ Distillation
Distillation is used to separate a solid that does dissolve in liquid from a liquid when you want to KEEP the liquid, or to separate two miscible liquids (liquids that mix).
Distillation separates miscible liquids based on their boiling points. The liquid with the lower boiling point will evaporate first. The vapour passes through a Liebig condenser where it cools and condenses back into a liquid (the distillate) in the receiving flask.
📌 Examples: Salt and water (keep the pure water); water and alcohol (separate based on boiling points).
🎯
Evaporation vs Distillation Both separate a dissolved solid from a liquid — but the key difference is what you want to KEEP. Use evaporation when you want the solid (e.g. getting salt from seawater). Use distillation when you want the liquid (e.g. getting pure drinking water from seawater).

⚗️ Experimental Investigation 17.3 — How can rock salt be separated into its parts?

Rock salt is a mixture of salt and rock.

Equipment: Mortar and pestle, rock salt, beaker, water, glass stirring rod, filter funnel, filter paper, hotplate, evaporation dish.

⚠️ Safety: The hotplate can reach high temperatures — be careful when handling to avoid burns. Allow equipment to cool before touching it.
Method:
  1. Crush the rock salt using the mortar and pestle.
  2. Place the crushed rock salt in a beaker containing 60 cm³ of water. Stir the mixture. The salt will dissolve in the water.
  3. Set up the apparatus for filtration.
  4. Slowly pour the contents of the beaker through the filter funnel.
  5. The sand remains on the filter paper. This is the residue. Allow the paper and sand to dry at room temperature.
  6. Set up the apparatus for evaporation and evaporate the filtrate (salt and water).
  7. A white solid remains in the evaporation dish. This is the salt.
📖 Key Terms
Compound
A substance formed when two or more elements are chemically combined by bonds.
Molecule
A group of two or more atoms bonded together. The smallest part of an element or compound with its chemical properties.
Mixture
Two or more elements or compounds physically combined. Can be separated without a chemical reaction.
Filtration
Separation technique for a solid that does NOT dissolve in a liquid. Uses filter paper.
Chromatography
Separation technique for dissolved substances. Different substances travel at different rates up paper.
Evaporation
Separation technique where a liquid is heated away, leaving the dissolved solid behind.
Distillation
Separation technique that uses boiling points to separate liquids; the vapour is collected and condensed.

Problem 1 — Classify: Element, Compound or Mixture?
Classify each of the following substances as an element, a compound or a mixture:

magnesium · water · chlorine · air · saltwater · sulfur · hydrogen · iron oxide · diet cola
Elements (one type of atom only): → Magnesium, Chlorine, Sulfur, Hydrogen Compounds (chemically bonded elements, new substance): → Water (H₂O), Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) Mixtures (physically combined, can be separated): → Air (N₂, O₂, CO₂, Ar...), Saltwater, Diet cola
✅ Elements: Mg, Cl, S, H  |  Compounds: Water, Iron oxide  |  Mixtures: Air, Saltwater, Diet cola
💡 Elements = one type of atom. Compounds = chemically bonded (new substance formed). Mixtures = physically combined (no new substance, can be separated).
Problem 2 — Outline the Difference Between a Compound and a Mixture
Outline the difference between a compound and a mixture. Use table salt (NaCl) as an example in your answer.
A compound is formed when two or more elements are CHEMICALLY combined (joined by bonds). A new substance with new properties is formed. It cannot be separated by physical means. A mixture is formed when two or more substances are PHYSICALLY combined. No new substance is formed. Each substance keeps its own properties. It CAN be separated by physical methods. Example: • NaCl (table salt) is a COMPOUND — sodium and chlorine are chemically bonded together. Sodium is reactive and dangerous; chlorine is poisonous — but together they make harmless table salt with completely different properties. • Seawater (NaCl dissolved in H₂O) is a MIXTURE — the salt and water are physically combined. They can be separated by evaporation.
✅ Compound = chemically bonded, new substance, new properties. Mixture = physically combined, no new substance, separable.
💡 The "sodium chloride" story is the perfect exam example: two dangerous elements become a harmless compound. That's the power of chemical bonding.
Problem 3 — Choosing Separation Techniques
Suggest a separation technique suitable for the following tasks, and name the equipment needed:

a) To obtain salt from seawater.
b) To get pure drinking water from seawater.
c) To separate sand and seawater.
d) To determine the dyes used in the coating of sweets.
a) Obtain salt from seawater: → EVAPORATION Equipment: Evaporation dish, Bunsen burner, tripod, wire gauze. Heat until water evaporates, salt remains. b) Get pure drinking water from seawater: → DISTILLATION Equipment: Distilling flask, Liebig condenser, thermometer, receiving flask. Water vapour collected and condensed back to pure liquid water. c) Separate sand and seawater: → FILTRATION Equipment: Filter funnel, filter paper, beaker, retort stand and clamp. Sand stays on filter paper. d) Determine dyes in sweet coating: → CHROMATOGRAPHY Equipment: Chromatography paper, beaker, water (or solvent). Different dyes travel different distances up the paper.
✅ a) Evaporation  |  b) Distillation  |  c) Filtration  |  d) Chromatography
💡 Key rule: Does the solid dissolve? Yes → evaporation or distillation. No → filtration. Need to separate dissolved colours? → chromatography. Want to keep the liquid? → distillation not evaporation.
Problem 4 — Identifying Models (Element, Compound or Mixture)
For each model description below, state whether it represents an element, compound, or mixture, and explain your answer:

Model 1: Contains only identical atoms of one type (all same colour).
Model 2: Contains two different types of atoms always paired together in a fixed ratio.
Model 3: Contains three different types of atoms scattered randomly in varying amounts.
Model 4: Contains two types of atoms mixed but NOT bonded together.
Model 1: All identical atoms, one type → ELEMENT (e.g. a box of all red atoms = pure element like oxygen O₂) Model 2: Two atom types in fixed ratio, always bonded → COMPOUND (e.g. H₂O — always 2H to 1O, chemically bonded) Model 3: Three atom types scattered randomly → MIXTURE (e.g. air — N₂, O₂, CO₂ in varying proportions, physically combined, not bonded) Model 4: Two atom types mixed but not bonded → MIXTURE (e.g. saltwater — Na⁺, Cl⁻ and H₂O mixed together)
✅ 1 = Element  |  2 = Compound  |  3 = Mixture  |  4 = Mixture
💡 In particle diagrams: same atoms = element. Different atoms always bonded in fixed ratios = compound. Different atoms mixed randomly = mixture.
Problem 5 — Rock Salt Separation Method
Rock salt is salt found in the ground. It contains particles of salt mixed with sand and other rocks. Develop a method for how you might obtain a pure sample of dry salt from the rock salt.
Step 1: CRUSH the rock salt using a mortar and pestle. Step 2: DISSOLVE in water — add to beaker of water and stir. Salt dissolves; sand/rock does NOT. Step 3: FILTER the mixture: → Pour through filter funnel with filter paper. → Sand/rock (residue) stays on filter paper. → Salt water (filtrate) passes through. Step 4: EVAPORATE the filtrate: → Pour filtrate into evaporation dish. → Heat gently with Bunsen burner. → Water evaporates; pure dry salt remains.
✅ Crush → Dissolve in water → Filter (remove sand) → Evaporate (remove water) → Pure dry salt
💡 This is a classic two-step separation: filtration first (to remove insoluble sand), then evaporation (to remove the soluble water). The order matters!
