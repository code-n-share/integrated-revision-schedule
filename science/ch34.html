🌌 Celestial Objects
Celestial Object
Any natural body beyond the Earth's atmosphere is known as a celestial object. Celestial objects include moons, asteroids, comets, stars and the other planets in our solar system.
💡 Basically anything in space that occurs naturally — from tiny asteroids to giant stars.
Orbit
The path that a celestial object follows around another celestial object is its orbit. For example, the Earth orbits the Sun.
💡 Think of orbit like a racetrack — objects follow a curved path around a central object due to gravity.
Space
Space is the empty region between celestial objects.
💡 Space isn't completely empty (tiny particles exist) but it's considered a vacuum compared to Earth's atmosphere.

🌕 Moons
Moon
A moon is a celestial object that orbits a planet. The Earth's moon is simply called the Moon. The moons of other planets have been given names — e.g. Titan is a moon of Saturn.
💡 Moons orbit planets; planets orbit stars. Think: moon → planet → star.

The number of moons a planet can have varies greatly. Planet Earth has only one moon, but Jupiter and Saturn have more than 80 moons each.

Moons vary in composition (the materials they are made up of). Earth's moon is composed of oxygen, silicon, magnesium, iron and other elements. Titan is composed of ice and rock. The atmosphere of different moons also varies — Earth's moon has no atmosphere, but Titan has an atmosphere of nitrogen and methane.

☄️ Asteroids
Asteroid
An asteroid is a small lump of rock that orbits the Sun. Asteroids range in size from less than 1 km to 1000 km in diameter. They are thought to have been left over from the formation of the solar system.
💡 Think of asteroids as leftover rocky rubble from when the solar system was being built billions of years ago.

The asteroid belt lies between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter. It contains a large number of asteroids that vary in shape and size. Some well-known asteroids include Apophis, Ceres, Chiron, Eros and Vesta.

📍
Location: The asteroid belt sits between Mars and Jupiter — remember this by thinking of the belt as a boundary between the inner rocky planets and the outer gas giants.
💫 Comets
Comet
A comet is a small lump of ice and dust that orbits the Sun.
💡 Key difference from an asteroid: asteroids are rock, comets are ice and dust.

A comet has three main parts:

⚪
Nucleus (Centre)
The nucleus of a comet is made up of dust and ice.
The solid core — the "dirty snowball" part of the comet.
🌫️
Coma (Fuzzy Layer)
The coma is the fuzzy layer around the nucleus. It is caused by heat from the Sun and is made up of carbon dioxide, dust and water vapour.
Forms as the comet nears the Sun and ice begins to evaporate.
✨
Tail
The tail is made up of gas and dust produced by the nucleus. The tail is blown away from the Sun by solar winds, so it always points away from the Sun.
Always points away from the Sun — regardless of which direction the comet is travelling!

Comets can only be seen when they travel near the Sun, as they reflect the Sun's light. When a comet moves towards the Sun, it is blown away from it by solar winds, causing its tail to travel behind it. When a comet moves away from the Sun, its tail travels in front of it.

Comets originate in the Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune, or in the Oort cloud at the edge of our solar system.

💡
Eureka! — Halley's Comet

Halley's Comet is one of the most famous comets in the solar system. It is named after English astronomer Edmond Halley (1656–1742), who first discovered that the comet returns to Earth every 75 years. It can be seen with the naked eye and is due to return in 2061.

On 12 November 2014, as part of the European Space Agency's Rosetta mission, the Philae lander became the first spacecraft to land on a comet. Philae was able to record images of the comet's surface and take measurements that had never before been possible.

Craters
Comets, asteroids and other large space rocks can crash onto the surface of moons and planets. On impact, these objects can cause craters that vary in size, shape and depth. Some of the Moon's craters can be seen from Earth.
💡 The dark circles on the Moon's surface are craters — formed by high-speed asteroid and comet impacts over billions of years.
⭐ Stars
Star
A star is a celestial object that emits light. This is why they can be seen clearly in the night sky. The Sun is a star.
💡 Stars emit (produce) their own light. Planets and moons only reflect light from a star.

Stars are mainly composed of hydrogen and contain small amounts of helium and other elements. They are held together by gravity.

Nuclear reactions are the source of a star's energy. The gases that make up the star burn and release energy in the form of heat and light.

👩‍🔬
Eureka! — Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin

British astronomer Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin (1900–1979) was the first scientist to identify the composition of stars. Her 1925 research concluded that stars, including the Sun, were mostly made up of the gases hydrogen and helium. As this discovery went against scientific beliefs held at the time, it was denied by other astronomers. It turns out she was, in fact, correct.

🪐 Planets
Planet
A planet is a celestial object that:
  • orbits a star
  • has enough mass to have a nearly round shape
  • has enough gravity to clear the space debris (waste) in its orbit
💡 Pluto lost its planet status because it hasn't cleared its orbital neighbourhood — it's now a "dwarf planet".

Many planets are visible from Earth, as they can reflect the light from the Sun.

☀️ The Solar System
Solar System
The solar system is made up of a star (the Sun) and the celestial objects that orbit it. The Sun is orbited by eight planets, their moons and smaller objects such as asteroids and comets. The solar system is held together by gravity.

The solar system contains four inner planets and four outer planets.

🪨
Terrestrial Planets (Inner)
The four inner planets are called terrestrial planets because they are made up of rock, like the Earth. They are: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars.
"Terrestrial" comes from Latin for Earth/land — rocky, solid surfaces.
🌬️
Gas Giants (Outer)
The four outer planets are called gas giants because they are mainly made of gases such as hydrogen and helium. They are: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
No solid surface to stand on — they're enormous balls of swirling gas.

🌌 Galaxies
Galaxy
A galaxy is a system of billions of stars. Galaxies differ in size based on the number of stars they contain.
💡 Our solar system is a tiny part of a galaxy, which is itself a tiny part of the universe.

Galaxies also differ in shape. Some galaxies have a spiral shape and others have an elliptical (oval) shape.

Planet Earth and our solar system are part of a galaxy called the Milky Way. Our solar system is in one of the spiral arms of this galaxy.

Astronomers can see billions of other galaxies in the universe using powerful telescopes, and they predict that there are many more galaxies yet to be discovered.

🌌
The Milky Way

The Milky Way is a spiral galaxy containing an estimated 100–400 billion stars. Our Sun is located about two-thirds of the way out from the centre, in one of the spiral arms. The Milky Way is approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter.

Match each celestial object to its correct description:

💡 Key difference to remember: Asteroids = rock. Comets = ice and dust. Stars emit light; planets reflect it.

Problem 3 — Halley's Comet Age Calculation
Halley's Comet is visible from Earth approximately every 75 years. It was last visible in 1986. Calculate what age you will be the next time Halley's Comet is visible with the naked eye from Earth. (Assume you were born in 2012.)
Last visible: 1986 Period: every 75 years Next visible: 1986 + 75 = 2061 Age in 2061 = 2061 − 2012 = 49 years old
✅ Halley's Comet next visible in 2061. Age = 49 (adjust for your own birth year).
💡 Formula: Next visibility = last year + 75. Then subtract your birth year from 2061.
Problem 4 — Inner vs Outer Planets
State one similarity and one difference between the inner and outer planets in our solar system.
Similarity: Both inner and outer planets orbit the Sun. Difference: • Inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are terrestrial planets made of rock. • Outer planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) are gas giants made mainly of hydrogen and helium. (Also acceptable: outer planets are much larger / have more moons / are further from the Sun)
✅ Similarity: both orbit the Sun. Difference: inner = rocky; outer = gas giants.
💡 Remember: the asteroid belt acts as the boundary between terrestrial planets and gas giants.

Problem 7 — Past Paper: Final Exam 2022, Q6
An object weighs less on the Moon than on Earth. Tick (✓) the box next to the sentence that explains why:

□ It is colder on the Moon than on Earth.
□ The Moon has a smaller radius than Earth.
□ The Moon has a smaller mass than Earth.
□ The Moon has no atmosphere.
✅ The Moon has a smaller mass than Earth. Explanation: Weight depends on gravity. Gravity depends on mass. The Moon has much less mass than Earth, so its gravitational pull is weaker. Objects therefore weigh less on the Moon (about 1/6 of their weight on Earth).
✅ The Moon has a smaller mass than Earth.
💡 Weight = mass × gravity. Less mass → less gravity → less weight. Temperature and atmosphere do NOT affect weight/gravity.
