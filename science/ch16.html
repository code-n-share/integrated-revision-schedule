🔬 Atoms
Atom
An atom is a tiny particle. Atoms are the basic building blocks of all matter.
💡 Everything you can touch, see or feel is made of atoms. They are far too small to see with a light microscope.

Atoms are far too small to see with a light microscope. Scientists have to use very powerful electron microscopes to get a closer look at atoms.

💡
Eureka! — John Dalton (1766–1844)

The chemist John Dalton developed a theory about atoms. He suggested that everything is made up of these tiny particles, and that some substances are made up of the same atoms, or that atoms could join together in different ways to create new substances. Dalton used his theory to explain the presence of the wide variety of substances on Earth.

💡
Eureka! — Robert Boyle (1627–1691)

Scientist and inventor Robert Boyle, born in Lismore, Co. Waterford, was one of the pioneers of the modern scientific method. He was the first scientist to suggest the name 'element'.

🧱 Elements
Element
An element is a substance that contains only one type of atom. For example, gold is an element, as it is made up only of gold atoms. Nitrogen is also an element, as it is made up only of nitrogen atoms. Elements cannot be separated into simpler substances.
💡 All matter is made from elements. There are over 100 known elements listed in the periodic table (covered in Chapter 19).
🥇
Gold (Au)
Made up only of gold atoms. A solid element at room temperature. Does not react easily — that's why it's used in jewellery.
💡 Every atom in a gold bar is identical.
💨
Nitrogen (N)
Made up only of nitrogen atoms. A gas at room temperature. Makes up about 78% of the air we breathe.
💡 Elements can be solids, liquids or gases.
📋
The Periodic Table
All known elements (over 100) are listed in the periodic table. Each element has its own unique chemical symbol and properties.
💡 Chapter 19 covers the periodic table in detail.
🔤 Chemical Symbols

Each element has its own name and chemical symbol. Scientists all over the world use these symbols to indicate each element. When writing a chemical symbol, always use a capital letter first. If the symbol has a second letter, it is always a small letter.

🇬🇧
Based on English Name
Most symbols come from the English name of the element. e.g. the English word 'nitrogen' gives the symbol N.
🏛️
Based on Latin Name
Some symbols come from Latin names. e.g. Gold is Au from the Latin word aurum, meaning 'yellow'. Iron is Fe from ferrum.
👤
Named after Scientists
Some symbols come from the scientist who discovered them. e.g. Cm is the symbol for curium, named after Marie and Pierre Curie.

⚛️ Subatomic Particles

Scientists now know that an atom is made up of smaller particles called subatomic particles. There are three subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons.

Structure of an Atom
Properties of Subatomic Particles
Subatomic Particle Mass Charge Location in the Atom
Proton 1 Positively charged (+1) Nucleus
Neutron 1 No charge (0) Nucleus
Electron 1/1836 Negatively charged (−1) Orbiting the nucleus
🎯
Concentrate! Protons and neutrons have the same mass (both = 1). Electrons have so little mass that scientists treat their mass as zero. The smaller number on an element symbol is always the atomic number (protons).
🔢 Atomic Numbers and Mass Numbers
Atomic Number
The atomic number is the number of protons in the atom of that element. The number of protons equals the number of electrons in a neutral atom (because positive and negative charges are equal).
💡 Atomic number = number of protons = number of electrons (in a neutral atom)
Mass Number
The mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom.
💡 Mass number − Atomic number = Number of neutrons
Number of Neutrons
Neutrons = Mass Number − Atomic Number
Example: Reading Atom Notation — Lithium (Li)

🔵 Electronic Configuration

Electron shells are the spaces where electrons orbit. The shells are also called energy levels. Danish scientist Niels Bohr (1885–1962) came up with the idea of electron shells.

1️⃣
First Shell (innermost)
The one closest to the nucleus. Has space for up to 2 electrons.
💡 Always fills up first before the second shell.
2️⃣
Second Shell
Has space for up to 8 electrons.
💡 Fills up after the first shell is full.
3️⃣
Third Shell
Also has space for up to 8 electrons.
💡 Electrons fill shells from the inside out.
Electronic Configuration
Describing the location of electrons in an atom is called the electronic configuration. Scientists use a shorthand version showing the number of electrons in each shell, working outwards from the nucleus.
💡 Example: Nitrogen → N: 2, 5  |  Sodium → Na: 2, 8, 1
Bohr Models: Nitrogen and Sodium
Nitrogen (N) — Atomic No. 7
N: 2, 5
2 in shell 1, 5 in shell 2
Sodium (Na) — Atomic No. 11
Na: 2, 8, 1
2 in shell 1, 8 in shell 2, 1 in shell 3
⚖️ Isotopes
Isotopes
Atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons are called isotopes. The atoms of some elements can exist with different masses. The number of protons in the atoms of the element is the same, but the number of neutrons is different.
💡 Same element, different mass number. Same protons, different neutrons.
Example: Isotopes of Carbon

🎯
Key Rule for Isotopes Isotopes of the same element always have: the SAME number of protons (same atomic number), the SAME number of electrons, but DIFFERENT numbers of neutrons (different mass numbers). The chemical properties of isotopes are identical.
📖 Key Terms
Atom
A tiny particle; the basic building block of all matter.
Element
A substance that contains only one type of atom. Cannot be broken into simpler substances.
Chemical Symbol
A shorthand notation for an element, using 1 or 2 letters (e.g. Na for sodium).
Subatomic Particles
Particles smaller than an atom: protons, neutrons, and electrons.
Protons
Positively charged (+1) subatomic particles found in the nucleus. Mass = 1.
Neutrons
Neutral (0) subatomic particles found in the nucleus. Mass = 1.
Electrons
Negatively charged (−1) particles that orbit the nucleus in shells. Mass ≈ 0.
Atomic Number
The number of protons in an atom of an element. Always the smaller number.
Mass Number
The total number of protons and neutrons in an atom.
Electronic Configuration
The arrangement of electrons in shells around the nucleus (e.g. Na: 2, 8, 1).
Isotope
Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Problem 1 — Reading Atomic Notation
Use the following information to answer the questions below:

Oxygen: Atomic number = 8, Mass number = 16
Chlorine: Atomic number = 17, Mass number = 35
Zinc: Atomic number = 30, Mass number = 65
Tungsten: Atomic number = 74, Mass number = 187

a) Which element has the highest number of protons?
b) How many protons are in an atom of oxygen?
c) How many neutrons are in an atom of zinc?
d) Which element contains the same number of protons and neutrons?
a) Tungsten has atomic number 74 → 74 protons (highest) b) Oxygen: atomic number = 8 → 8 protons c) Zinc: neutrons = mass number − atomic number neutrons = 65 − 30 = 35 neutrons d) Oxygen: protons = 8, neutrons = 16 − 8 = 8 → Same number of protons and neutrons ✓
✅ a) Tungsten  |  b) 8 protons  |  c) 35 neutrons  |  d) Oxygen
💡 Key formula: Neutrons = Mass number − Atomic number. Remember: atomic number = protons = electrons (in a neutral atom).

Problem 3 — Electronic Configuration
Write the electronic configuration for the following atoms:
a) Hydrogen (atomic no. = 1)   b) Magnesium (atomic no. = 12)   c) Neon (atomic no. = 10)   d) Phosphorus (atomic no. = 15)
Shell rules: Shell 1 → max 2 | Shell 2 → max 8 | Shell 3 → max 8 a) Hydrogen (1 electron): Shell 1: 1 → H: 1 b) Magnesium (12 electrons): Shell 1: 2, Shell 2: 8, Shell 3: 2 → Mg: 2, 8, 2 c) Neon (10 electrons): Shell 1: 2, Shell 2: 8 → Ne: 2, 8 d) Phosphorus (15 electrons): Shell 1: 2, Shell 2: 8, Shell 3: 5 → P: 2, 8, 5
✅ H: 1  |  Mg: 2, 8, 2  |  Ne: 2, 8  |  P: 2, 8, 5
💡 Always fill the inner shells first (2, then 8, then 8). The number of electrons = the atomic number for a neutral atom.
Problem 4 — Isotopes of Chlorine
Two isotopes of chlorine exist: Cl-35 and Cl-37. The atomic number of chlorine is 17.
a) How many protons, neutrons and electrons does Cl-35 have?
b) How many protons, neutrons and electrons does Cl-37 have?
c) What do the two isotopes have in common?
d) How do the two isotopes differ?
a) Cl-35 (mass no. = 35, atomic no. = 17): Protons = 17 Neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18 Electrons = 17 b) Cl-37 (mass no. = 37, atomic no. = 17): Protons = 17 Neutrons = 37 − 17 = 20 Electrons = 17 c) In common: Both have 17 protons and 17 electrons. Both are the same element (chlorine). Same chemical properties. d) How they differ: Different number of neutrons (Cl-35 has 18; Cl-37 has 20). Different mass numbers.
✅ a) 17p, 18n, 17e  |  b) 17p, 20n, 17e  |  c) Same protons/electrons  |  d) Different neutrons
💡 Isotopes = same element (same protons), different mass (different neutrons). The number in the name (Cl-35) IS the mass number.
