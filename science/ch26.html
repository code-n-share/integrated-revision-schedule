🔑 Key Terms & What is Density?
⚖️
Density
How tightly packed atoms are in an object — the closer the atoms, the higher the density.
💡 Same size, different mass? Density explains it!
🏋️
Mass
The amount of matter in an object. Measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).
💡 Measured with a mass balance.
📦
Volume
The amount of space an object takes up. Measured in cm³ or m³.
💡 Measured with a ruler (regular shapes) or a graduated cylinder (irregular shapes).
🎈
Flotation
Describes how well an object or substance floats when placed in another substance, such as a liquid or gas.
💡 Lower density than surroundings = float. Higher = sink.

Imagine two identically sized bags — one filled with feathers and the other filled with sand. The bag of sand is much heavier because there are more atoms packed into the same space — the sand has a greater density than the feathers.

Density
Density is how tightly packed atoms are in an object. The closer together the atoms are, the more of them fit into a space, and the higher the density.
💡 SI units for density: g/cm³ or kg/m³
🧮 Calculating Density

To calculate density, you need to know the object's mass (measured in g or kg) and its volume (measured in cm³ or m³).

Density Formula
density = mass volume
🔺
Magic Triangle – Remember the Formula! Cover the quantity you want to find, and the triangle shows the formula:
• density = mass ÷ volume
• mass = density × volume
• volume = mass ÷ density

Cover what you want to find — the triangle shows the calculation!

📐
Always include units! Using SI units throughout a calculation makes them easier to remember. Always write the unit in your answer (e.g. g/cm³ or kg/m³).
📘 Example – Calculating Density of a Cube
Calculate the density of a cube with a mass of 200 g and a volume of 64 cm³.
density = mass ÷ volume density = 200 g ÷ 64 cm³ density = 3.1 g/cm³
✅ Answer: 3.1 g/cm³
Simple: Just divide mass by volume. Always write the unit — here it's g/cm³.
🧪 Measuring Volume of Irregular Solids

For regular shapes (cubes, rectangles), volume is calculated from measurements. For irregular solids, we use the water displacement method:

  1. Place the irregular solid on a mass balance and record its mass.
  2. Fill an overflow can with water and place a graduated cylinder beneath the spout.
  3. Submerge the solid in the water — the water displaced equals the object's volume.
  4. Calculate density by dividing mass by the volume of water displaced.
💡
Archimedes' Principle
The ancient Greek scientist Archimedes (4th century BCE) discovered that when an object is placed in water, the increase in water level equals the volume of the object. Legend says he was so excited by this discovery that he shouted "Eureka!" — meaning "I have found it!" He used this method to prove a king's crown was not pure gold.
Comparing Density of Liquids (Experimental Investigation 26.2)

To measure the density of a liquid:

  1. Measure the mass of a graduated cylinder (empty).
  2. Add 50 ml (50 cm³) of liquid to the cylinder.
  3. Record the new mass and subtract to find the mass of the liquid only.
  4. Calculate density = mass ÷ volume. Repeat for other liquids.
🎈 Flotation
Flotation
Flotation describes how well an object or substance floats when it is placed in another substance, such as a liquid or gas.
💡 Helium floats in air because helium is less dense than air.
🏊
Object FLOATS if…
its density is lower than the substance it is placed in.
🪨
Object SINKS if…
its density is greater than the substance it is placed in.
💧 Flotation in Water

The density of water is 1000 kg/m³ (or 1 g/cm³). This means:

  • Substances with a density less than 1 g/cm³ will float in water.
  • Substances with a density greater than 1 g/cm³ will sink in water.
🧊
Why do icebergs float?
Ice has a density of 0.9 g/cm³ — less dense than liquid water (1 g/cm³), so ice floats. Only about one-tenth of an iceberg is visible above the surface! Extreme care is taken when sailing near icebergs due to the large amount of submerged ice.
🌊
The Dead Sea
The Dead Sea is a salt lake between Israel and Jordan — one of the world's saltiest bodies of water, about 9.6 times as salty as the ocean! The higher salt content increases the water's density, making it very easy for people to float.

🚢
Why do steel ships float? Steel has a density of 7.8 g/cm³, yet ships are built of steel and they float in seawater. This is because a ship is not solid steel — it contains a large amount of air inside. The overall (average) density of the ship including all the air is less than 1 g/cm³, so it floats!

Problem 1 — Basic Density Calculation
A wooden fencepost has a volume of 0.02 m³ and a mass of 18 kg. Calculate its density in kg/m³.
density = mass ÷ volume density = 18 kg ÷ 0.02 m³ density = 900 kg/m³
✅ Answer: 900 kg/m³
💡 Always divide mass by volume. Check that units are consistent — both kg and m³ here, so the answer is kg/m³.
Problem 2 — Finding Volume from Density
Aluminium has a density of 2700 kg/m³. Calculate the volume of 240 g of this metal.
Hint: convert grams to kg first.
mass = 240 g = 0.24 kg volume = mass ÷ density volume = 0.24 ÷ 2700 volume = 0.0000889 m³ (8.89 × 10⁻⁵ m³)
✅ Answer: 8.89 × 10⁻⁵ m³
💡 Rearrange: volume = mass ÷ density. Always convert to consistent units before calculating!
Problem 3 — Rectangular Block
A rectangular cement block is 70 cm long, 0.6 m wide and 1 cm thick.
a) Calculate its volume in m³.
b) Calculate its density if its mass is 58 kg.
a) Convert all to metres: length = 70 cm = 0.7 m width = 0.6 m thickness = 1 cm = 0.01 m volume = 0.7 × 0.6 × 0.01 = 0.0042 m³ b) density = mass ÷ volume density = 58 ÷ 0.0042 density ≈ 13,810 kg/m³
✅ a) Volume = 0.0042 m³  |  b) Density ≈ 13,810 kg/m³
💡 Convert ALL measurements to the same unit before multiplying. Then divide mass by volume for density.
Problem 4 — Gold Cube (Past Paper Style)
A rectangular cube of gold has a length of 10 cm and a mass of 19.25 kg.
a) Calculate its volume (in cm³).
b) Calculate its density (in g/cm³).
a) volume = length³ = 10 × 10 × 10 = 1000 cm³ b) mass = 19.25 kg = 19,250 g density = mass ÷ volume density = 19,250 ÷ 1000 density = 19.25 g/cm³
✅ a) Volume = 1000 cm³  |  b) Density = 19.25 g/cm³
💡 Convert mass to grams (×1000) so it matches cm³. A cube's volume = side³.
Problem 5 — Liquid in a Graduated Cylinder
A graduated cylinder is placed on a mass balance and has a reading of 124 g when empty. After a liquid is placed in it, the volume reads 45 cm³ and the new mass is 200 g. Calculate the density of this liquid.
mass of liquid = 200 − 124 = 76 g volume of liquid = 45 cm³ density = mass ÷ volume density = 76 ÷ 45 density ≈ 1.69 g/cm³
✅ Answer: ≈ 1.69 g/cm³
💡 Don't forget to subtract the empty cylinder mass! Only the liquid's mass goes in the formula.
Problem 6 — Die (Cube) Density
A student measures the length of one side of a die as 16 mm. It has a mass of 4 g.
a) Calculate its volume in cm³.
b) Calculate its density in g/cm³.
a) Convert: 16 mm = 1.6 cm volume = 1.6 × 1.6 × 1.6 = 4.096 cm³ b) density = mass ÷ volume density = 4 ÷ 4.096 density ≈ 0.977 g/cm³
✅ a) 4.096 cm³  |  b) ≈ 0.977 g/cm³
💡 Convert mm → cm by dividing by 10. The die would just barely float in water (density < 1 g/cm³)!
Problem 7 — Density of Metal (Final Exam 2019 style)
A block measuring 8 cm × 4 cm × 2 cm has a mass of 128 g.
a) Calculate the volume of the block.
b) Calculate the density of the block. Include the unit in your answer.
a) volume = 8 × 4 × 2 = 64 cm³ b) density = mass ÷ volume density = 128 ÷ 64 density = 2 g/cm³
✅ a) 64 cm³  |  b) 2 g/cm³
💡 This is a classic past-paper style question. Multiply all three dimensions for volume, then divide mass by volume. Don't forget the unit!
Problem 8 — Metal in Water (Displacement)
A graduated cylinder contains 60 cm³ of water. A regular cuboid of metal with a mass of 78.4 g is placed in it. The new volume reads 74 cm³. Calculate the density of the metal placed in the water.
volume of metal = 74 − 60 = 14 cm³ density = mass ÷ volume density = 78.4 ÷ 14 density = 5.6 g/cm³
✅ Answer: 5.6 g/cm³
💡 The rise in water level = volume of the object (displacement method). Subtract the two water levels to find it.
