Solutions & Solubility

Elements of Science · Chemistry
✅ Explain solute, solvent & solution ✅ Measure solubility at different temperatures ✅ Interpret solubility curves ✅ Compare dilute, concentrated & saturated solutions ✅ Convert concentration units
🔑 Key Terms
🧂
Solute
The substance that dissolves in a liquid.
💡 In salt water, salt is the solute — it's the one that disappears into the liquid.
💧
Solvent
The liquid that the solute dissolves into.
💡 Water is the most common solvent — it dissolves more substances than any other liquid.
🥤
Solution
The mixture formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent.
💡 Salt water, seawater, and fizzy drinks are all solutions.
✅
Soluble
A substance that can dissolve in a liquid.
💡 Sugar is soluble in water — it completely mixes in.
❌
Insoluble
A solid that cannot dissolve in a liquid.
💡 Sand is insoluble in water — it sinks to the bottom instead of dissolving.
📈
Solubility Curve
A graph showing how much solute dissolves in a fixed volume of solvent at different temperatures.
💡 Use the curve to read off solubility at any temperature.
🧪 What is a Solution?

When a substance dissolves in a liquid, its particles break up and move to fill the spaces between the particles of the liquid. The two different types of particles become completely mixed up — this is a solution.

Solute → Solvent → Solution
The substance that dissolves is the solute. The substance the solute dissolves into is the solvent. A solution is formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent.
💡 Example: Sugar (solute) + Water (solvent) → Sugar solution
💡
Soluble vs Insoluble Substances that can dissolve in a liquid are soluble. Solids that cannot dissolve are insoluble. Examples of soluble substances: salt, sugar, instant coffee. Examples of insoluble substances: sand, chalk, candle wax.
📘 Test Tube — Identify the Solute and Solvent
Identify the solute and the solvent in each of the following:
a) Seawater → Solute: salt | Solvent: water b) Sparkling water → Solute: carbon dioxide gas | Solvent: water c) A cup of instant coffee → Solute: instant coffee powder | Solvent: water (hot)
Simple: The solvent is always the liquid. The solute is whatever dissolved into it.
🌡️ Solubility
Solubility
Solubility is a measure of the amount of a solute that dissolves in a fixed volume of solvent. It is measured in g/l (grams per litre) or g/100 cm³.
💡 Think of it as: how much stuff can the liquid "hold" before it's full?
Three Factors Affecting Solubility
🧬
1. Nature of the Substance
Is the substance soluble, partially soluble, or insoluble? Different substances have different maximum solubilities.
🧫
2. Volume of the Solvent
More solvent can dissolve more solute. Doubling the volume of water allows twice as much solute to dissolve.
🌡️
3. Temperature of the Solvent
For most solids, solubility increases as temperature increases. For gases, solubility decreases as temperature increases.
📉 Solubility Curves
Solubility Curve
A graph that shows the amount of a solute that dissolves in a fixed volume of solvent at different temperatures.
💡 The x-axis is temperature (°C); the y-axis is solubility (g/100 g of water).
⬆️
Solids dissolve MORE as temperature increases For example, sodium chloride (table salt) solubility rises from about 35 g/100 g water at 20°C to about 39 g/100 g water at 100°C — a gradual increase.
⬇️
Gases dissolve LESS as temperature increases Oxygen gas in water becomes less soluble as water warms up. This is why fish struggle in warm water — there is less dissolved oxygen for them to breathe.

🎨 Types of Solutions

The term concentration describes how much solute is dissolved in the solvent. It is measured in g/l (grams per litre).

🪣
Dilute Solution
Contains a small amount of solute dissolved in a large amount of solvent.
🧴
Concentrated Solution
Contains a large amount of solute in a small amount of solvent.
⚗️
Saturated Solution
Contains as much solute as can dissolve at a given temperature — no more will dissolve.
🔑
Exam tip — Saturated vs Concentrated A saturated solution is NOT necessarily concentrated. You can have a dilute and saturated solution if the substance has low solubility. Saturated just means no more solute will dissolve at that temperature.
💎 Crystallisation
Crystallisation
Crystallisation is the process by which crystals form when a saturated solution is cooled. As the solution cools, it can hold less solute, so the excess solute comes out of solution as solid crystals.
💡 Salt, snowflakes, and diamonds are all crystals. They form when particles arrange themselves into a regular pattern.
🔬
Eureka! – Dame Kathleen Lonsdale (1903–1971) Born in Newbridge, County Kildare, Ireland. Working as part of a crystallography research team in London, she investigated how diamonds are created and was one of the first scientists to use X-rays to study crystals.
🧮 Concentration Calculations
Converting Concentration to g per 100 cm³
Scaling Concentration
concentration (g/100 cm³) = (mass of solute ÷ volume of solvent) × 100
📘 Worked Example
1 g of citric acid in 10 cm³ of water — convert to g per 100 cm³.
= (1 ÷ 10) × 100 = 10 g per 100 cm³
✅ Answer: 10 g/100 cm³
Simple: If you scaled up 10 cm³ to 100 cm³ (×10), you'd also have 10× as much solute → 10 g.
📘 Figure It Out — Practice Conversions
Convert each of the following to g per 100 cm³:
a) 15 g of salt in 50 cm³ of water = (15 ÷ 50) × 100 = 30 g/100 cm³ b) 10 g of copper sulfate in 25 cm³ of water = (10 ÷ 25) × 100 = 40 g/100 cm³ c) 20 g of sugar in 75 cm³ of water = (20 ÷ 75) × 100 ≈ 26.7 g/100 cm³
Simple method: Divide mass by volume, then multiply by 100. Always check your units!
Calculating Solubility from Experimental Data
📘 Past Paper Style — Finding Solubility (g/cm³)
A student added a compound to 50 cm³ of water at 20°C, stirring until no more dissolved. The greatest mass dissolved was 15 g. Calculate the solubility of the compound in g/cm³.
solubility = mass of solute ÷ volume of solvent solubility = 15 g ÷ 50 cm³ solubility = 0.3 g/cm³
✅ Answer: 0.3 g/cm³
Simple: Divide the mass dissolved by the volume of water used. The answer is the solubility at that temperature.

Problem 1 — Identifying Solute & Solvent
A student dissolves 5 g of sugar in 200 cm³ of water. Identify: (a) the solute, (b) the solvent, (c) the solution.
a) Solute = sugar (the substance that dissolved) b) Solvent = water (the liquid it dissolved into) c) Solution = sugar solution (the resulting mixture)
✅ Solute: sugar | Solvent: water | Solution: sugar water
💡 The solute disappears into the solvent. Together they make the solution.
Problem 2 — Concentration Conversion
Convert the following concentrations to g per 100 cm³:
a) 20 g of salt in 50 cm³ of water
b) 1 g of copper sulfate in 15 cm³ of water
c) 20 g of sugar in 40 cm³ of water
a) (20 ÷ 50) × 100 = 40 g/100 cm³ b) (1 ÷ 15) × 100 ≈ 6.7 g/100 cm³ c) (20 ÷ 40) × 100 = 50 g/100 cm³
✅ a) 40 | b) 6.7 | c) 50 — all in g/100 cm³
💡 Formula: (mass ÷ volume) × 100. That's all there is to it!
Problem 3 — Solubility from Graph (Sample Paper 2018)
A graph shows solubility curves for three solid compounds (1, 2, 3) from 0°C to 100°C. Compound 3 rises steeply; compound 2 rises gradually; compound 1 rises very slowly.
a) Which compound shows the greatest increase in solubility from 0°C to 100°C?
b) Hot water was needed in this investigation. Name a piece of equipment used to heat water.
a) Compound 3 — it has the steepest curve, meaning its solubility increases most with temperature. b) A Bunsen burner, electric hotplate, or kettle could all be used to heat water.
✅ a) Compound 3 | b) Hotplate / Bunsen burner
💡 "Greatest increase" = steepest slope on the graph. Always look for the line that rises fastest.
Problem 4 — Saturated Solution (Final Exam 2022)
A student investigated the solubility of a compound in water. She added the compound to 50 cm³ of water at 20°C until no more dissolved. The greatest mass she dissolved was 15 g.
a) Calculate the solubility of the compound in g/cm³.
b) Describe two things the student could have done to allow a greater mass to dissolve.
a) solubility = mass ÷ volume solubility = 15 ÷ 50 = 0.3 g/cm³ b) Two ways to dissolve more: 1. Increase the temperature of the water (warm water dissolves more solid solute) 2. Use a greater volume of water (more solvent means more solute can dissolve)
✅ a) 0.3 g/cm³ | b) Increase temperature OR increase volume of water
💡 Remember: the solution is saturated at 20°C. To dissolve more, you must change temperature or amount of solvent.
Problem 5 — Types of Solutions
Match each solution description to the correct term (dilute, concentrated, or saturated):
a) Orange squash mixed with a large amount of water
b) A solution where adding more sugar just sinks to the bottom
c) A very dark, strong cup of coffee made with very little water
a) Dilute solution — small amount of solute in lots of solvent b) Saturated solution — no more solute will dissolve c) Concentrated solution — large amount of solute in little solvent
✅ a) Dilute | b) Saturated | c) Concentrated
💡 Key word for saturated: "no more will dissolve." Key word for dilute: "small amount of solute."
Problem 6 — Making a Solution (Sample Paper 2018, Q11)
Citric acid is a white crystalline solid found in lemons. Describe how to make a solution of 5 g of citric acid dissolved in 100 ml of water. Name each piece of equipment you would use.
Equipment: Mass balance (weighing scales), weigh boat, beaker (100 ml), glass stirring rod, graduated cylinder Method: 1. Use the mass balance to measure out 5 g of citric acid into a weigh boat. 2. Using a graduated cylinder, measure out 100 ml of water into a beaker. 3. Add the citric acid to the beaker of water. 4. Stir with the glass stirring rod until all the citric acid has dissolved. 5. The result is a 5 g/100 ml citric acid solution.
✅ Balance → graduated cylinder → beaker → stir until dissolved
💡 Always measure mass first, then volume of solvent. Add solute to solvent, not the other way around.
