📖 Key Terms at a Glance
Matter
Anything that takes up space and has a mass.
Particles
The tiny units that all matter is made from.

The idea that all matter is made from very small particles; explains properties of solids, liquids and gases.
Solid
State of matter with a fixed shape and fixed volume. Particles are tightly packed.
Liquid
State of matter with a fixed volume but no fixed shape. Particles can slide past each other.
Gas
State of matter with no fixed shape or volume. Particles spread far apart.
Diffusion
The spreading out of particles to fill the space they are in, until concentration is even.
Melting

Freezing

Evaporation
Surface particles gain enough energy to escape a liquid as a gas (at any temperature).
Boiling

Condensation
A gas cools and changes to a liquid.
⚗️ Matter
Matter
Matter is anything that takes up space and has a mass. There are three states of matter: solid, liquid and gas.
💡 Examples — Solid: ice, wood, rock | Liquid: water, milk | Gas: steam (water vapour), oxygen
🧪 Experimental Investigation 14.1
Does air take up space and have mass?
Equipment
Basin Cup Mass balance Water Tissue paper Balloon
⚠️
Safety
Clear up any spills immediately.
To show that air takes up space
  1. Fill the basin with water so the cup will be submerged.
  2. Place the dry tissue paper down at the bottom of the cup.
  3. Turn the cup upside down and place the cup into the basin of water.
  4. Keep the cup under the water for 20 seconds and remove slowly.
  5. Record your observations. (The tissue stays dry — air occupies the space in the cup.)
To show that air has mass
  1. Use a mass balance to record the mass of the empty balloon.
  2. Blow up the balloon and tie.
  3. Use the mass balance to record the mass of the inflated balloon.
  4. Record your observations. (The inflated balloon is heavier — air has mass.)
🔬 Particle Theory
Particle Theory
Scientists believe that all matter is made from very small particles. Particle theory is used to explain the properties of solids, liquids and gases.
💡 Particles in all three states are never completely still — they all have some energy that causes them to move.
💡
Eureka – Ancient Greeks
The ancient Greeks were the first to suggest that everything is made up of particles that are too small to be seen.
🧊 Properties of Solids, Liquids & Gases

Solids, liquids and gases differ in how quickly the particles move and how close together the particles are.

Particle Arrangement in the Three States

Solid
Liquid
Gas
🧊 The Solid State

In the solid state, the particles that make up the substance touch each other because they are packed tightly together. The particles do not move around, but are not completely still — they vibrate on the spot. This explains why solids have a fixed shape and cannot flow.

💧 The Liquid State

In the liquid state, the particles are very close together and most particles touch each other. The particles have more energy than in a solid, so they can move around and slide past each other. This explains why a liquid can flow and can change shape.

💨 The Gas State

In the gas state, the particles are spread out far apart. There is a lot of space between particles in a gas. As particles move to fill the container they are in, gases do not have a fixed volume.

Compression of Gases

Compression happens when the particles are pressed together to make them fit into a smaller space. For example, the gas particles in a spray can of deodorant or hairspray are pressed together. When the contents of the can are sprayed, the particles spread out again to fill the air.

🎯
CONCENTRATE! Gases can be compressed because there is lots of space between the particles. Solids and liquids cannot be easily compressed because the particles are already close together.

💨 Diffusion
Diffusion
Diffusion is the spreading out of particles to fill the space they are in, until there is an even concentration of particles in the space.
💡 Example: if a scented diffuser is in one corner of a room, your nose will pick up the smell in another part — the particles spread throughout the room even though you cannot see them.

Diffusion occurs mainly in liquids and gases. Diffusion happens by itself — you do not need to mix or stir the substances. Temperature and the size of the particles affect the speed of diffusion.

🎯
CONCENTRATE! Diffusion happens from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration. It continues until the concentration is even throughout. Higher temperature = faster diffusion.
🔥 Changes of State

When a solid is heated, its particles gain energy and move apart. The particles break free from their tightly packed positions and the solid changes to a liquid — this is melting. When a liquid is heated further, the liquid turns into a gas — this is boiling. These are physical changes: no new substance is formed, there is no change in mass and the reaction can be easily reversed.

Melting

Heat causes water particles to vibrate faster. The solid ice cube changes state to become liquid water. The temperature at which a substance melts is called its melting point. The melting point of ice is 0 °C.

Freezing

Cooling a liquid takes energy from its particles so they can only vibrate, not move around. The liquid changes state into a solid. When a liquid changes into a solid it is called freezing. The freezing point of water is 0 °C.

Evaporation and Boiling

Both evaporation and boiling cause a change of state from a liquid to a gas, but they take place differently.

  • Evaporation: Particles at the surface gain enough energy to escape and change state from a liquid to a gas. The liquid does not bubble, and evaporation happens at all temperatures. (e.g. puddles drying in sunlight)
  • Boiling: All particles in the liquid have enough energy to break free. Bubbles form throughout the liquid. The temperature at which a liquid changes to a gas is called the boiling point. The boiling point of water is 100 °C.
Condensation

When a gas is cooled, energy is taken from its particles. The particles slow down and move more closely together. The gas changes to a liquid — this is called condensation. Steam will condense to form water. (e.g. water droplets on a cold car window on a wet day)

Sublimation

When some substances are heated, they do not melt to form a liquid. Instead, they change state from a solid to a gas directly. This is called sublimation. For example, solid carbon dioxide (known as 'dry ice') sublimes above −78.5 °C. The vapour it creates is used for special effects at concerts.

Melting & Boiling Points — Common Substances

Room temperature ≈ 20 °C. If melting point > 20 °C → solid; if boiling point < 20 °C → gas; otherwise → liquid.

🧪 Experimental Investigation 14.3
What is the boiling point of water?
Equipment
Graduated cylinder Water Beaker Bunsen burner Tripod stand Wire gauze Retort stand & clamp Thermometer Glass stirring rod
⚠️
Safety
• Wear safety goggles.
• Tie back hair.
• Ensure the workspace is clear of flammable materials.
• Make sure equipment has cooled down before touching it.
Method
  1. Measure 150 cm³ of water in the graduated cylinder and transfer it to the beaker.
  2. Heat the beaker of water over a Bunsen burner on the tripod stand and wire gauze. Use the retort stand and clamp to hold the thermometer in the water.
  3. Observe what happens to the water. Stir gently with the glass stirring rod at intervals.
  4. When you observe a steady stream of bubbles, this temperature is the boiling point.
  5. Record the temperature at which the water boils.
  6. Repeat a number of times for accuracy.

Problem 1 — States of Matter
In which state is a substance if it: (a) has a fixed volume and a fixed shape, (b) can flow and can be compressed, (c) has a fixed volume and can flow, (d) takes the shape of its container but cannot be compressed?
a) Fixed volume + fixed shape → SOLID (particles packed tightly together; can only vibrate) b) Can flow + can be compressed → GAS (particles spread far apart; lots of empty space) c) Fixed volume + can flow → LIQUID (particles close but can slide past each other) d) Takes container shape + cannot compress → LIQUID (close-packed particles; no room to compress)
✅ a) Solid  |  b) Gas  |  c) Liquid  |  d) Liquid
💡 Key rule: if it CAN be compressed → gas. If it has a fixed shape → solid. If it can flow but NOT be compressed → liquid.
Problem 2 — Particle Theory: Solids, Liquids, Gases
Use the particle theory to explain why it is possible to pour a liquid but not a solid.
In a LIQUID: → Particles are close together but have more energy than in a solid → Particles can move around and slide past each other → This allows the liquid to flow and take the shape of its container → Therefore, liquids can be poured. In a SOLID: → Particles are tightly packed in fixed positions → Particles can only vibrate on the spot; they cannot move past each other → The solid maintains its fixed shape → Therefore, solids cannot be poured.
✅ Liquid particles can slide past each other (so they flow); solid particles are fixed in place (so they cannot).
💡 Always refer to particle movement and arrangement when answering particle theory questions.
Problem 3 — Diffusion
What does the term 'diffusion' mean? Give one example of diffusion in everyday life.
Diffusion = the spreading out of particles to fill the space they are in, until there is an even concentration of particles throughout the space. Everyday example: → A perfume spray released in one corner of a room spreads throughout the room until everyone can smell it. → The smell of roast chicken cooking fills the whole kitchen. → A drop of food dye dropped into a glass of water gradually spreads until the colour is even throughout.
✅ Diffusion = particles spreading out to fill space evenly. Example: perfume spreading across a room.
💡 Diffusion occurs on its own — no stirring needed. Higher temperature = faster diffusion.
Problem 4 — Changes of State (naming)
Name the state of matter that results when: (a) a solid melts, (b) a liquid evaporates, (c) a gas condenses, (d) a solid sublimates, (e) a liquid freezes.
a) Solid melts → LIQUID (e.g. ice → water at 0 °C) b) Liquid evaporates → GAS (e.g. water → water vapour) c) Gas condenses → LIQUID (e.g. steam → water droplets) d) Solid sublimates → GAS (e.g. dry ice → CO₂ gas) e) Liquid freezes → SOLID (e.g. water → ice at 0 °C)
✅ a) Liquid  |  b) Gas  |  c) Liquid  |  d) Gas  |  e) Solid
💡 Learn the change-of-state diagram: Solid ⇌ Liquid ⇌ Gas, plus the shortcut Solid → Gas (sublimation).

Problem 7 — Final Examination 2022, Q13 (Melting Point of Sugar)
A student placed 1 g of sucrose (table sugar) on top of two metal cubes (zinc and aluminium) on a hot plate. The sugar melted, but the metals did not.
(a) What is meant by the melting point of a substance?
(b) Explain why the sugar melted but the metals did not.
a) The melting point of a substance is the temperature at which it changes state from a solid to a liquid. b) Sucrose has a much LOWER melting point than zinc and aluminium. The hotplate reached a temperature high enough to melt the sugar but NOT high enough to melt either metal. → Sugar melting point ≈ 186 °C → Zinc melting point = 420 °C → Aluminium melting point = 660 °C
✅ Melting point = temperature at which solid becomes liquid. Sugar has a lower melting point than the metals.
💡 Different substances have different melting points — this is a physical property that can be used to identify substances.
